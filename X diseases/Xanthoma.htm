Xanthoma

Understanding Xanthoma

Xanthomas are yellowish deposits of fatty substances, primarily cholesterol, that accumulate under the skin. They can appear anywhere on the body but are commonly found on the eyelids (xanthelasma), elbows, knees, hands, feet, and tendons. Xanthomas are not tumors and are usually benign (non-cancerous).

The presence of xanthomas can sometimes indicate an underlying medical condition, particularly disorders of lipid metabolism (how the body processes fats). Therefore, it's important to have them evaluated by a healthcare professional.

Types of Xanthomas

Xanthomas are classified based on their appearance and location:

  • **Xanthelasma:** Small, yellowish plaques that occur on or around the eyelids.
  • **Tendinous Xanthomas:** Firm, painless nodules found on tendons, especially the Achilles tendon and the tendons of the hands and elbows. They are often associated with familial hypercholesterolemia.
  • **Tuberous Xanthomas:** Large, yellowish nodules that typically appear on the elbows and knees. They are often associated with high levels of triglycerides or cholesterol.
  • **Eruptive Xanthomas:** Small, yellow-red bumps that appear suddenly, often in crops, on the back, buttocks, and extensor surfaces of the arms and legs. They are usually associated with very high triglyceride levels.
  • **Planar Xanthomas:** Flat, yellowish patches that can occur anywhere on the body. Palmar planar xanthomas (on the palms of the hands) are often associated with a rare type of high cholesterol called familial dysbetalipoproteinemia.
  • **Striate Palmar Xanthomas:** Yellowish or orange lines in the creases of the palms. These are also characteristic of familial dysbetalipoproteinemia.

Causes and Risk Factors

Xanthomas are caused by the accumulation of lipids, particularly cholesterol, in skin cells. This accumulation is often related to elevated levels of lipids in the blood (hyperlipidemia). Common underlying causes and risk factors include:

  • **Hypercholesterolemia:** High levels of cholesterol in the blood, often due to genetics (familial hypercholesterolemia) or lifestyle factors.
  • **Hypertriglyceridemia:** High levels of triglycerides in the blood, which can be caused by genetics, diabetes, obesity, excessive alcohol intake, or certain medications.
  • **Familial dysbetalipoproteinemia:** A genetic disorder that leads to high levels of both cholesterol and triglycerides.
  • **Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC):** A chronic liver disease.
  • **Nephrotic syndrome:** A kidney disorder that causes the body to excrete too much protein in the urine.
  • **Hypothyroidism:** An underactive thyroid gland.
  • **Diabetes mellitus:** Especially poorly controlled diabetes.
  • **Certain medications:** Such as retinoids, thiazide diuretics, and beta-blockers can sometimes raise lipid levels.
  • **Obesity.**
  • **Diet high in saturated and trans fats.**

In some cases, xanthomas can occur even when blood lipid levels are normal.

Symptoms of Xanthoma

The primary symptom of xanthoma is the appearance of yellowish bumps or patches on the skin or tendons. These lesions are usually:

  • Yellowish in color.
  • Flat or raised.
  • Vary in size from very small to several centimeters.
  • Usually painless, although they can sometimes be itchy or tender, especially eruptive xanthomas.
  • Firm to the touch (especially tendinous and tuberous xanthomas).

The location and appearance of the xanthomas can provide clues about the underlying lipid disorder.

Diagnosis of Xanthoma

Xanthomas are often diagnosed based on their characteristic appearance during a physical examination. However, to determine the underlying cause, a doctor will typically order blood tests to measure lipid levels, including:

  • Total cholesterol
  • LDL cholesterol (bad cholesterol)
  • HDL cholesterol (good cholesterol)
  • Triglycerides

Depending on the findings, further tests may be needed to evaluate for underlying conditions such as liver disease, kidney disease, or thyroid problems. In some cases, a skin biopsy may be performed to confirm the diagnosis of xanthoma under a microscope. Genetic testing may be considered if a familial lipid disorder is suspected.

Treatment and Management of Xanthoma

The primary goal of treating xanthomas is to address the underlying lipid disorder. Treatment strategies may include:

  • **Lifestyle modifications:**
    • Following a heart-healthy diet low in saturated and trans fats and cholesterol.
    • Losing weight if overweight or obese.
    • Regular physical activity.
    • Quitting smoking.
    • Limiting alcohol intake.
  • **Medications:** If lifestyle changes are not enough to lower lipid levels, medications may be prescribed, such as:
    • Statins (to lower LDL cholesterol)
    • Fibrates (to lower triglycerides and raise HDL cholesterol)
    • Niacin (to lower LDL cholesterol and triglycerides and raise HDL cholesterol)
    • Bile acid sequestrants (to lower LDL cholesterol)
    • PCSK9 inhibitors (to lower LDL cholesterol significantly)
    • Omega-3 fatty acids (to lower triglycerides)
  • **Treatment of underlying medical conditions:** Managing diabetes, hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, or liver disease can help improve lipid levels and potentially reduce xanthomas.
  • **Direct removal of xanthomas:** While addressing the underlying cause is crucial, xanthomas can sometimes be removed for cosmetic reasons using procedures such as:
    • Surgical excision
    • Laser therapy
    • Cryotherapy (freezing)
    • Chemical peels
    However, it's important to note that if the underlying lipid disorder is not managed, xanthomas may recur.

Important Considerations

  • Xanthomas themselves are usually harmless but can be a sign of an increased risk for cardiovascular disease due to underlying high cholesterol levels.
  • Early diagnosis and management of the underlying lipid disorder are important for preventing complications.
  • Treatment of xanthomas is often focused on addressing the root cause rather than just removing the skin lesions.
  • Regular monitoring of lipid levels is essential.

Managing Xanthoma

Effective management focuses on addressing the underlying lipid issues and may include:

  • Lifestyle modifications (diet, exercise, weight management).
  • Medications to lower lipid levels.
  • Treatment of any underlying medical conditions.
  • Consideration of removal procedures for cosmetic reasons.
