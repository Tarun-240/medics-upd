About Wolff-Parkinson-White Syndrome

Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome is a heart condition present at birth (congenital). It causes a rapid heartbeat (tachycardia). In WPW syndrome, an extra electrical pathway in the heart leads to the fast heartbeat. It is a relatively rare condition.

Symptoms

Symptoms of Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome can vary. Some people with WPW syndrome never experience symptoms. Symptoms may come and go.

When present, symptoms may include:

  • Rapid heartbeat (tachycardia)
  • Fluttering or pounding heartbeat (palpitations)
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Shortness of breath
  • Anxiety
  • Fainting
  • Fatigue

Infants with WPW syndrome may have symptoms such as:

  • Poor feeding
  • Trouble breathing
  • Bluish or grayish skin
  • Lethargy

Causes

Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome is caused by an extra electrical pathway between the heart's upper chambers (atria) and lower chambers (ventricles). This extra pathway is present at birth. The extra pathway can allow electrical signals to travel around the heart very quickly, causing a rapid heartbeat.

Diagnosing

Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome is diagnosed with an electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG). This painless test records the heart's electrical activity. An ECG shows how the heart is beating.

If rapid heartbeats don't happen often, a doctor may recommend:

  • Holter monitor
  • Event monitor
  • Electrophysiology (EP) study

Prevention

Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome is a congenital condition, meaning it is present at birth. It cannot be prevented. However, the complications associated with it can be prevented with treatment.

Treating

Treatment for Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome depends on the severity of symptoms and the frequency of rapid heartbeats.

Treatment options include:

  • Vagal maneuvers.
  • Medications.
  • Cardioversion.
  • Radiofrequency catheter ablation.
  • Surgery.
