Heart Attack (Myocardial Infarction)

Understanding Heart Attack

A heart attack, also known as a myocardial infarction (MI), occurs when the blood flow to a part of the heart is blocked, usually by a blood clot. Without blood, the heart muscle doesn't receive oxygen and nutrients, and damage or death of the heart tissue can occur. Heart attacks are a serious medical emergency requiring immediate attention.

Symptoms of a Heart Attack

Symptoms of a heart attack can vary from person to person in intensity and presentation. It's crucial to recognize these signs and act quickly:

  • **Chest pain, pressure, squeezing, or tightness:** This is the most common symptom. The pain may be severe or mild and can last for more than a few minutes or come and go. It may feel like a heavy weight on the chest.
  • **Pain radiating to other areas:** Pain can spread to the arm (most commonly the left arm, but it can be either arm), shoulder, neck, jaw, or back.
  • **Shortness of breath (dyspnea):** May occur with or without chest pain.
  • **Nausea, vomiting, or indigestion.**
  • **Sweating (diaphoresis) or cold, clammy skin.**
  • **Lightheadedness or dizziness.**
  • **Unexplained fatigue.**
  • **Anxiety or a feeling of impending doom.**

It's important to note that women, older adults, and people with diabetes may experience less typical symptoms, such as shortness of breath, nausea, back or jaw pain, or unusual fatigue. **Do not ignore these symptoms.**

Causes of a Heart Attack

The primary cause of a heart attack is coronary artery disease (CAD). In CAD, fatty deposits (plaque) build up in the arteries that supply blood to the heart. This plaque can narrow the arteries, reducing blood flow. A heart attack typically occurs when:

  • A plaque ruptures, forming a blood clot that completely blocks the artery.
  • Less commonly, a sudden spasm of a coronary artery can also block blood flow.

Risk Factors for a Heart Attack

Several factors can increase your risk of having a heart attack:

  • High blood pressure (hypertension)
  • High cholesterol
  • Smoking
  • Diabetes
  • Obesity or being overweight
  • Family history of heart disease
  • Older age
  • Lack of physical activity
  • Stress
  • Unhealthy diet

Diagnosis of a Heart Attack

Prompt diagnosis is critical during a suspected heart attack. Doctors will use several tools:

  • **Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG):** Measures the electrical activity of the heart and can show if a heart attack is occurring or has occurred.
  • **Blood tests:** Measure cardiac enzymes (proteins) that are released into the blood when heart muscle is damaged.
  • **Echocardiogram:** An ultrasound of the heart that can show how well the heart is pumping and identify areas of damage.
  • **Coronary angiography (cardiac catheterization):** A thin, flexible tube is inserted into a blood vessel and guided to the heart. Dye is injected, and X-rays are taken to visualize the coronary arteries and identify any blockages.

Treatment of a Heart Attack

Treatment for a heart attack focuses on quickly restoring blood flow to the blocked artery and preventing further damage to the heart. Immediate treatment may include:

  • **Aspirin:** To help prevent further blood clotting.
  • **Nitroglycerin:** To widen blood vessels and improve blood flow.
  • **Oxygen therapy:** If blood oxygen levels are low.
  • **Pain medication:** To relieve chest pain.
  • **Medications to break up blood clots (thrombolytics or fibrinolytics):** Given intravenously to dissolve the clot blocking the artery.
  • **Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI):** A procedure where a catheter with a balloon tip is inserted into the blocked artery. The balloon is inflated to widen the artery, and a stent (a small mesh tube) is often placed to keep it open.
  • **Coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG):** Open-heart surgery where a healthy blood vessel from another part of the body is used to create a new route for blood to flow around the blocked artery.

After the immediate treatment, ongoing care focuses on rehabilitation, medication to prevent future events, and lifestyle changes to manage risk factors.

Emergency Information

If you suspect you or someone else is having a heart attack:

**CALL EMERGENCY MEDICAL SERVICES IMMEDIATELY.**

Do not drive yourself to the hospital if possible.

Chew and swallow an aspirin (unless allergic) while waiting for help.
