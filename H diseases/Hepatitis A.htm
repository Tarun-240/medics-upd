Hepatitis A

Understanding Hepatitis A

Hepatitis A is a highly contagious liver infection caused by the hepatitis A virus (HAV). It is usually a mild, short-term illness, and most people recover fully without permanent liver damage. However, in rare cases, it can cause severe liver disease. Hepatitis A is commonly spread through the fecal-oral route, meaning someone unknowingly ingests the virus from contact with faeces-contaminated food or water or through close personal contact with an infected person.

Symptoms of Hepatitis A

Symptoms of hepatitis A can vary from mild to severe and may not appear for 2 to 6 weeks after infection. Not everyone who gets hepatitis A will have symptoms. When symptoms do occur, they can include:

  • Fatigue
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Abdominal pain, especially in the upper right side under the ribs (where the liver is located)
  • Loss of appetite
  • Fever
  • Dark urine
  • Pale or clay-colored stools
  • Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and whites of the eyes)
  • Itching

Symptoms usually last less than two months, although some people can be ill for as long as six months.

Causes and Transmission of Hepatitis A

Hepatitis A virus (HAV) is primarily transmitted through the fecal-oral route. This can happen through:

  • Consuming food or water contaminated with the faeces of an infected person. This can occur when food handlers don't wash their hands properly after using the toilet.
  • Close personal contact with an infected person, such as through household contact or sexual contact.
  • Traveling to areas where hepatitis A is common and consuming contaminated food or water.
  • Outbreaks associated with contaminated food products, such as raw or undercooked shellfish.

Hepatitis A is not spread through coughing or sneezing.

Diagnosis of Hepatitis A

Hepatitis A is diagnosed through a blood test that detects the presence of IgM antibodies to HAV. These antibodies indicate a recent hepatitis A infection. Other liver function tests may also be done to assess the health of the liver.

Treatment of Hepatitis A

There is no specific treatment for hepatitis A. The body will clear the virus on its own. Treatment focuses on relieving symptoms and providing supportive care:

  • Rest to help the body recover.
  • Drink plenty of fluids to prevent dehydration, especially if experiencing vomiting or diarrhea.
  • Avoid alcohol and unnecessary medications that can put extra stress on the liver.
  • Eat a healthy diet as tolerated. Small, frequent meals may be easier to manage if nausea is present.
  • Manage symptoms like itching with antihistamines, as recommended by a doctor.

Hospitalization is usually not required unless symptoms are severe or complications develop.

Prevention of Hepatitis A

Hepatitis A is preventable through vaccination and good hygiene practices:

  • **Vaccination:** The hepatitis A vaccine is safe and highly effective. It is recommended for all children starting at age 1 year, as well as for adults at increased risk, such as travelers to areas where hepatitis A is common, men who have sex with men, people who use injection drugs, and people with chronic liver disease. The vaccine is given in two doses, usually six months apart.
  • **Good hygiene:**
    • Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water for at least 20 seconds, especially after using the toilet, before preparing or eating food, and after being in public places.
    • Avoid drinking untreated water.
    • Be cautious about food and water when traveling to areas with poor sanitation.
  • **Post-exposure prophylaxis:** If you have been exposed to someone with hepatitis A and are not vaccinated, your doctor may recommend a dose of the hepatitis A vaccine or an injection of immune globulin (antibodies) to provide short-term protection.

Prevention Tips

Key ways to protect yourself from Hepatitis A.

  • Get the Hepatitis A vaccine
  • Wash hands thoroughly and often
  • Be cautious with food and water when traveling
  • Practice good hygiene
