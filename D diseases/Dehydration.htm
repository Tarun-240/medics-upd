Dehydration

About Dehydration

Dehydration occurs when your body loses more fluids than you take in and doesn't have enough water and other fluids to carry out its normal functions. Even mild dehydration can drain your energy and make you feel tired.

Water makes up a significant portion of your body weight and is essential for many bodily processes, including regulating temperature, transporting nutrients, and removing waste products.

Causes of Dehydration

Dehydration can result from various factors, including:

  • Not drinking enough fluids: This is the most common cause.
  • Excessive sweating: Especially during intense exercise or in hot weather.
  • Vomiting and diarrhea: These can lead to significant fluid loss.
  • Increased urination: Caused by certain medications (diuretics), uncontrolled diabetes, or alcohol consumption.
  • Fever: Higher body temperature can increase fluid loss.

Symptoms of Dehydration

The symptoms of dehydration can range from mild to severe, depending on the amount of fluid lost.

Mild to Moderate Dehydration:

  • Thirst
  • Dry mouth
  • Dark yellow urine
  • Decreased urination
  • Headache
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Fatigue

Severe Dehydration (requires immediate medical attention):

  • Extreme thirst
  • Very dark yellow or amber urine
  • No urination for many hours
  • Dry, shriveled skin
  • Sunken eyes
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Rapid breathing
  • Low blood pressure
  • Lack of tears
  • Confusion, irritability
  • Seizures

Risk Factors for Dehydration

Certain individuals are at a higher risk of dehydration:

  • Infants and young children: Their smaller body weight means they lose a higher percentage of body fluid with illness.
  • Older adults: They may have a decreased sense of thirst and may have medical conditions or take medications that increase fluid loss.
  • People with chronic illnesses: Such as diabetes or kidney disease.
  • Athletes: Especially those participating in endurance sports or exercising in hot and humid conditions.
  • People living in hot climates: Increased sweating can lead to dehydration if fluids are not adequately replaced.

Diagnosing Dehydration

A doctor can usually diagnose dehydration based on a physical exam and a review of your symptoms and medical history. They may also perform tests such as:

  • Urine tests: To check the color and concentration of your urine.
  • Blood tests: To check electrolyte levels and kidney function.

Treating Dehydration

The treatment for dehydration depends on its severity.

Mild to Moderate Dehydration:

  • Drinking clear fluids: Water, sports drinks (with electrolytes), broth, and diluted juice are good options.
  • Avoiding sugary drinks: These can sometimes worsen dehydration.

Severe Dehydration:

  • Intravenous (IV) fluids: Administered in a hospital setting to quickly replenish fluids and electrolytes.

Preventing Dehydration

Preventing dehydration is key and involves:

  • Drinking plenty of fluids throughout the day, especially when it's hot or when you're exercising.
  • Increasing fluid intake when you're sick, especially if you have vomiting or diarrhea.
  • Eating foods with high water content, such as fruits and vegetables.
  • Being mindful of fluid intake for infants, young children, and older adults.

Signs of Dehydration

Recognize the symptoms early.

  • Thirst
  • Dry mouth
  • Dark urine
  • Headache
  • Dizziness
  • Fatigue
  • (Severe) Lack of urination
  • (Severe) Sunken eyes
