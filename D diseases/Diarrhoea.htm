Diarrhoea

About Diarrhoea

Diarrhoea is a common condition characterized by loose, watery stools and an increased frequency of bowel movements. It usually lasts a few days and often resolves on its own. However, severe or persistent diarrhoea can lead to dehydration and other complications, especially in young children, older adults, and those with weakened immune systems.

While usually not serious, understanding the causes and knowing how to manage diarrhoea is important for preventing complications and promoting recovery.

Causes of Diarrhoea

Diarrhoea can be caused by a variety of factors, including:

  • Viral infections: Such as norovirus and rotavirus (a common cause in children).
  • Bacterial infections: Such as Salmonella, E. coli, and Campylobacter, often from contaminated food or water.
  • Parasitic infections: Such as Giardia and Cryptosporidium.
  • Food poisoning: Caused by toxins produced by bacteria in food.
  • Medications: Certain antibiotics, antacids, and other drugs can cause diarrhoea as a side effect.
  • Food intolerances and allergies: Such as lactose intolerance.
  • Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS): Can sometimes cause diarrhoea-predominant symptoms.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): Such as Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis.
  • Traveler's diarrhoea: Often caused by consuming contaminated food or water in new environments.

Symptoms of Diarrhoea

The primary symptom of diarrhoea is frequent, loose, watery stools. Other associated symptoms may include:

  • Abdominal cramps or pain.
  • Bloating.
  • Nausea and vomiting.
  • Urgent need to have a bowel movement.
  • Fever.
  • Blood in the stool (may indicate a more serious infection or IBD).
  • Dehydration (signs include decreased urination, dark urine, dry mouth, dizziness).

The duration and severity of symptoms can vary depending on the cause of the diarrhoea.

When to Seek Medical Advice for Diarrhoea

While most cases of diarrhoea are mild and self-limiting, it's important to seek medical attention if you experience any of the following:

  • Diarrhoea lasting more than a few days (for adults) or more than 24 hours (for young children).
  • Severe dehydration.
  • Severe abdominal pain.
  • High fever (above 102°F or 39°C).
  • Blood or pus in the stool.
  • Black, tarry stools.
  • Diarrhoea in infants or young children.
  • Diarrhoea in older adults or those with weakened immune systems.

Diagnosing the Cause of Diarrhoea

In most cases of mild diarrhoea, specific diagnostic tests are not needed. However, if symptoms are severe or persistent, a doctor may recommend:

  • Stool tests: To identify bacteria, viruses, or parasites.
  • Blood tests: To check for signs of infection, inflammation, or dehydration.
  • Colonoscopy or sigmoidoscopy: In cases of chronic diarrhoea to examine the colon.
  • Food diary and elimination diet: To identify potential food intolerances or allergies.

Treating Diarrhoea

The main goals of diarrhoea treatment are to prevent dehydration and manage symptoms:

  • Fluid replacement: Drink plenty of clear fluids like water, broth, and oral rehydration solutions (ORS), especially for children and older adults.
  • Dietary changes: Follow a bland diet (BRAT diet: bananas, rice, applesauce, toast) initially and gradually reintroduce normal foods. Avoid fatty, spicy, or sugary foods, and dairy products temporarily.
  • Over-the-counter medications: Anti-diarrhoeal medications like loperamide (Imodium) or bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol) may help reduce symptoms in adults, but consult a doctor before giving them to children.
  • Antibiotics or antiparasitic medications: Prescribed by a doctor if the diarrhoea is caused by a bacterial or parasitic infection.
  • Probiotics: May help restore the balance of gut bacteria in some cases.

Preventing Diarrhoea

Practicing good hygiene and food safety can help prevent many cases of diarrhoea:

  • Wash your hands frequently with soap and water, especially after using the toilet and before preparing or eating food.
  • Cook food thoroughly, especially meat and eggs.
  • Avoid cross-contamination in the kitchen by keeping raw and cooked foods separate.
  • Drink safe water; if unsure, boil it or use water purification methods.
  • Practice safe food handling and storage.
  • Get vaccinated against rotavirus (for infants).
  • When traveling, be mindful of what you eat and drink (e.g., avoid tap water, raw vegetables, and undercooked foods in high-risk areas).

Signs of Diarrhoea

Common indicators of this condition.

  • Loose, watery stools
  • Frequent bowel movements
  • Abdominal cramps
  • Bloating
  • Nausea
  • Urgent need to go
  • Dehydration signs (severe)
