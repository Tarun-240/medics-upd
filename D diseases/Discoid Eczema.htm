Discoid Eczema

About Discoid Eczema

Discoid eczema, also known as nummular eczema or discoid dermatitis, is a chronic type of eczema that causes distinctive circular or oval patches of itchy, swollen, and cracked skin. The term "discoid" refers to the disc-like or coin-shaped appearance of these patches.

Without treatment, discoid eczema can persist for weeks, months, or even years and may recur in previously affected areas. It can affect any part of the body, but it typically spares the face and scalp.

Symptoms of Discoid Eczema

The first sign of discoid eczema is usually the appearance of small spots or bumps on the skin. These rapidly merge to form larger, circular or oval patches, ranging in size from a few millimeters to several centimeters. Other common symptoms include:

  • **Appearance:** Patches are typically pink or red on lighter skin and can be dark brown or paler than the surrounding skin on darker skin tones.
  • **Initial Stage:** Patches are often swollen, may have small fluid-filled blisters (oozing fluid), and can be intensely itchy, especially at night.
  • **Later Stage:** Over time, the patches may become dry, crusty, cracked, and flaky.
  • **Central Clearing:** Sometimes, the center of a patch may clear, leaving a ring of discolored skin, which can be mistaken for ringworm.
  • **Number of Patches:** Individuals may have just one patch, but most develop several. The skin between patches is often dry.
  • **Infection:** Patches can sometimes become infected, indicated by increased oozing, a yellow crust, warmth, swelling, tenderness, pain, and potentially feeling unwell, hot, or shivery.

Causes and Triggers of Discoid Eczema

The exact cause of discoid eczema is unknown, but several factors are thought to play a role:

  • **Dry Skin:** It is often associated with particularly dry skin, which may not provide an effective barrier against irritants.
  • **Irritants:** Previously harmless substances like soaps and detergents can irritate the skin.
  • **Contact Dermatitis:** Skin contact with certain irritating substances or allergens may trigger or worsen discoid eczema.
  • **Skin Injury:** Minor skin injuries such as insect bites, burns, or scrapes can sometimes trigger an outbreak.
  • **Medications:** Certain medications, including interferon and ribavirin, TNF-alpha blockers, and statins, have been linked to discoid eczema.
  • **Bacterial Infection:** In some cases, a bacterial infection (Staphylococcus aureus) may be associated with discoid eczema.
  • **Poor Circulation:** In older adults, poor blood flow may be a contributing factor.

Unlike atopic eczema, discoid eczema does not typically run in families.

Diagnosing Discoid Eczema

Discoid eczema is usually diagnosed based on its characteristic round or oval appearance during a physical examination by a healthcare professional. Sometimes, it can be mistaken for other skin conditions like ringworm or other types of eczema. In some cases, a skin biopsy may be performed to confirm the diagnosis and rule out other conditions. Patch testing might be considered in chronic cases to identify potential contact allergens.

Treating Discoid Eczema

The goals of treatment are to relieve itching, reduce inflammation, clear the rash, prevent infection, and manage dry skin. Common treatments include:

  • **Emollients:** Regular and liberal use of moisturizers (emollients) is crucial to hydrate the skin and protect its barrier function. Different types (ointments for very dry skin, creams or lotions for less dry skin) may be recommended. Emollients should also be used as soap substitutes.
  • **Topical Corticosteroids:** These prescription creams or ointments are applied directly to the affected skin to reduce inflammation and are a primary treatment for flare-ups. The potency will depend on the severity of the eczema. Emollients should be applied about 30 minutes before topical corticosteroids.
  • **Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors:** Tacrolimus and pimecrolimus creams can reduce inflammation and may be used as steroid-sparing agents, particularly for long-term management or on sensitive areas.
  • **Antihistamines:** Oral antihistamines can help to relieve severe itching, especially at night, which can improve sleep quality.
  • **Antibiotics:** If the eczema becomes infected, topical or oral antibiotics may be prescribed to treat the bacterial infection. Combination antibiotic/steroid creams may be used for localized infections.
  • **Phototherapy (UV Light Therapy):** For widespread or persistent discoid eczema, exposure to ultraviolet (UV) light under medical supervision can help reduce inflammation.
  • **Wet Wraps:** Applying wet bandages over topical corticosteroids can enhance their effectiveness in severe cases.
  • **Systemic Immunosuppressants:** In very severe cases that don't respond to other treatments, oral medications that suppress the immune system may be considered by a dermatologist.

It's important to avoid known irritants and protect the skin from injury to help prevent flare-ups.

Key Features of Discoid Eczema

Look for these characteristic signs.

  • Circular or oval patches
  • Intense itching
  • Red or discolored skin
  • Swollen or raised patches
  • May be oozing or crusty
  • Often on limbs and trunk
  • Dry skin between patches
