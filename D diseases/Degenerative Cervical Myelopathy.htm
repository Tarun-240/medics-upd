Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy (DCM)

About Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy

Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy (DCM) is a progressive condition resulting from the degeneration of the structures in the cervical spine (neck). This degeneration, which can include spinal stenosis (narrowing of the spinal canal), disc herniation, and bone spurs, can compress the spinal cord, leading to a range of neurological symptoms.

DCM is a significant cause of spinal cord dysfunction in adults. Early diagnosis and appropriate management are crucial to slow its progression and improve patient outcomes.

Causes and Risk Factors of Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy

DCM is primarily caused by age-related degenerative changes in the cervical spine. These changes can include:

  • Cervical Spondylosis: Wear and tear of the discs and joints in the neck.
  • Spinal Stenosis: Narrowing of the spinal canal, often due to bone spurs or thickened ligaments.
  • Disc Herniation: Bulging or rupture of the intervertebral discs, which can compress the spinal cord.
  • Ossification of the Posterior Longitudinal Ligament (OPLL): Hardening of a ligament behind the spinal cord, leading to canal narrowing.

Risk factors that may contribute to or accelerate the development of DCM include:

  • Age: The condition is more common in older adults.
  • Genetics: A family history of spinal degeneration may increase risk.
  • Lifestyle factors: Heavy lifting or repetitive neck movements over time might contribute.
  • Previous neck injury: Trauma to the cervical spine can predispose individuals to earlier degeneration.

Symptoms of Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy

The symptoms of DCM can develop gradually and vary widely among individuals. Common symptoms include:

  • Neck pain and stiffness
  • Numbness, tingling, or weakness in the arms, hands, and sometimes legs
  • Clumsiness and difficulty with fine motor skills (e.g., buttoning shirts, writing)
  • Gait disturbances and balance problems
  • Changes in bowel or bladder function (in more severe cases)
  • Electric shock-like sensations that run down the spine and into the limbs (Lhermitte's sign)

The progression of symptoms can be unpredictable, with periods of stability followed by periods of worsening.

Diagnosing Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy

Diagnosis of DCM typically involves a combination of:

  • Neurological Examination: Assessing reflexes, strength, sensation, and gait.
  • Imaging Studies:
    • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): The most important imaging tool for visualizing the spinal cord and soft tissues, identifying compression and other abnormalities.
    • X-rays: Can show bony changes like spondylosis and spinal alignment.
    • Computed Tomography (CT) Scan: May be used to better visualize bony structures, especially in cases of OPLL or when MRI is contraindicated.
  • Electromyography (EMG) and Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS): May be used to assess nerve function and rule out other conditions.

Treating Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy

The goals of DCM treatment are to relieve pressure on the spinal cord, manage symptoms, prevent further neurological deterioration, and improve quality of life. Treatment options depend on the severity of the condition and the individual's overall health.

  • Non-Surgical Treatment: Often the first approach for mild to moderate symptoms.
    • Cervical collar: To limit neck movement and provide support.
    • Physical therapy: To improve neck strength, flexibility, and posture.
    • Pain medication: Over-the-counter or prescription pain relievers and anti-inflammatory drugs.
    • Steroid injections: Epidural or trigger point injections to reduce inflammation and pain.
  • Surgical Treatment: May be recommended for moderate to severe myelopathy or when non-surgical treatments fail to provide adequate relief. Surgical procedures aim to decompress the spinal cord by removing the structures causing compression (e.g., bone spurs, herniated discs, thickened ligaments). Common surgical approaches include anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF), cervical laminectomy, and laminoplasty.

Management and Prognosis of Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy

The long-term management of DCM often involves a combination of strategies, including ongoing physical therapy, pain management, and lifestyle modifications. The prognosis for DCM varies depending on the severity and duration of symptoms before treatment. Early intervention generally leads to better outcomes.

While DCM is a progressive condition, appropriate treatment can often stabilize or slow its progression and improve neurological function and quality of life for many individuals.

Common DCM Symptoms

Symptoms can develop gradually and vary.

  • Neck pain and stiffness
  • Arm/hand numbness or weakness
  • Clumsiness
  • Gait and balance issues
  • Bowel/bladder changes (severe)
