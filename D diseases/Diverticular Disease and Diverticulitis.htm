Diverticular Disease and Diverticulitis

About Diverticular Disease

Diverticular disease encompasses two related conditions that affect the large intestine (colon): diverticulosis and diverticulitis.

  • **Diverticulosis** is the presence of small pouches or sacs, called diverticula, that bulge outward through weak spots in the wall of the colon. This condition is very common, especially in people over the age of 40, and many individuals with diverticulosis have no symptoms.
  • **Diverticulitis** occurs when one or more of these diverticula become inflamed or infected. This can lead to more serious symptoms and complications.

Sometimes, the term "diverticular disease" is used when diverticula cause symptoms, even without inflammation or infection (this is sometimes referred to as symptomatic uncomplicated diverticular disease or SUDD).

Causes and Risk Factors

The exact causes of diverticular disease and diverticulitis are not fully understood, but several factors are believed to increase the risk:

  • **Low-fiber diet:** A diet lacking in fiber can lead to constipation and increased pressure during bowel movements, potentially contributing to the formation of diverticula.
  • **Age:** The prevalence of diverticulosis increases with age.
  • **Genetics:** There may be a genetic predisposition to developing diverticular disease.
  • **Obesity:** Being overweight or obese increases the risk.
  • **Lack of physical activity.**
  • **Smoking.**
  • **Certain medications:** Some medications, such as NSAIDs and steroids, may increase the risk.

Diverticulitis is thought to occur when stool or bacteria get trapped in a diverticulum, leading to inflammation and infection.

Symptoms

Symptoms can vary depending on whether you have diverticulosis or diverticulitis:

Diverticulosis

Most people with diverticulosis have no symptoms. If symptoms do occur, they may include:

  • Mild cramps in the lower abdomen.
  • Bloating.
  • Constipation or diarrhea.
  • Less commonly, bleeding from the rectum.

Diverticulitis

The symptoms of diverticulitis are usually more sudden and severe:

  • **Severe abdominal pain:** Most often in the lower left side of the abdomen, but can occur on the right side, especially in people of Asian descent. The pain may come on suddenly or gradually worsen over several days.
  • Fever and chills.
  • Nausea and vomiting.
  • Constipation or diarrhea.
  • Tenderness in the abdomen.

Diagnosis

Diverticulosis is often discovered during tests for other bowel issues, such as a colonoscopy. Diverticulitis is usually diagnosed during an acute attack based on:

  • **Medical history and physical exam:** Your doctor will ask about your symptoms and medical history.
  • **Imaging tests:**
    • **CT scan:** This is the most common imaging test used to diagnose diverticulitis as it can show inflammation and complications.
    • **Colonoscopy:** Usually avoided during an active diverticulitis flare-up due to the risk of perforation, but may be performed several weeks after recovery to rule out other conditions.
    • **Flexible sigmoidoscopy:** Similar to a colonoscopy but examines only the lower part of the colon.
    • **Barium enema or virtual colonoscopy:** May be used in some cases.
  • **Blood tests:** To check for signs of infection and inflammation.
  • **Stool tests:** To rule out other causes of gastrointestinal symptoms.

Treatment

Treatment depends on the severity of the condition:

Diverticulosis

For most people with diverticulosis who have no or mild symptoms, treatment focuses on preventing complications:

  • **High-fiber diet:** Eating plenty of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and legumes can help keep stools soft and promote regular bowel movements.
  • **Adequate fluid intake:** Drinking plenty of water helps fiber work effectively.
  • **Regular exercise.**

Diverticulitis

Treatment for diverticulitis aims to resolve the infection and inflammation and prevent complications:

  • **Uncomplicated Diverticulitis (mild symptoms without complications):**
    • **Rest:** To allow the colon to heal.
    • **Clear liquid diet:** Initially, to give the digestive system a break, followed by a low-fiber diet as symptoms improve, and then a gradual return to a high-fiber diet.
    • **Antibiotics:** Oral antibiotics are often prescribed to treat the infection.
    • **Pain relievers:** Over-the-counter medications like acetaminophen may be recommended; NSAIDs like ibuprofen and naproxen are generally avoided as they may increase the risk of complications.
  • **Complicated Diverticulitis (severe symptoms or complications such as abscess, perforation, fistula, or obstruction):**
    • **Hospitalization:** Often required for more intensive treatment.
    • **Intravenous (IV) antibiotics.**
    • **Pain management.**
    • **Drainage of abscesses:** May be done percutaneously (through the skin) with image guidance.
    • **Surgery:** May be necessary to remove the diseased part of the colon (colectomy), especially in cases of perforation, large abscesses, fistulas, or if there are recurrent severe episodes. In some cases, a temporary colostomy (where the colon is diverted to an opening in the abdomen) may be needed to allow the bowel to heal.

Prevention

While not always preventable, the following lifestyle changes may help reduce the risk of developing diverticular disease and diverticulitis:

  • Eat a high-fiber diet.
  • Drink plenty of fluids.
  • Engage in regular physical activity.
  • Maintain a healthy weight.
  • Avoid smoking.

Key Aspects

Understanding the differences.

  • **Diverticulosis:** Pouches in the colon, often no symptoms.
  • **Diverticulitis:** Inflammation/infection of these pouches, causing pain and other symptoms.
  • High-fiber diet is important for managing diverticulosis and preventing diverticulitis.
  • Diverticulitis may require antibiotics and, in severe cases, surgery.
  • Seek medical attention for severe abdominal pain or other concerning symptoms.
