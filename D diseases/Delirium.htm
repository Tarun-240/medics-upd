Delirium

About Delirium

Delirium is a serious disturbance in mental abilities that results in confused thinking and reduced awareness of the environment. The onset of delirium is usually rapid — within hours or a few days. It can often be traced to one or more underlying medical conditions or factors.

Delirium is characterized by changes in attention, awareness, and cognition. It is a medical emergency that requires prompt identification and treatment to address the underlying cause and prevent complications.

Causes and Risk Factors of Delirium

Delirium can be triggered by a wide range of factors, often in combination. Common causes and risk factors include:

  • Infections: Such as urinary tract infections (UTIs), pneumonia, or sepsis.
  • Medications: Certain drugs or combinations of drugs, especially anticholinergics, opioids, and sedatives.
  • Dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
  • Metabolic disturbances: Such as low blood sugar, kidney failure, or liver failure.
  • Surgery and anesthesia.
  • Pain, especially if poorly managed.
  • Constipation or urinary retention.
  • Sleep deprivation.
  • Alcohol withdrawal or withdrawal from other substances.
  • Head injury or stroke.
  • Severe illness or hospitalization, especially in the intensive care unit (ICU).
  • Underlying neurological conditions: Such as dementia or Parkinson's disease (these increase susceptibility).
  • Older age: Older adults are more vulnerable to delirium.
  • Multiple medical conditions.

Symptoms and Features of Delirium

The signs and symptoms of delirium can fluctuate in intensity throughout the day. Key features include disturbances in:

  • **Attention:** Difficulty focusing, sustaining, or shifting attention. Easily distracted.
  • **Awareness:** Reduced awareness of the environment. May seem disoriented to time, place, or person.
  • **Cognition:**
    • Memory impairment: Difficulty remembering recent events.
    • Disorganized thinking: Rambling or illogical speech, difficulty following conversations.
    • Perceptual disturbances: Hallucinations (seeing or hearing things that aren't there) or illusions (misinterpreting real stimuli).
    • Language difficulties: Trouble finding words or understanding speech.
  • **Behavioral changes:**
    • Agitation, restlessness, or aggression.
    • Withdrawal, quietness, or reduced activity.
    • Sleep-wake cycle disturbances: Daytime drowsiness and nighttime agitation.
    • Emotional lability: Rapid and unpredictable shifts in mood.

Diagnosing Delirium

Diagnosing delirium involves a thorough medical evaluation, including:

  • Medical history and physical examination: To identify potential underlying causes.
  • Mental status assessment: Evaluating attention, orientation, memory, language, and perception. Standardized tools like the Confusion Assessment Method (CAM) are often used.
  • Laboratory tests: Blood tests (to check electrolytes, kidney and liver function, infection markers), urine tests (to check for infection), and sometimes cerebrospinal fluid analysis.
  • Imaging studies: Brain scans (CT or MRI) may be necessary if a neurological cause is suspected.
  • Electroencephalogram (EEG): May be used in some cases to assess brain activity.

It's crucial to differentiate delirium from dementia, as they have different causes and management strategies. Delirium has an acute onset and fluctuating course, while dementia is typically chronic and progressive.

Treating Delirium

The primary goal of delirium treatment is to identify and address the underlying cause. Management strategies also focus on:

  • Treating the underlying medical condition: Such as infection, dehydration, or medication side effects.
  • Creating a safe and supportive environment:
    • Ensuring adequate lighting and reducing noise.
    • Providing frequent reorientation (reminding the person of time, place, and who they are).
    • Encouraging family involvement.
    • Avoiding restraints whenever possible.
  • Managing symptoms:
    • Medications (antipsychotics) may be used in some cases to manage severe agitation or hallucinations, but these are used cautiously and typically short-term.
    • Addressing pain, constipation, and sleep disturbances.
  • Preventing complications: Such as falls, pressure ulcers, and aspiration.

Preventing Delirium

Preventive strategies are particularly important for high-risk individuals, such as older adults in the hospital. These may include:

  • Addressing risk factors: Managing medications, ensuring adequate hydration and nutrition, preventing infections, and optimizing sleep.
  • Early mobilization.
  • Frequent reorientation.
  • Providing familiar objects and a consistent routine.
  • Vision and hearing aids for those who need them.

Key Features of Delirium

Look for these core disturbances.

  • Disturbed Attention
  • Reduced Awareness
  • Disorganized Thinking
  • Memory Impairment
  • Perceptual Disturbances
  • Fluctuating Course
  • Acute Onset
