Depression

About Depression

Depression, also known as major depressive disorder, is a common and serious mood disorder that negatively affects how you feel, the way you think, and how you act. It causes feelings of sadness and/or a loss of interest in activities you once enjoyed. It can lead to a variety of emotional and physical problems and can decrease a person's ability to function at work and at home.

It's important to remember that depression is more than just feeling "blue" or sad for a few days. It's a persistent condition that can interfere significantly with daily life. Fortunately, depression is treatable.

Symptoms of Depression

Depression can present with a wide range of symptoms that can vary in severity from mild to severe. Common symptoms include:

  • Persistent sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness.
  • Loss of interest or pleasure in most or all normal activities, such as hobbies or sports.
  • Significant weight loss when not dieting or weight gain (a change of more than 5% of body weight in a month), or decrease or increase in appetite nearly every day.
  • Insomnia (trouble sleeping) or hypersomnia (sleeping too much) nearly every day.
  • Fatigue or loss of energy nearly every day.
  • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive or inappropriate guilt nearly every day (not merely self-reproach for being sick).
  • Difficulty thinking, concentrating, or making decisions nearly every day.
  • Thoughts of death or suicide, or suicide attempts.
  • Agitation or restlessness.
  • Slowed thinking, speaking, or body movements.

To be diagnosed with major depressive disorder, symptoms must be present for at least two weeks and cause significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.

Causes and Risk Factors of Depression

The exact causes of depression are complex and not fully understood. It likely results from a combination of genetic, biological, environmental, and psychological factors. Some common risk factors include:

  • Family history of depression.
  • Major life changes, trauma, or stress.
  • Certain physical illnesses and medications.
  • Postpartum period in women.
  • Lack of social support.
  • Substance abuse.
  • Other mental health disorders, such as anxiety disorders.
  • Personality traits, such as low self-esteem or being overly pessimistic.

Diagnosing Depression

There is no single medical test to diagnose depression. A healthcare professional, such as a doctor or mental health specialist, will typically diagnose depression based on:

  • A thorough interview about your symptoms, feelings, thoughts, and behavior patterns.
  • A review of your medical history.
  • A physical exam to rule out other medical conditions that may be causing your symptoms.
  • Sometimes, blood tests may be done to check for thyroid problems or vitamin deficiencies that can mimic symptoms of depression.
  • The healthcare professional will use diagnostic criteria from the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5) to determine if your symptoms meet the criteria for a depressive disorder.

Treating Depression

Depression is highly treatable. Common treatment approaches include:

  • Psychotherapy (Talk Therapy): This involves talking to a therapist or counselor to learn coping skills, identify negative thought patterns, and develop strategies for managing symptoms. Types of therapy that are effective for depression include cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) and interpersonal therapy (IPT).
  • Medication: Antidepressant medications can help regulate brain chemistry and alleviate symptoms of depression. Different types of antidepressants are available, and a healthcare provider can help determine which one is right for you. It's important to take medication as prescribed and to be aware that it can take several weeks to notice significant improvement.
  • Lifestyle Changes: Making healthy lifestyle choices can also support recovery from depression. This includes regular exercise, a balanced diet, sufficient sleep, and avoiding alcohol and drugs.
  • Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT): In cases of severe depression that haven't responded to other treatments, ECT may be considered. It involves a brief electrical stimulation of the brain while the patient is under anesthesia.
  • Brain Stimulation Therapies: Newer therapies like transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) are also used for some individuals with depression.

Treatment plans are often tailored to the individual's specific needs and may involve a combination of these approaches. It's crucial to work closely with a healthcare professional to find the most effective treatment.

Seeking Help for Depression

If you think you might be depressed, it's important to reach out for help. Talk to your doctor, a mental health professional, or a trusted friend or family member. There are many resources available to support you.

Remember, you don't have to go through this alone, and seeking help is a sign of strength.

Common Depression Symptoms

Recognize these potential signs.

  • Persistent sadness
  • Loss of interest
  • Changes in appetite or weight
  • Sleep problems
  • Fatigue
  • Feelings of worthlessness
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Thoughts of death or suicide
