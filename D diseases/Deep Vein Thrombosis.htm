Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

About Deep Vein Thrombosis

Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is a serious condition that occurs when a blood clot forms in one or more of the deep veins in your body, usually in your legs. These clots can block blood flow and cause pain and swelling.

A significant concern with DVT is the risk of pulmonary embolism (PE), which happens when a part of the clot breaks off and travels to the lungs, potentially causing life-threatening complications.

Symptoms of Deep Vein Thrombosis

Symptoms of DVT can vary, and sometimes there may be no noticeable symptoms. When symptoms do occur, they may include:

  • Swelling in the affected leg (rarely in both legs)
  • Pain in your leg that often starts in your calf and feels like cramping or soreness
  • A feeling of warmth in the affected leg
  • Red or discolored skin on the affected leg
  • Veins that are visible on the surface of the skin

It's important to note that these symptoms can also be caused by other conditions. If you experience any of these, it's crucial to seek medical attention promptly.

Risk Factors for Deep Vein Thrombosis

Several factors can increase your risk of developing DVT:

  • Prolonged immobility: Sitting or lying down for long periods, such as during long flights or bed rest.
  • Surgery: Especially major surgery in the abdomen, pelvis, or legs.
  • Injury to a vein: Such as a fracture or severe muscle injury.
  • Pregnancy and the postpartum period: Hormonal changes and increased pressure on veins.
  • Birth control pills or hormone replacement therapy (HRT): These can increase the risk of blood clots.
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese increases pressure on veins.
  • Smoking: Damages blood vessels and increases the risk of clots.
  • Cancer: Certain cancers and their treatments can increase the risk.
  • Age: The risk of DVT increases with age (over 60).
  • Family history of DVT or PE: Genetic factors can play a role.
  • Certain medical conditions: Such as inflammatory bowel disease, lupus, and some blood disorders.
  • Central venous catheters: These can irritate blood vessels.

Diagnosing Deep Vein Thrombosis

Doctors use several methods to diagnose DVT:

  • Physical exam: To check for signs and symptoms.
  • D-dimer blood test: Measures a substance released when a blood clot breaks down. A negative result can help rule out DVT.
  • Duplex ultrasound: A noninvasive test that uses sound waves to visualize blood flow and identify clots. This is the most common imaging test for DVT.
  • Venography: A more invasive procedure where dye is injected into a vein, and X-rays are taken to look for clots. This is less commonly used now due to the effectiveness of ultrasound.
  • MRI or CT scans: May be used in some cases, especially to look for clots in veins that are harder to see with ultrasound, such as in the pelvis.

Treating Deep Vein Thrombosis

The primary goals of DVT treatment are to:

  • Stop the clot from getting bigger.
  • Prevent the clot from breaking loose and traveling to the lungs (pulmonary embolism).
  • Reduce the chance of another clot forming.

Treatment options include:

  • Anticoagulants (blood thinners): Medications that help prevent blood clots from forming and existing clots from growing. These can be given as pills, injections, or intravenously. Common examples include heparin, warfarin, and direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) like rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban, and dabigatran.
  • Thrombolytics (clot busters): Drugs given intravenously to dissolve large, severe blood clots. These are typically used in emergency situations due to the risk of bleeding.
  • Compression stockings: Elastic stockings that help improve blood flow in the legs and reduce swelling. They are often recommended for long-term use after a DVT.
  • Vena cava filter: A device that can be surgically inserted into a large vein in the abdomen (vena cava) to catch blood clots before they travel to the lungs. This is usually reserved for people who cannot take blood thinners or who have recurrent PEs despite anticoagulation.

Preventing Deep Vein Thrombosis

Several measures can help prevent DVT, especially in high-risk situations:

  • Move your legs frequently: If you're sitting for a long time, take breaks to walk around or flex and extend your ankles and knees.
  • Stay hydrated: Drink plenty of fluids to help keep your blood flowing.
  • Wear compression stockings: Especially during long periods of sitting or standing.
  • Take blood thinners as prescribed: If you're at high risk, your doctor may prescribe medication to prevent clots.
  • Get up and move after surgery or illness: As soon as it's safe to do so.
  • Maintain a healthy weight: Obesity increases the risk of DVT.
  • Avoid smoking: Smoking damages blood vessels.
