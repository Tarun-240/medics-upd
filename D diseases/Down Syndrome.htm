Down Syndrome

About Down Syndrome

Down syndrome is a genetic condition caused by an extra copy of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21). This additional genetic material alters the course of development, leading to characteristic physical features and often associated medical conditions and intellectual disability.

It is the most common chromosomal condition and occurs in people of all races and economic levels. The incidence of Down syndrome increases with the mother's age.

Causes of Down Syndrome

Down syndrome is typically caused by one of three types of chromosomal abnormalities:

  • **Trisomy 21 (Nondisjunction):** This accounts for about 95% of cases. It occurs when a pair of chromosome 21 fails to separate during the formation of the egg or sperm cell. As a result, the embryo receives three copies of chromosome 21 instead of the usual two.
  • **Translocation:** In about 4% of cases, part of chromosome 21 becomes attached to another chromosome (usually chromosome 14). The person still has two copies of chromosome 21, but also the extra material attached to another chromosome, resulting in the characteristics of Down syndrome. This form can be inherited from a parent.
  • **Mosaicism:** This occurs in about 1% of cases. It happens after fertilization when some cells in the body have an extra copy of chromosome 21, while other cells have the typical two copies. People with mosaic Down syndrome may have fewer characteristics of the condition depending on the proportion of cells with the extra chromosome.

Characteristics and Health Considerations

Individuals with Down syndrome share some characteristic physical features, though each person is unique and will have varying degrees of these features:

  • Flattened facial profile, especially the bridge of the nose.
  • Small head, ears, and mouth.
  • Upward slanting eyes (palpebral fissures) with small folds of skin on the inner corner (epicanthal folds).
  • Protruding tongue.
  • Short neck.
  • Small hands and feet.
  • A single deep crease across the palm of the hand (simian crease).
  • Poor muscle tone (hypotonia).

People with Down syndrome have an increased risk for certain medical conditions, including:

  • Congenital heart defects.
  • Hearing loss.
  • Vision problems (e.g., cataracts, strabismus, refractive errors).
  • Thyroid abnormalities.
  • Gastrointestinal issues (e.g., duodenal atresia, Hirschsprung's disease).
  • Sleep apnea.
  • Increased risk of leukemia in childhood.
  • Early-onset Alzheimer's disease.

Intellectual disability is also characteristic of Down syndrome, typically ranging from mild to moderate. However, individuals with Down syndrome are capable of learning and achieving many milestones throughout their lives with appropriate support and education.

Diagnosis

Down syndrome can be diagnosed before birth (prenatally) or after birth.

Prenatal Screening and Diagnostic Tests:

  • **Screening Tests:** These tests estimate the risk of a fetus having Down syndrome. They include blood tests (maternal serum screening) and ultrasound examinations (nuchal translucency scan).
  • **Diagnostic Tests:** These tests can definitively determine if a fetus has Down syndrome. They include chorionic villus sampling (CVS), amniocentesis, and cell-free DNA (cfDNA) testing (which is a screening test but has a higher detection rate than traditional screening tests).

Postnatal Diagnosis:

After birth, Down syndrome is usually suspected based on the physical characteristics of the baby. A karyotype, a blood test that analyzes the baby's chromosomes, is performed to confirm the diagnosis.

Management and Support

There is no cure for Down syndrome, but early intervention and ongoing support can significantly improve the quality of life for individuals with Down syndrome. Management often involves a multidisciplinary team of healthcare professionals, including:

  • Pediatricians and specialists (e.g., cardiologists, endocrinologists, gastroenterologists).
  • Early intervention specialists.
  • Speech therapists.
  • Occupational therapists.
  • Physical therapists.
  • Educational specialists.
  • Psychologists and counselors.

Support focuses on addressing individual needs, promoting development and learning, managing associated health conditions, and fostering independence and inclusion in the community.

Living with Down Syndrome

With appropriate support and opportunities, individuals with Down syndrome can lead fulfilling lives, attend school, participate in activities, form relationships, work, and contribute to their communities. The focus has shifted towards inclusion and recognizing the unique abilities and strengths of each individual.
