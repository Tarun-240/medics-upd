Muscular Dystrophy

Understanding Muscular Dystrophy

Muscular dystrophy (MD) is a group of genetic diseases that cause progressive weakness and loss of muscle mass. Abnormal genes (mutations) interfere with the production of proteins needed to form healthy muscle. There are many different types of muscular dystrophy, each with its own pattern of muscle weakness, age of onset, rate of progression, and genetic cause.

Some common types of muscular dystrophy include:

  • **Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD):** The most common type, primarily affecting boys. Symptoms typically begin between the ages of 2 and 6, with progressive muscle weakness starting in the legs and pelvis, then spreading to the arms, neck, and other areas.
  • **Becker Muscular Dystrophy (BMD):** Similar to DMD but with a later onset and slower progression. It also primarily affects boys.
  • **Myotonic Dystrophy (Steinert's Disease):** The most common form of adult-onset MD. It affects muscle strength as well as the ability of muscles to relax after contraction (myotonia). It can also affect other body systems.
  • **Limb-Girdle Muscular Dystrophy (LGMD):** A diverse group of disorders that affect the muscles of the hips and shoulders. The onset and progression vary depending on the specific genetic defect.
  • **Facioscapulohumeral Muscular Dystrophy (FSHD):** Primarily affects muscles in the face, shoulders, and upper arms, but can also involve muscles in the legs and abdomen. The onset and severity are variable.
  • **Congenital Muscular Dystrophy (CMD):** A group of dystrophies that are present at birth. The severity varies greatly, with some forms progressing slowly and others being more severe.

Common Symptoms of Muscular Dystrophy

The symptoms of muscular dystrophy vary depending on the type and the muscles affected. Common symptoms include:

  • **Progressive Muscle Weakness:** This is the hallmark symptom and worsens over time.
  • **Frequent Falls:** Due to muscle weakness, especially in the legs.
  • **Difficulty Rising from a Sitting or Lying Position:** Often requiring the use of arms to push off the floor (Gowers' sign).
  • **Trouble Running and Jumping:** Decreased ability to perform these activities.
  • **Waddling Gait:** An unsteady way of walking due to hip muscle weakness.
  • **Toe Walking:** Walking on the toes due to tight heel cords.
  • **Large Calf Muscles (Pseudohypertrophy):** In some types, like DMD, calf muscles may appear larger due to fatty and connective tissue replacing muscle tissue.
  • **Muscle Pain and Stiffness:** Can occur in some forms of MD.
  • **Learning Disabilities:** More common in some types of MD, such as DMD.
  • **Delayed Motor Skills:** Reaching milestones like walking later than expected.
  • **Scoliosis (Curvature of the Spine):** Can develop due to weakened trunk muscles.
  • **Breathing Difficulties:** Weakness of respiratory muscles can lead to problems.
  • **Swallowing Difficulties (Dysphagia):** Weakness of throat muscles can make swallowing hard.
  • **Heart Problems (Cardiomyopathy):** Some types of MD can affect the heart muscle.

Causes and Risk Factors of Muscular Dystrophy

Muscular dystrophies are caused by genetic mutations that interfere with the production of muscle proteins. These mutations are often inherited, meaning they are passed down from parents to children. The specific inheritance pattern depends on the type of MD:

  • **X-linked recessive:** The gene is located on the X chromosome. DMD and BMD are inherited this way, primarily affecting males who inherit the mutated gene from their mothers. Females can be carriers.
  • **Autosomal recessive:** Both parents must carry the mutated gene for their child to be affected. Some forms of LGMD and CMD are inherited this way.
  • **Autosomal dominant:** Only one parent needs to carry the mutated gene for the child to be affected. FSHD and some forms of LGMD and myotonic dystrophy are inherited this way.
  • **Spontaneous mutations:** In some cases, the genetic mutation can occur spontaneously in the affected individual and is not inherited.

The primary risk factor for muscular dystrophy is having a family history of the condition.

Diagnosis of Muscular Dystrophy

Diagnosing muscular dystrophy typically involves a combination of:

  • **Medical History and Physical Examination:** Evaluating symptoms, age of onset, and family history. A physical exam will assess muscle strength, reflexes, and coordination.
  • **Creatine Kinase (CK) Blood Test:** CK is an enzyme that leaks out of damaged muscles. High levels of CK in the blood can suggest muscle damage.
  • **Genetic Testing:** Blood tests can identify specific gene mutations associated with different types of muscular dystrophy. This is often the most definitive way to diagnose and classify the specific type of MD.
  • **Electromyography (EMG):** Measures the electrical activity of muscles and can help distinguish between muscle and nerve problems.
  • **Nerve Conduction Studies:** Often done with EMG to assess nerve function.
  • **Muscle Biopsy:** A small sample of muscle tissue is removed and examined under a microscope to look for characteristic changes in muscle fibers and the presence or absence of specific proteins.
  • **Imaging Tests:** MRI can be used to visualize muscle tissue and identify patterns of muscle involvement.
  • **Echocardiogram and Electrocardiogram (ECG):** To assess heart function, as some types of MD can affect the heart.
  • **Pulmonary Function Tests:** To evaluate lung function, as respiratory muscle weakness can occur.

Treatment and Management of Muscular Dystrophy

Currently, there is no cure for most forms of muscular dystrophy. Treatment focuses on managing symptoms, slowing the progression of the disease, preventing complications, and improving quality of life. Management often involves a multidisciplinary team of specialists:

  • **Medications:**
    • **Corticosteroids (e.g., prednisone, deflazacort):** Can help improve muscle strength and slow the progression of some types of MD, particularly DMD. However, they have significant side effects.
    • **Eteplirsen, Golodirsen, Casimersen, and Viltolarsen:** These exon-skipping drugs are approved for some individuals with specific mutations in the DMD gene. They aim to help the body produce a slightly shorter but still functional dystrophin protein.
    • **Ataluren:** Approved for some individuals with a specific type of mutation (nonsense mutation) in the DMD gene.
    • **Cardiac Medications:** Such as ACE inhibitors and beta-blockers, to manage heart problems.
    • **Respiratory Medications:** To help with breathing difficulties.
  • **Therapy:**
    • **Physical Therapy:** To maintain muscle strength and flexibility, prevent contractures (shortening of muscles and tendons), and improve mobility.
    • **Occupational Therapy:** To help with daily living activities and adapt the environment to promote independence.
    • **Speech Therapy:** To address problems with speech and swallowing.
    • **Respiratory Therapy:** To help manage breathing problems, including techniques to improve lung function and the use of assistive devices like ventilators.
  • **Assistive Devices:** Wheelchairs, walkers, braces, and other devices can help maintain mobility and independence.
  • **Surgery:** May be needed to correct scoliosis or release contractures.
  • **Nutritional Support:** A balanced diet and sometimes feeding tubes may be necessary to ensure adequate nutrition and prevent weight loss.
  • **Monitoring for Complications:** Regular check-ups to monitor heart and lung function, and to screen for scoliosis and other issues.

Research is ongoing to develop new treatments for muscular dystrophy, including gene therapy and other targeted therapies.

Support and Resources

Living with muscular dystrophy can present many challenges. Support from family, friends, support groups, and healthcare professionals is crucial. Resources are available to provide information, emotional support, and practical assistance.

  • Parent Project Muscular Dystrophy (PPMD)
