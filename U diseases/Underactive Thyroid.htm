About Underactive Thyroid

An underactive thyroid, also known as hypothyroidism, is a condition in which the thyroid gland doesn't produce enough thyroid hormone. The thyroid gland is a small, butterfly-shaped gland in the front of your neck. Thyroid hormones help control your metabolism, so hypothyroidism can slow down many of your body's functions.

Symptoms

Symptoms of an underactive thyroid can be mild and develop slowly over time. Common symptoms include:

  • Fatigue
  • Weight gain
  • Constipation
  • Dry skin
  • Hair loss
  • Feeling cold
  • Muscle weakness
  • Joint pain and stiffness
  • Depression
  • Memory problems
  • Slow heart rate

Causes

The most common cause of an underactive thyroid is Hashimoto's disease, an autoimmune condition in which your immune system attacks your thyroid gland. Other causes include:

  • Thyroiditis: Inflammation of the thyroid gland.
  • Iodine deficiency: A lack of iodine, which is essential for thyroid hormone production (less common in developed countries).
  • Thyroid surgery: Removal of all or part of your thyroid gland.
  • Radiation therapy: Treatment for certain cancers.
  • Certain medications: Such as lithium.
  • Congenital hypothyroidism: Being born with an underactive thyroid.

Diagnosing

Diagnosing an underactive thyroid typically involves blood tests:

  • Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) test: This is usually the first test used to screen for hypothyroidism.
  • Thyroxine (T4) test: Measures the level of T4, one of the main thyroid hormones.

In some cases, other tests may be done to find the cause of the underactive thyroid.

Prevention

There is no specific way to prevent most cases of an underactive thyroid. However, if you have a condition that increases your risk, such as Hashimoto's disease, regular monitoring of your thyroid function is important.

Treating

The standard treatment for an underactive thyroid is:

  • Thyroid hormone replacement medication: Usually levothyroxine, a synthetic form of T4. This medication restores adequate hormone levels.

Regular follow-up appointments and blood tests are necessary to ensure the correct dosage of medication.
