Uterine Fibroids

About Uterine Fibroids

Uterine fibroids are noncancerous growths that develop in or on the uterus. They are also known as leiomyomas or myomas. Fibroids can vary in size, from as small as a pea to as large as a grapefruit.

Many women have fibroids, and they often cause no symptoms. However, in some cases, they can lead to a range of symptoms and complications.

Types of Fibroids

Fibroids are classified based on their location in the uterus:

  • Submucosal fibroids: These grow in the inner lining of the uterus (endometrium) and can cause heavy bleeding.
  • Intramural fibroids: These are located within the muscular wall of the uterus (myometrium).
  • Subserosal fibroids: These grow on the outer surface of the uterus.
  • Pedunculated fibroids: These are attached to the uterus by a stalk and can grow either inside or outside the uterus.

Symptoms of Uterine Fibroids

Many women with fibroids have no symptoms. However, when symptoms do occur, they can include:

  • Heavy or prolonged menstrual bleeding
  • Pelvic pain or pressure
  • Frequent urination
  • Constipation
  • Backache or leg pain
  • Difficulty getting pregnant

The severity of symptoms often depends on the size, location, and number of fibroids.

Causes of Uterine Fibroids

The exact cause of uterine fibroids is unknown, but several factors are believed to play a role:

  • Hormones: Estrogen and progesterone stimulate the growth of the uterine lining and may also promote fibroid growth.
  • Genetic changes: Some fibroids have genetic changes that differ from those in normal uterine muscle cells.
  • Growth factors: Substances that help the body maintain tissues, such as insulin-like growth factor, may affect fibroid growth.
  • Extracellular matrix (ECM): The ECM is a substance that makes cells stick together. It is more abundant in fibroids and may contribute to their growth.

Risk Factors for Uterine Fibroids

Several factors can increase the risk of developing uterine fibroids:

  • Age: Fibroids become more common as women age, especially during the reproductive years (30s and 40s) until menopause.
  • Race: Black women are more likely to develop fibroids than women of other races. They may also develop fibroids at a younger age and have more severe symptoms.
  • Family history: Having a family member with fibroids increases your risk.
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese increases the risk.
  • Diet: Eating a lot of red meat and low amounts of green vegetables may increase the risk.
  • Vitamin D deficiency

Diagnosing Uterine Fibroids

Fibroids are often discovered during a routine pelvic exam. To confirm the diagnosis, a doctor may perform one or more of the following tests:

  • Pelvic exam: To feel for any abnormalities in the size or shape of the uterus.
  • Ultrasound: To visualize the uterus and fibroids. This can be done transabdominally (through the abdomen) or transvaginally (through the vagina).
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI): To provide detailed images of the uterus, fibroids, and other pelvic organs. This is often used for larger fibroids or to plan surgery.
  • Hysteroscopy: A thin, lighted tube with a camera is inserted through the cervix into the uterus to examine the uterine cavity. This is useful for diagnosing submucosal fibroids.
  • Blood tests: To check for iron deficiency anemia if heavy bleeding is present.
  • Biopsy: If the uterine lining appears abnormal, a biopsy may be taken to rule out cancer.

Treating Uterine Fibroids

Treatment for uterine fibroids depends on several factors, including the severity of symptoms, the size and location of the fibroids, and the woman's desire to have children.

Treatment options include:

  • Watchful waiting: If symptoms are mild or absent, no treatment may be needed. Fibroids often shrink after menopause.
  • Medications:
    • Hormonal medications:
      • Gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) agonists: These drugs shrink fibroids and are often used before surgery.
      • Progestin-releasing intrauterine device (IUD): Can help control heavy bleeding but doesn't shrink fibroids.
    • Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs): To help with pain.
  • Surgery:
    • Myomectomy: Surgical removal of fibroids while leaving the uterus intact. This is a good option for women who want to preserve fertility. Can be done hysteroscopically, laparoscopically, or through open abdominal surgery.
    • Hysterectomy: Surgical removal of the uterus. This is a permanent solution but results in infertility.
    • Uterine artery embolization (UAE): A minimally invasive procedure that blocks blood supply to the fibroids, causing them to shrink.
    • MRI-guided focused ultrasound surgery (MRgFUS): A noninvasive procedure that uses focused ultrasound waves to heat and destroy fibroid tissue.

Complications of Uterine Fibroids

While fibroids are usually not dangerous, they can sometimes lead to complications, including:

  • Anemia: Due to heavy menstrual bleeding.
  • Pain: Severe pelvic pain.
  • Infertility: Fibroids can interfere with implantation or block the fallopian tubes.
  • Pregnancy complications: Fibroids may increase the risk of miscarriage, preterm labor, and cesarean delivery.
  • Urinary problems: Fibroids can press on the bladder, causing frequent urination or difficulty emptying the bladder.
  • Constipation: Fibroids can press on the rectum.

Living with Uterine Fibroids

Living with uterine fibroids can be challenging, but several strategies can help manage symptoms and improve quality of life:

  • Follow your treatment plan: Work with your doctor to develop a treatment plan that's right for you.
  • Manage pain: Use pain relievers or other methods as recommended by your doctor.
  • Eat a healthy diet: A balanced diet can help manage symptoms and improve overall health.
  • Exercise regularly: Regular physical activity can help reduce pain and improve blood flow.
  • Reduce stress: Stress can worsen some symptoms. Practice relaxation techniques like yoga or meditation.
  • Get enough rest: Fatigue can make symptoms seem worse.
  • Track your symptoms: Keep a record of your symptoms to help your doctor monitor your condition and adjust your treatment as needed.

Treating Uterine Fibroids

Treatment depends on symptoms, fibroid size/location, and desire for future pregnancy.

  • Watchful waiting (for mild/no symptoms)
  • Medications
    • Hormonal medications (GnRH agonists, IUD)
    • NSAIDs (for pain)
  • Surgery
    • Myomectomy (fibroid removal, uterus preserved)
    • Hysterectomy (uterus removal)
    • Uterine artery embolization (UAE)
    • MRI-guided focused ultrasound
