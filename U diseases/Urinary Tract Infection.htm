About Urinary Tract Infection

A urinary tract infection (UTI) is an infection in any part of your urinary system — your kidneys, ureters, bladder and urethra. Most UTIs involve the lower urinary tract — the bladder and the urethra. Women are more likely to develop a UTI than are men.

Symptoms

Urinary tract infection (UTI) symptoms vary, depending on which part of your urinary system is infected.

Lower urinary tract infection (bladder, urethra)

  • Pain or burning with urination
  • Frequent urination
  • Feeling the urge to urinate despite having an empty bladder
  • Cloudy or bloody urine

Upper urinary tract infection (kidneys)

  • Pain in your upper back and side (flank)
  • Fever
  • Shaking chills
  • Nausea and vomiting

Causes

Urinary tract infections typically occur when bacteria enter the urinary tract through the urethra and begin to multiply in the bladder. Although the urinary system is equipped to keep out invaders, these defenses sometimes fail. When that happens, bacteria may take hold and grow into a full-blown infection in the urinary tract.

Most UTIs are caused by Escherichia coli (E. coli) bacteria.

Risk factors

  • Female anatomy. A woman has a shorter urethra than a man does, which shortens the distance that bacteria must travel to reach the bladder.
  • Sexual activity. Sexual intercourse can push bacteria into a woman’s urinary tract.
  • Menopause. After menopause, a decline in circulating estrogen causes changes in the urinary tract that make you more vulnerable to infection.
  • Use of certain kinds of birth control. Women who use diaphragms for birth control may be at higher risk of developing a UTI.
  • Urinary tract abnormalities. Babies born with urinary tract abnormalities may not allow urine to leave the body normally or cause urine to back up in the urethra, increasing risk of UTI.
  • A suppressed immune system. Diabetes or other diseases that impair the immune system can increase your risk of developing UTIs.
  • Catheter use. People who can't urinate on their own and use a tube (catheter) to drain urine from their bladder have an increased risk of UTIs.
  • Urinary tract surgery. Urinary tract surgery or an examination of your urinary tract using medical instruments can both increase your risk of developing a UTI.

Diagnosing

To diagnose a urinary tract infection, your doctor may ask you to provide a urine sample for laboratory analysis. The urine test looks for white blood cells, red blood cells or bacteria. A urine culture may be done to identify the specific bacteria causing your infection and help your doctor choose the best antibiotic.

If you have frequent UTIs, your doctor may recommend additional tests:

  • Imaging. A CT scan or MRI may be used to image your urinary tract.
  • Cystoscopy. A cystoscopy uses a long, thin tube with a lens (cystoscope) to see inside your urethra and bladder.

Prevention

You can take these steps to reduce your risk of urinary tract infections:

  • Drink plenty of water.
  • Wipe front to back.
  • Avoid potentially irritating feminine products.
  • Empty your bladder soon after intercourse.
  • Change your birth control method.

Treating

Urinary tract infections are typically treated with antibiotics. Your doctor may also prescribe a pain reliever that numbs your bladder and urethra to ease burning during urination, but pain relief usually occurs soon after starting an antibiotic.

If you have frequent UTIs, you may need to take a low-dose antibiotic for a longer time.

Treating

  • Antibiotics
  • Pain Relief
  • Long-term antibiotics for frequent UTIs
