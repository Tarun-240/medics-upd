About Urticaria

Urticaria, also known as hives, is a skin rash triggered by a reaction to an allergen. The rash consists of raised, itchy welts that can vary in size and appear anywhere on the body. Hives are a common condition, and for most people, the welts disappear within 24 hours to a few weeks.

Symptoms

The main sign of urticaria is an outbreak of raised, itchy welts on the skin.

Other symptoms that may accompany hives include:

  • Itchiness
  • Swelling (angioedema)
  • Redness
  • Pain

Causes

Urticaria occurs when certain cells in the body, called mast cells, release histamine and other chemicals into the bloodstream. This release is often triggered by an allergic reaction.

Common triggers include:

  • Food: Shellfish, fish, nuts, eggs, milk
  • Medications: Penicillin, aspirin, ibuprofen
  • Insect stings or bites
  • Pollen
  • Latex

Other causes

  • Stress
  • Infections
  • Exercise
  • Sun exposure

Diagnosing

Urticaria is usually diagnosed based on a physical examination of the skin.

Your doctor may also ask questions about your medical history and any potential triggers.

In some cases, allergy tests, such as skin tests or blood tests, may be recommended to identify the specific allergen.

Prevention

The best way to prevent urticaria is to avoid known triggers.

If you are unsure of your triggers, you can try to keep a food diary or avoid common allergens.

Treating

Urticaria treatment aims to relieve symptoms and address the underlying cause. Hives often disappear on their own, but treatment can provide relief from itching and other uncomfortable symptoms.

Treatment options may include:

  • Antihistamines: To block the release of histamine.
  • Corticosteroids: To reduce inflammation.
  • Epinephrine (adrenaline): For severe reactions.
  • Cool compresses: To soothe the skin.
  • Oatmeal baths: To relieve itching.
