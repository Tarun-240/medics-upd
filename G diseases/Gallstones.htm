Gallstones

About Gallstones

Gallstones are hard deposits that form in the gallbladder, a small, pear-shaped organ located beneath the liver. The gallbladder stores bile, a digestive fluid produced by the liver that helps digest fats.

Gallstones can range in size from as small as a grain of sand to as large as a golf ball. Some people develop just one gallstone, while others develop many.

Symptoms of Gallstones

Many people with gallstones don't experience any symptoms, often referred to as "silent gallstones." However, if a gallstone blocks a bile duct, it can cause symptoms, including:

  • Sudden, intense pain in the upper right abdomen: This pain, known as a gallbladder attack or biliary colic, can last from a few minutes to several hours.
  • Pain that radiates to the back or right shoulder
  • Nausea and vomiting

Other symptoms that may occur include:

  • Abdominal bloating
  • Indigestion
  • Fever (if an infection is present)
  • Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes) - this is rare, and usually means there is a stone in the common bile duct.

Causes of Gallstones

Gallstones form when substances in the bile harden. The exact cause isn't always clear, but several factors contribute to their formation:

  • High cholesterol: Most gallstones are made of cholesterol. High levels of cholesterol in bile can lead to the formation of cholesterol gallstones.
  • High bilirubin: Bilirubin is a substance produced when red blood cells are broken down. Conditions that cause the liver to produce too much bilirubin, such as liver diseases, some blood disorders and infections, can lead to pigment gallstones.
  • Concentrated bile: If the gallbladder doesn't empty completely or often enough, bile can become too concentrated, promoting gallstone formation.

Risk Factors for Gallstones

Certain factors increase the risk of developing gallstones:

  • Sex: Women are more likely to develop gallstones than men.
  • Age: The risk increases with age.
  • Obesity or overweight: Being obese or overweight increases cholesterol levels in bile.
  • Diet: A diet high in fat and cholesterol and low in fiber increases the risk.
  • Family history: A family history of gallstones increases your risk.
  • Diabetes: People with diabetes have a higher risk.
  • Rapid weight loss: Losing weight very quickly can increase the risk.
  • Certain medications: Some medications, such as estrogen and some cholesterol-lowering drugs, can increase the risk.
  • Ethnicity: Native Americans have a higher risk of developing gallstones.

Diagnosing Gallstones

Gallstones are typically diagnosed through:

  • Abdominal ultrasound: This is the most common and effective way to visualize gallstones.
  • Abdominal CT scan: Can be used if ultrasound is unclear or to look for complications.
  • Blood tests: To check for signs of infection, inflammation, or blockage of the bile ducts.
  • Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP): This procedure can be used to diagnose and sometimes treat gallstones in the bile ducts.
  • HIDA scan (cholescintigraphy): This imaging test can show how the gallbladder is functioning.

Treating Gallstones

Treatment for gallstones depends on the severity of symptoms. Options include:

  • No treatment ("watchful waiting"): If there are no symptoms, treatment may not be necessary.
  • Medications:
    • Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA): This medication can dissolve cholesterol gallstones, but it can take months or years to work and is not effective for pigment stones. Stones may recur after treatment stops.
  • Cholecystectomy (gallbladder removal surgery): This is the most common treatment for symptomatic gallstones.
    • Laparoscopic cholecystectomy: A minimally invasive procedure with a shorter recovery time.
    • Open cholecystectomy: Traditional surgery, which may be necessary in some cases.
  • Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP): If a gallstone is lodged in the bile duct, ERCP can be used to remove it.
  • Shock wave lithotripsy: This procedure uses shock waves to break up gallstones, but it is not commonly used.

Preventing Gallstones

While it may not be possible to prevent gallstones entirely, some measures can help reduce the risk:

  • Maintain a healthy weight: Obesity is a major risk factor.
  • Eat a healthy diet: Choose a diet low in fat and high in fiber.
  • Lose weight gradually: Avoid rapid weight loss.
  • Exercise regularly: Physical activity can help prevent gallstones.
  • Manage cholesterol levels: If you have high cholesterol, work with your doctor to manage it.
