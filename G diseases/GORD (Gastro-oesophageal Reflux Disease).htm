GORD (Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease)

About GORD

Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD), also known as acid reflux, is a chronic digestive disorder that occurs when stomach acid or, occasionally, stomach content flows back into the oesophagus (the tube that connects the mouth and stomach). This backflow irritates the lining of the oesophagus and causes a variety of symptoms.

It's important to distinguish between occasional acid reflux, which is common, and GORD, which is a more persistent and often more severe condition.

Symptoms of GORD

The most common symptoms of GORD include:

  • Heartburn: A burning sensation in the chest, often occurring after eating and may be worse at night.
  • Regurgitation: The sensation of stomach contents coming back up into the throat or mouth.

Other symptoms may include:

  • Chest pain: Can sometimes mimic heart pain (angina).
  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia): A feeling of food being stuck in the throat.
  • Sore throat: Chronic sore throat, hoarseness, or laryngitis.
  • Chronic cough: Persistent cough, especially at night.
  • Laryngitis: Inflammation of the voice box.
  • Asthma-like symptoms: GORD can trigger or worsen asthma.
  • Nausea
  • Globus sensation: The feeling of a lump in the throat.

Causes of GORD

GORD is primarily caused by problems with the lower oesophageal sphincter (LOS). The LOS is a ring of muscle at the bottom of the oesophagus that acts as a valve between the oesophagus and stomach. Normally, the LOS opens when you swallow to allow food to pass into your stomach, and then it closes to prevent stomach acid from flowing back up.

Factors that can contribute to GORD include:

  • Hiatal hernia: A condition in which part of the stomach protrudes through the diaphragm, which can weaken the LOS.
  • Weakened LOS: The LOS muscle may be weak or relax inappropriately, allowing acid reflux.
  • Increased stomach pressure: Conditions that increase pressure in the abdomen, such as obesity, pregnancy, or delayed stomach emptying, can contribute to GORD.
  • Diet: Certain foods and beverages can trigger reflux, including fatty or fried foods, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, and spicy foods.
  • Smoking: Smoking can weaken the LOS and decrease saliva production, which helps neutralize stomach acid.
  • Obesity: Excess weight puts pressure on the abdomen, increasing the risk of reflux.
  • Medications: Some medications, such as certain pain relievers, muscle relaxants, and blood pressure medications, can relax the LOS.
  • Delayed stomach emptying (Gastroparesis): If the stomach empties slowly, it can increase the pressure in the stomach.

Risk Factors for GORD

Factors that can increase the risk of developing GORD include:

  • Obesity or overweight
  • Hiatal hernia
  • Pregnancy
  • Smoking
  • Diet high in fat
  • Certain medications
  • Age: GORD is more common in older adults.
  • Connective tissue disorders: Diseases like scleroderma can weaken the LOS.

Diagnosing GORD

GORD is often diagnosed based on a description of symptoms. However, further tests may be needed to confirm the diagnosis, assess the severity of the condition, or check for complications:

  • Upper endoscopy (EGD): A flexible tube with a camera is inserted into the oesophagus to examine its lining and check for damage. A biopsy may be taken.
  • Oesophageal pH monitoring: A catheter with a pH sensor is placed in the oesophagus to measure acid levels over a period of 24 to 48 hours. A wireless capsule can also be used.
  • Oesophageal manometry: This test measures the pressure and function of the LOS and oesophageal muscles.
  • Barium swallow: X-rays are taken after drinking a barium solution, which coats the oesophagus and stomach, making them visible on the images.
  • Impedance-pH monitoring: This test measures both acid and non-acid reflux into the esophagus.

Treating GORD

Treatment for GORD aims to relieve symptoms, heal oesophageal inflammation, and prevent complications. Treatment options include:

  • Lifestyle modifications:
    • Weight loss: If overweight or obese.
    • Dietary changes: Avoiding trigger foods (fatty foods, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods).
    • Elevating the head of the bed: To help prevent acid reflux at night.
    • Eating smaller, more frequent meals.
    • Not lying down for 2-3 hours after eating.
    • Quitting smoking.
  • Medications:
    • Antacids: Provide quick, short-term relief of heartburn.
    • H2 receptor antagonists (H2 blockers): Reduce stomach acid production (e.g., ranitidine, famotidine).
    • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs): More potent acid reducers (e.g., omeprazole, lansoprazole, esomeprazole).
    • Prokinetics: Help the stomach empty faster and strengthen the LOS (e.g., metoclopramide, domperidone - less commonly used due to side effects).
  • Surgery:
    • Fundoplication: A surgical procedure to strengthen the LOS by wrapping the upper part of the stomach around it. Laparoscopic fundoplication is common.
  • Endoscopic Therapies:
    • Transoral incisionless fundoplication (TIF): A minimally invasive procedure to reconstruct the LES.
    • Stretta procedure: Uses radiofrequency energy to strengthen the LES.

Complications of GORD

If left untreated, GORD can lead to several complications:

  • Oesophagitis: Inflammation of the oesophagus.
  • Oesophageal stricture: Narrowing of the oesophagus, leading to difficulty swallowing.
  • Barrett's oesophagus: A condition in which the lining of the oesophagus changes, increasing the risk of oesophageal cancer.
  • Oesophageal cancer: Long-term GORD is a risk factor for adenocarcinoma of the oesophagus.
  • Respiratory problems: GORD can contribute to asthma, chronic cough, and other respiratory issues.
  • Dental problems: Stomach acid can erode tooth enamel.

Prognosis

With proper treatment, many people with GORD can find relief from symptoms. However, it is often a chronic condition that requires ongoing management. Lifestyle changes and medication can effectively control symptoms and prevent complications in most cases.
