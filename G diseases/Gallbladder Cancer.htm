Gallbladder Cancer

About Gallbladder Cancer

Gallbladder cancer is a relatively rare cancer that begins in the gallbladder, a small, pear-shaped organ located beneath the liver. The gallbladder stores bile, a digestive fluid produced by the liver that helps digest fats.

Because it is often discovered in its later stages, when the cancer has spread, gallbladder cancer can be difficult to treat. However, if it is found early, there is a better chance for successful treatment.

Symptoms of Gallbladder Cancer

In its early stages, gallbladder cancer often causes no symptoms. When symptoms do occur, they may include:

  • Abdominal pain, especially in the upper right portion
  • Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes)
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Loss of appetite
  • Weight loss
  • Fever
  • Itching
  • A lump in the abdomen

Many of these symptoms can also be caused by other conditions, making gallbladder cancer difficult to diagnose in its early stages.

Causes of Gallbladder Cancer

The exact cause of gallbladder cancer is not fully understood, but it is thought to develop when cells in the gallbladder begin to grow abnormally and form tumors.

Several factors can increase the risk of developing gallbladder cancer:

  • Gallstones: While most people with gallstones do not develop gallbladder cancer, they are a significant risk factor.
  • Chronic gallbladder inflammation: Long-term inflammation of the gallbladder, often caused by gallstones, can increase the risk.
  • Porcelain gallbladder: A condition in which the gallbladder wall becomes hardened and calcified.
  • Choledochal cysts: Abnormalities in the bile ducts.
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese increases the risk.
  • Age: The risk increases with age.
  • Sex: Gallbladder cancer is more common in women than in men.
  • Family history: Having a family history of gallbladder cancer increases the risk.
  • Exposure to certain chemicals: Some workplace exposures may increase risk.

Diagnosing Gallbladder Cancer

Diagnosing gallbladder cancer can be challenging, as early-stage cancer often causes no symptoms. It is often found incidentally during surgery for other conditions, such as gallstones.

The following tests may be used to diagnose gallbladder cancer:

  • Blood tests: To assess liver function.
  • Imaging tests:
    • Ultrasound: Often the first test used to examine the gallbladder.
    • CT scan: Provides detailed images of the abdomen and can help determine the extent of the cancer.
    • MRI: Can provide further information about the tumor and surrounding tissues.
    • Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP): An procedure to examine the bile ducts and gallbladder.
  • Biopsy: A tissue sample is taken to examine under a microscope. This can be done during an ERCP or through a needle biopsy.
  • Laparoscopy: A surgical procedure to examine the abdomen and take tissue samples.

Treating Gallbladder Cancer

Treatment for gallbladder cancer depends on the stage of the cancer. Options include:

  • Surgery:
    • Cholecystectomy: Surgical removal of the gallbladder. If the cancer is found very early, this may be the only surgery needed.
    • Extended cholecystectomy: Removal of the gallbladder, part of the liver, and nearby lymph nodes.
    • Radical surgery: For advanced cancers, may involve removing the gallbladder, liver, pancreas, and intestines.
  • Chemotherapy: To kill cancer cells, often used in combination with radiation therapy or after surgery.
  • Radiation therapy: To destroy cancer cells, may be used before or after surgery, or to relieve symptoms.
  • Immunotherapy: To help the body's immune system fight the cancer.
  • Targeted therapy: Using drugs that attack specific molecules involved in cancer growth.

Prognosis and Prevention of Gallbladder Cancer

The prognosis for gallbladder cancer depends on the stage at which it is diagnosed. Early-stage gallbladder cancer has a better prognosis than advanced-stage cancer.

There is no specific way to prevent gallbladder cancer, but some measures may help reduce the risk:

  • Maintain a healthy weight: Obesity is a risk factor.
  • Manage gallstones: If you have gallstones, discuss management options with your doctor.
  • Treat chronic gallbladder inflammation: If you have chronic inflammation, seek medical treatment.
