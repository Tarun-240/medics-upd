About Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome

Greater trochanteric pain syndrome (GTPS) is a condition that causes pain on the outer side of your hip. It involves the greater trochanter, the bony point at the top of your femur (thighbone), and the tendons and bursae (fluid-filled sacs) that surround it.

Symptoms

The main symptom of GTPS is persistent pain on the outside of your hip. This pain may:

  • Be sharp, burning, or aching
  • Worsen with activities like walking, running, or standing for long periods
  • Radiate down your outer thigh
  • Be more noticeable at night or when lying on the affected side
  • Cause stiffness and limited movement in the hip

Causes

GTPS is often caused by overuse or repetitive activities that irritate the tendons and bursae around the greater trochanter. Other causes and risk factors include:

  • Repetitive activities, such as running or cycling
  • Overuse of the hip muscles
  • Injury to the hip
  • Muscle weakness in the hip abductors
  • Uneven leg length
  • Abnormal gait or walking pattern
  • Tight iliotibial band (IT band)
  • Arthritis
  • Age (more common in middle-aged and older adults)
  • Being female

Diagnosing

GTPS is usually diagnosed through:

  • Physical examination, including specific movements and palpation of the hip area
  • Medical history review
  • Imaging tests (X-rays, MRI, or ultrasound) may be used to rule out other conditions or confirm the diagnosis

Prevention

You can reduce your risk of developing GTPS by:

  • Maintaining a healthy weight
  • Avoiding repetitive activities that strain the hip
  • Strengthening your hip abductor muscles
  • Stretching your hip muscles and IT band
  • Using proper form during exercise
  • Gradually increasing the intensity of your workouts

Treating

Treatment for GTPS aims to relieve pain and improve function. Options include:

  • Rest
  • Ice or heat application
  • Pain medication (e.g., NSAIDs)
  • Physical therapy, including exercises and stretches
  • Corticosteroid injections
  • Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injections
  • Surgery (in rare, severe cases)
