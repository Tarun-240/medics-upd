About Sepsis

Sepsis is a life-threatening condition that occurs when the body's response to an infection damages its own tissues and organs. It's a medical emergency that requires prompt diagnosis and treatment.

Symptoms

Symptoms of sepsis can vary, but may include:

  • Fever or low body temperature
  • Rapid heart rate
  • Rapid breathing
  • Confusion or disorientation
  • Severe pain or discomfort
  • Clammy or sweaty skin
  • Decreased urination

Causes

Sepsis is caused by the body's overwhelming response to an infection. The infection can start anywhere in the body, but common sources include:

  • Pneumonia
  • Urinary tract infections (UTIs)
  • Skin infections
  • Abdominal infections

Any type of infection can lead to sepsis, and it can develop in anyone. However, some people are at higher risk, including:

  • Older adults
  • People with weakened immune systems
  • Infants and young children
  • People with chronic illnesses (e.g., diabetes, lung disease, cancer)
  • People with severe wounds or burns

Diagnosing

Diagnosing sepsis involves a combination of:

  • Physical examination
  • Blood tests (e.g., to check for infection, organ function)
  • Other laboratory tests (e.g., urine tests, cultures)
  • Imaging tests (e.g., X-rays, CT scans)

Prevention

While sepsis is not always preventable, you can reduce your risk by:

  • Getting vaccinated (e.g., for pneumonia, flu)
  • Practicing good hygiene (e.g., handwashing)
  • Promptly treating infections
  • Managing chronic conditions effectively

Treating

Sepsis is a medical emergency and is typically treated in a hospital. Treatment may include:

  • Antibiotics (to fight the infection)
  • Intravenous fluids (to maintain blood pressure and organ function)
  • Oxygen therapy
  • Vasopressors (medications to increase blood pressure)
  • Surgery (to remove the source of infection, if necessary)
  • Organ support (e.g., dialysis, mechanical ventilation)
