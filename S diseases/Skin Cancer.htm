Skin Cancer (Melanoma)

Understanding Melanoma

Melanoma is the most serious type of skin cancer. It develops in melanocytes, the cells that produce melanin, the pigment that gives skin its color. Melanoma can occur anywhere on the body, but it most commonly starts on the trunk (chest and back) in men and on the legs in women. It can also develop on the face, neck, and scalp. Less commonly, melanoma can form in the eyes or in internal organs.

While melanoma is less common than basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma, it is more dangerous because it's more likely to spread to other parts of the body if not detected and treated early.

Common Signs and Symptoms

Melanoma often develops from a mole or other spot on the skin that you may already have. It can also appear suddenly as a new dark spot. Recognizing changes in your skin is crucial for early detection. The "ABCDE" rule is a helpful guide for identifying suspicious moles or spots:

  • **A - Asymmetry:** One half of the mole or spot does not match the other half.
  • **B - Border:** The edges are irregular, notched, or blurred.
  • **C - Color:** The color is uneven and may include shades of black, brown, tan, red, white, or blue.
  • **D - Diameter:** The spot is usually larger than 6 millimeters (about the size of a pencil eraser), although melanomas can sometimes be smaller.
  • **E - Evolving:** The mole or spot is changing in size, shape, color, or elevation, or a new symptom such as bleeding, itching, or crusting develops.

Other signs and symptoms of melanoma can include:

  • A new pigmented or unusual-looking spot on your skin.
  • A change in sensation (itchiness, tenderness, or pain) in a mole or spot.
  • Changes in the surface of a mole or spot, such as scaliness, oozing, or bleeding.
  • A dark streak under a fingernail or toenail.

Risk Factors

Several factors can increase your risk of developing melanoma:

  • **Excessive ultraviolet (UV) radiation exposure:** From sunlight and tanning beds. This is a major preventable risk factor.
  • **History of sunburns:** Especially blistering sunburns during childhood or adolescence.
  • **Having many moles or unusual moles (dysplastic nevi):** The more moles you have, the higher your risk. Unusual moles that look different from common moles are also a risk factor.
  • **Family history of melanoma:** Having a close relative (parent, sibling, or child) who has had melanoma increases your risk.
  • **Fair skin, freckling tendency, light hair, and blue or light-colored eyes:** These individuals have less melanin, which provides less protection from UV radiation.
  • **Weakened immune system:** Due to conditions like HIV/AIDS or organ transplant medications.
  • **Personal history of skin cancer:** If you've had melanoma or other types of skin cancer before, you have a higher risk of developing melanoma again.
  • **Older age:** Although melanoma can occur at any age, the risk increases with age.
  • **Xeroderma pigmentosum:** A rare inherited condition that makes the skin extremely sensitive to UV radiation.

Diagnosis

Early detection is key to successfully treating melanoma. Diagnostic procedures include:

  • **Skin examination:** A doctor will examine your skin, noting the size, shape, color, and texture of any suspicious moles or spots.
  • **Dermoscopy:** A special handheld lens that magnifies the skin and allows the doctor to see deeper structures of a mole.
  • **Biopsy:** If a mole or spot is suspicious, the doctor will remove all or part of it and send it to a laboratory to be examined under a microscope by a pathologist. Different types of biopsies can be performed (e.g., shave biopsy, punch biopsy, excisional biopsy).
  • **Sentinel lymph node biopsy:** If melanoma is diagnosed, this procedure may be done to determine if the cancer has spread to the nearby lymph nodes. The sentinel lymph node is the first lymph node that cancer cells are likely to spread to.
  • **Imaging tests:** If there is evidence that melanoma has spread, imaging tests such as CT scans, MRI scans, and PET scans may be used to look for cancer in other parts of the body.

The stage of melanoma is determined based on the thickness of the melanoma, whether it has ulcerated, the presence of cancer cells in nearby lymph nodes, and whether it has spread to distant sites. The stage helps guide treatment decisions and predict prognosis.

Treatment

Treatment for melanoma depends on the stage of the cancer, its location, and the patient's overall health. Treatment options may include:

  • **Surgery:** The primary treatment for early-stage melanoma involves surgically removing the melanoma and a surrounding area of normal skin (excision). The width and depth of the excision depend on the melanoma's thickness.
  • **Lymph node dissection:** If melanoma has spread to nearby lymph nodes, they may be surgically removed.
  • **Sentinel lymph node biopsy:** If the sentinel lymph node contains cancer cells, a more extensive lymph node dissection may be performed.
  • **Immunotherapy:** Drugs that help your body's immune system fight cancer cells. Different types of immunotherapy are used to treat melanoma, especially advanced melanoma.
  • **Targeted therapy:** Drugs that target specific mutations in melanoma cells. These are used in melanomas that have certain genetic changes.
  • **Radiation therapy:** May be used to treat melanoma that has spread to lymph nodes or distant sites, or in cases where surgery is not possible.
  • **Chemotherapy:** Less commonly used for melanoma compared to other cancers, but may be an option for advanced melanoma.
  • **Clinical trials:** Patients may have the option to participate in clinical trials evaluating new and innovative treatments for melanoma.

Treatment plans are highly individualized and are determined by a team of specialists, including dermatologists, surgical oncologists, medical oncologists, and radiation oncologists.

Prevention

The best way to reduce your risk of melanoma is to protect your skin from UV radiation:

  • **Seek shade:** Especially during the midday sun (typically between 10 a.m. and 4 p.m.).
  • **Wear protective clothing:** Long-sleeved shirts, pants, wide-brimmed hats, and sunglasses.
  • **Use sunscreen:** Apply a broad-spectrum sunscreen with an SPF of 30 or higher to all exposed skin, even on cloudy days. Reapply every two hours, or more often if you're swimming or sweating.
  • **Avoid tanning beds and sunlamps:** These emit harmful UV radiation.
  • **Examine your skin regularly:** Look for new moles or spots and changes in existing moles, freckles, or blemishes. Use a full-length mirror and a hand mirror to check all areas of your body.
  • **See a dermatologist for regular skin exams:** Especially if you have risk factors for skin cancer.

Important Information

Early detection is crucial for successful melanoma treatment. Be vigilant about changes in your skin and see a dermatologist if you notice anything new, changing, or unusual. Protect your skin from excessive sun exposure and avoid tanning beds to reduce your risk.
