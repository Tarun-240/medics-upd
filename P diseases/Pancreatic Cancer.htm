Pancreatic Cancer

Understanding Pancreatic Cancer

Pancreatic cancer begins in the pancreas, a gland located behind the stomach. The pancreas produces enzymes that aid digestion and hormones, including insulin, that help regulate blood sugar. Pancreatic cancer is often aggressive and difficult to treat, partly because it is frequently diagnosed at a late stage when it has already spread to other parts of the body.

The most common type of pancreatic cancer is adenocarcinoma, which arises from the exocrine cells that produce digestive enzymes. Less common types include neuroendocrine tumours (PNETs), which develop from the hormone-producing (endocrine) cells.

Common Symptoms of Pancreatic Cancer

Symptoms of pancreatic cancer often don't appear until the disease is advanced. When they do occur, they can be vague and may include:

  • **Jaundice:** Yellowing of the skin and whites of the eyes, often accompanied by dark urine and pale stools. This is caused by a blockage of the bile duct.
  • **Abdominal Pain:** Often a dull ache in the upper or middle abdomen that may radiate to the back. The pain can worsen after eating or lying down.
  • **Weight Loss:** Unintentional and significant weight loss, often due to poor appetite and difficulty digesting food.
  • **Loss of Appetite:** Feeling full quickly or not wanting to eat.
  • **Fatigue:** Feeling unusually tired and weak.
  • **Nausea and Vomiting:**
  • **Back Pain:** Can be a sign that the cancer has spread to nerves around the pancreas.
  • **New-onset Diabetes or Difficulty Controlling Existing Diabetes:** Pancreatic cancer can affect insulin production.
  • **Changes in Bowel Habits:** Such as diarrhoea or fatty stools (steatorrhea).
  • **Itching:** Generalized itching can occur due to the buildup of bilirubin.

It's important to remember that these symptoms can also be caused by other, less serious conditions. However, if you experience any of these, especially jaundice or persistent abdominal pain, it's crucial to consult a doctor.

Risk Factors for Pancreatic Cancer

Several factors can increase the risk of developing pancreatic cancer:

  • **Age:** The risk increases with age, with most cases diagnosed after age 55.
  • **Smoking:** Smoking is one of the most significant modifiable risk factors.
  • **Obesity:** Being overweight or obese increases the risk.
  • **Diabetes:** Long-standing diabetes may increase the risk.
  • **Chronic Pancreatitis:** Long-term inflammation of the pancreas.
  • **Family History:** Having a family history of pancreatic cancer increases the risk. Certain genetic syndromes, such as BRCA1/2, Lynch syndrome, and Peutz-Jeghers syndrome, are also associated with a higher risk.
  • **Race:** African Americans have a higher risk of pancreatic cancer compared to other racial groups.
  • **Diet:** A diet high in red and processed meats and low in fruits and vegetables may increase the risk.
  • **Alcohol Abuse:** Heavy alcohol consumption may be a risk factor, often through its association with chronic pancreatitis.

Diagnosis of Pancreatic Cancer

Diagnosing pancreatic cancer often involves a combination of imaging tests, blood tests, and biopsies:

  • **Imaging Tests:**
    • **CT Scan (Computed Tomography):** Provides detailed cross-sectional images of the pancreas and surrounding organs to look for tumours and assess their extent.
    • **MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging):** Can provide detailed images of the soft tissues in the abdomen.
    • **Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS):** A thin, flexible tube with an ultrasound probe is passed down the oesophagus to the stomach and small intestine. This allows for detailed imaging of the pancreas and nearby structures, and biopsies can be taken during the procedure.
    • **ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography):** A procedure where a dye is injected into the bile and pancreatic ducts, and X-rays are taken to look for blockages or abnormalities. Biopsies can also be obtained.
    • **PET Scan (Positron Emission Tomography):** May be used to look for the spread of cancer.
  • **Blood Tests:**
    • **Tumour Markers:** Tests for substances like CA 19-9, which can be elevated in some people with pancreatic cancer. However, these markers are not always accurate and are not used for screening.
    • **Liver Function Tests:** To assess if the bile duct is blocked.
  • **Biopsy:** A tissue sample is necessary to confirm a diagnosis of pancreatic cancer. Biopsies can be obtained during EUS, ERCP, or through surgery.

Once cancer is diagnosed, staging is done to determine the extent of the cancer, which helps guide treatment decisions.

Treatment of Pancreatic Cancer

Treatment options for pancreatic cancer depend on the stage of the cancer, its location, the patient's overall health, and preferences. Treatment may include:

  • **Surgery:** If the cancer is localized and hasn't spread, surgery to remove the tumour offers the best chance for a cure. Surgical procedures may include the Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy), distal pancreatectomy, or total pancreatectomy.
  • **Chemotherapy:** Uses drugs to kill cancer cells. It can be given before surgery (neoadjuvant) to shrink the tumour, after surgery (adjuvant) to kill any remaining cancer cells, or as the primary treatment for advanced cancer.
  • **Radiation Therapy:** Uses high-energy beams to kill cancer cells. It may be used before or after surgery, or as the primary treatment in some cases, often in combination with chemotherapy (chemoradiation).
  • **Targeted Therapy:** Drugs that target specific abnormalities in cancer cells. Examples include erlotinib and olaparib (for those with BRCA mutations).
  • **Immunotherapy:** Drugs that help the body's immune system fight cancer. Currently, immunotherapy has a limited role in treating pancreatic adenocarcinoma but may be used for certain types of advanced pancreatic neuroendocrine tumours.
  • **Palliative Care:** Focuses on relieving symptoms and improving quality of life for patients with advanced cancer. This can include pain management, nutritional support, and treatments to relieve jaundice or bowel obstruction.

Treatment plans are often multidisciplinary, involving surgeons, oncologists (medical, radiation), gastroenterologists, nutritionists, and other specialists. Due to the challenges in treating pancreatic cancer, participation in clinical trials is often encouraged.
