Paget's Disease of the Nipple

Understanding Paget's Disease of the Nipple

Paget's disease of the nipple, also known as mammary Paget's disease, is a rare type of cancer involving the skin of the nipple and usually the darker area around it (the areola). It is almost always associated with an underlying breast cancer, either ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) or invasive breast cancer. This condition accounts for a small percentage of all breast cancer cases.

The disease was first described by Sir James Paget in the 19th century, who noted its association with subsequent development of breast cancer. It's important to recognize that while the initial skin changes might appear benign, they are a sign of an underlying malignancy in most cases.

Common Symptoms

Paget's disease of the nipple typically starts on the nipple and can spread to the areola. Symptoms may include:

  • **Persistent, scaly, red rash:** Often described as eczema-like in appearance.
  • **Itching, tingling, or burning sensation:** In the nipple and/or areola.
  • **Nipple discharge:** Which may be yellowish or bloody.
  • **Crusting or flaking skin:** On or around the nipple.
  • **A flattened or inverted nipple:** Turning inward.
  • **Pain or soreness:** In the nipple area.
  • **Thickened skin:** On the nipple or areola.
  • **A lump in the breast:** Present in about half of the cases.
  • **Bleeding or oozing:** From the nipple area.
  • The symptoms may come and go, sometimes leading individuals to delay seeking medical attention, mistaking it for a benign skin condition.

It's crucial to note that if you experience any persistent changes to your nipple or areola, even if they seem minor or improve temporarily, you should consult a doctor for evaluation.

Risk Factors

While the exact causes are not fully understood, certain factors may increase the risk of Paget's disease of the nipple, largely due to their association with breast cancer risk in general:

  • **Age:** The risk of breast cancer, including Paget's disease, increases with age, with the average age at diagnosis being in the late 50s.
  • **Being Female:** Although rare, men can also develop Paget's disease of the nipple, but it is much more common in women.
  • **Family History of Breast Cancer:** A family history of breast cancer increases the overall risk.
  • **Genetic Mutations:** Certain gene mutations, such as BRCA1 and BRCA2, can increase the risk of breast cancer.
  • Other general breast cancer risk factors may also play a role.

Diagnosis

Diagnosing Paget's disease of the nipple involves several steps:

  • **Physical Examination:** A doctor will examine the nipple and breast.
  • **Skin Biopsy:** A small sample of the affected skin on the nipple and/or areola is taken and examined under a microscope to look for Paget cells, which are characteristic cancer cells. This is the definitive way to diagnose the condition.
  • **Mammogram:** An X-ray of the breast to look for any underlying tumors.
  • **Ultrasound:** May be used, especially in younger women or those with dense breast tissue, to further evaluate the breast.
  • **Breast MRI:** In some cases, an MRI may be recommended to get a more detailed image of the breast tissue.
  • If a lump is present, a biopsy of the breast lump will also be performed to determine the type and extent of any underlying cancer.

Because Paget's disease of the nipple is often associated with other forms of breast cancer, a thorough evaluation of the entire breast is essential.

Treatment

Treatment for Paget's disease of the nipple typically involves surgery to remove the affected tissue and address any underlying breast cancer. Treatment approaches may include:

  • **Surgery:**
    • **Mastectomy:** Removal of the entire breast, including the nipple and areola. This is a common treatment, especially if there is invasive breast cancer present. Breast reconstruction may be an option after mastectomy.
    • **Breast-Conserving Surgery (Lumpectomy):** Removal of the tumor and a margin of healthy tissue, along with the entire nipple and areola. This may be followed by radiation therapy to the remaining breast tissue to destroy any remaining cancer cells.
    • **Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy:** To determine if the cancer has spread to the lymph nodes under the arm.
  • **Radiation Therapy:** May be used after breast-conserving surgery to treat the remaining breast tissue.
  • **Chemotherapy:** May be recommended if the underlying breast cancer is invasive or has spread to lymph nodes.
  • **Hormone Therapy:** If the underlying breast cancer is hormone receptor-positive.
  • **Targeted Therapy:** If the underlying breast cancer has specific characteristics, such as HER2 positivity.

The specific treatment plan will depend on the extent of the Paget's disease, the presence and characteristics of any underlying breast cancer, and the individual's overall health and preferences.

Important Information

Paget's disease of the nipple is a rare condition that requires prompt medical attention. Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial for managing the disease and any associated underlying breast cancer. If you notice any persistent changes to your nipple or areola, it is important to consult your doctor without delay.
