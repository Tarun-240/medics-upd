Allergies

About Allergies

Allergies are immune system responses to substances that are usually harmless, such as pollen, pet dander, or certain foods. When an allergic person comes into contact with an allergen, their immune system overreacts, leading to various symptoms.

Symptoms of Allergies

Allergy symptoms vary depending on the type of allergy and the individual. Common symptoms include:

  • Sneezing
  • Runny or stuffy nose
  • Itchy or watery eyes
  • Coughing
  • Skin rashes or hives
  • Itching
  • Swelling
  • Shortness of breath
  • Wheezing

Causes of Allergies

Allergies are caused by a complex interplay of genetic and environmental factors. Key factors include:

  • Genetic predisposition: A family history of allergies increases your risk.
  • Allergens: These are the substances that trigger allergic reactions. Common allergens include:
    • Pollen
    • Dust mites
    • Pet dander
    • Mold
    • Certain foods (e.g., peanuts, tree nuts, milk, eggs, soy, wheat, fish, shellfish)
    • Insect stings
    • Medications
    • Latex
  • Immune system response: In allergic individuals, the immune system produces antibodies called IgE in response to an allergen. These antibodies trigger the release of chemicals like histamine, leading to symptoms.

Diagnosing Allergies

Diagnosing allergies typically involves a combination of:

  • Medical history: Your doctor will ask about your symptoms, their frequency, and potential triggers.
  • Physical examination: Your doctor will look for signs of allergies, such as skin rashes or nasal congestion.
  • Allergy testing:
    • Skin tests: These involve pricking or injecting small amounts of allergens into your skin and observing the reaction.
    • Blood tests: These measure the level of IgE antibodies specific to certain allergens.

Treating Allergies

The primary goals of allergy treatment are to relieve symptoms and prevent future reactions. Treatment options include:

  • Avoidance: The most effective way to manage allergies is to avoid the allergens that trigger them.
  • Medications:
    • Antihistamines: These block the effects of histamine and relieve symptoms like itching, sneezing, and runny nose.
    • Decongestants: These help to clear a stuffy nose.
    • Corticosteroids: These reduce inflammation and are available as nasal sprays, inhalers, and oral medications.
    • Leukotriene modifiers: These block the action of leukotrienes, chemicals that contribute to allergy symptoms.
    • Allergy shots (immunotherapy): These involve gradually exposing you to increasing amounts of an allergen to desensitize your immune system.
  • Emergency epinephrine: For severe allergies (anaphylaxis), an epinephrine auto-injector (e.g., EpiPen) is crucial.

Prevention of Allergies

While allergies cannot always be prevented, some strategies may help reduce the risk of developing them or manage them effectively:

  • Avoid known allergens: Minimize exposure to substances that trigger your symptoms.
  • Breastfeeding: Breastfeeding infants may help reduce their risk of developing certain allergies.
  • Introduce allergenic foods early: For some foods, like peanuts, early introduction may reduce the risk of allergy, but consult with your pediatrician.
  • Manage indoor environment:
    • Use allergen-proof bedding.
    • Vacuum regularly with a HEPA filter.
    • Keep humidity levels low to prevent mold growth.
