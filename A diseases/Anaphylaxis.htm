Anaphylaxis

About Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction that occurs rapidly and can affect the whole body. It's a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment.

The reaction can be triggered by various allergens, and prompt recognition and treatment are crucial to prevent serious complications or death.

Symptoms of Anaphylaxis

Symptoms of anaphylaxis can appear within seconds or minutes of exposure to an allergen and may include:

  • Skin reactions: Hives, itching, flushing
  • Respiratory problems:
    • Difficulty breathing
    • Wheezing
    • Tightness in the throat
    • Swelling of the tongue or throat
    • Hoarseness
  • Cardiovascular symptoms:
    • Rapid heart rate
    • Low blood pressure
    • Dizziness or lightheadedness
    • Loss of consciousness
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramps
  • Other symptoms: Anxiety, confusion

It's important to note that not all symptoms may occur in every case, but breathing difficulties, low blood pressure, and loss of consciousness are particularly dangerous.

Causes of Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis is triggered by an allergic reaction to specific substances, called allergens. Common allergens include:

  • Food:
    • Peanuts
    • Tree nuts
    • Shellfish
    • Milk
    • Eggs
  • Insect stings: From bees, wasps, hornets, or yellow jackets
  • Medications:
    • Penicillin and other antibiotics
    • Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
    • Latex

Less common causes include exercise, and in some cases, the trigger may be unknown (idiopathic anaphylaxis).

Diagnosing Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis is primarily diagnosed clinically, based on the rapid onset of symptoms involving the skin, respiratory, and/or cardiovascular systems after exposure to a likely allergen.

There are no specific lab tests to diagnose an ongoing anaphylactic reaction. However, blood tests (such as measuring tryptase levels) can sometimes support the diagnosis retrospectively.

Treating Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis is a medical emergency that requires immediate treatment. The most important treatment is:

  • Epinephrine (adrenaline) injection: This is the first-line treatment and helps to reverse the symptoms of anaphylaxis. It should be administered promptly using an autoinjector (e.g., EpiPen).

Other treatments that may be administered in a hospital setting include:

  • Oxygen
  • Intravenous fluids
  • Antihistamines and corticosteroids: To help with some symptoms, but they are not a substitute for epinephrine.
  • Bronchodilators: To help with breathing difficulties.

Anyone who has experienced anaphylaxis should be monitored in a hospital for several hours, as there can be a second wave of symptoms (biphasic reaction).

Prevention of Anaphylaxis

Preventing anaphylaxis involves avoiding known allergens and being prepared for accidental exposure:

  • Avoid known allergens: Strict avoidance of identified triggers (e.g., specific foods, insect stings, medications).
  • Carry an epinephrine autoinjector: Anyone with a history of anaphylaxis should carry an autoinjector at all times and know how to use it.
  • Wear a medical alert bracelet: To inform others of the allergy in case of an emergency.
  • Develop an emergency action plan: Work with your doctor to create a plan outlining steps to take in case of a reaction.
