Arthritis

About Arthritis

Arthritis is a general term for conditions that cause pain, swelling, stiffness, and decreased range of motion in the joints. It can affect one or multiple joints. There are many different types of arthritis, each with its own causes, symptoms, and treatment approaches.

The most common types are osteoarthritis (OA) and rheumatoid arthritis (RA). OA involves wear and tear of cartilage, while RA is an autoimmune disease.

Symptoms of Arthritis

The symptoms of arthritis vary depending on the specific type, but common symptoms include:

  • Pain
  • Stiffness
  • Swelling
  • Decreased range of motion
  • Redness
  • Warmth

Specific symptoms for different types of arthritis:

  • Osteoarthritis (OA): Pain that worsens with activity, stiffness in the morning or after rest, bony bumps.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA): Joint swelling, fatigue, fever, symmetrical joint involvement.
  • Gout: Sudden, severe pain, redness, and swelling in a joint, often the big toe.
  • Psoriatic Arthritis: Joint pain and swelling with scaly skin patches (psoriasis).

Causes of Arthritis

The causes of arthritis vary depending on the type:

  • Osteoarthritis (OA): Wear and tear of cartilage, aging, joint injury, obesity, genetics.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA): Autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks the joint lining.
  • Gout: Uric acid crystal buildup in the joints.
  • Psoriatic Arthritis: Associated with psoriasis; immune system dysfunction.
  • Infections: Some infections can trigger arthritis (e.g., Lyme disease).

Diagnosing Arthritis

Diagnosing arthritis involves a combination of:

  • Medical history: The doctor will ask about your symptoms, when they started, and any other medical conditions.
  • Physical examination: To check your joints for swelling, redness, warmth, and range of motion.
  • Imaging tests:
    • X-rays: To show joint damage.
    • MRI: To provide detailed images of soft tissues.
    • CT scan: To visualize bones and joints.
  • Blood tests:
    • To check for inflammation markers (e.g., C-reactive protein, erythrocyte sedimentation rate).
    • To check for specific antibodies (e.g., rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP).
    • To check uric acid levels.
  • Joint fluid analysis: To check for infection or crystals.

Treating Arthritis

The goals of arthritis treatment are to relieve symptoms, improve joint function, and slow disease progression. Treatment options vary depending on the type of arthritis:

  • Medications:
    • Pain relievers: Acetaminophen, NSAIDs.
    • Disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs): For RA, to slow disease progression.
    • Corticosteroids: To reduce inflammation.
    • Biologics: For RA, to target specific parts of the immune system.
    • Uric acid-lowering medications: For gout.
  • Therapy:
    • Physical therapy: Exercises to strengthen muscles and improve joint function.
    • Occupational therapy: Help with daily activities.
  • Injections:
    • Corticosteroid injections: To reduce pain and inflammation in a specific joint.
    • Hyaluronic acid injections: To lubricate the joint (for OA).
  • Surgery:
    • Joint replacement: For severe joint damage.
    • Joint fusion: To stabilize a joint.
  • Lifestyle changes:
    • Weight management: To reduce stress on joints.
    • Exercise: Regular physical activity.
    • Healthy diet: To reduce inflammation.
  • Alternative therapies:
    • Acupuncture
    • Massage
    • Supplements (e.g., glucosamine and chondroitin)

Prevention of Arthritis

While not all types of arthritis are preventable, some measures can help reduce the risk or delay its onset:

  • Maintain a healthy weight: To reduce stress on weight-bearing joints.
  • Exercise regularly: To strengthen muscles and support joints.
  • Avoid joint injuries: Use proper techniques during sports and activities.
  • Eat a healthy diet: Rich in anti-inflammatory foods.
  • Manage risk factors: Control blood sugar, cholesterol, and blood pressure.
  • Don't smoke: Smoking increases the risk of some types of arthritis.
