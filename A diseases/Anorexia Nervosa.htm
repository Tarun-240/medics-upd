Anorexia Nervosa

About Anorexia Nervosa

Anorexia nervosa is a serious eating disorder characterized by an abnormally low body weight, an intense fear of gaining weight, and a distorted perception of weight or shape.

People with anorexia place a high value on controlling their weight and shape, using extreme efforts that tend to significantly interfere with their lives. It's not about food, it's an unhealthy way to cope with emotional problems.

Symptoms of Anorexia Nervosa

Anorexia nervosa can have a wide range of physical, emotional, and behavioral symptoms, including:

  • Significant weight loss
  • Intense fear of gaining weight
  • Distorted body image
  • Restricted eating patterns
  • Preoccupation with food, weight, and calories
  • Refusal to eat certain foods
  • Denial of hunger
  • Excessive exercise
  • Fatigue
  • Dizziness or fainting
  • Thinning hair
  • Dry or yellowish skin
  • Brittle nails
  • Low blood pressure
  • Irregular heartbeat
  • Amenorrhea (loss of menstruation) in females
  • Social withdrawal
  • Irritability or depressed mood

Causes of Anorexia Nervosa

The exact cause of anorexia nervosa is unknown. It likely results from a combination of biological, psychological, and environmental factors.

  • Genetics: Some people may have a genetic predisposition to developing eating disorders.
  • Brain chemistry: Imbalances in brain chemicals may play a role.
  • Psychological factors:
    • Perfectionism
    • Anxiety
    • Low self-esteem
    • Difficulty coping with stress
  • Sociocultural factors: Cultural emphasis on thinness and appearance can contribute.

Diagnosing Anorexia Nervosa

Diagnosing anorexia nervosa typically involves:

  • Physical exam: To check for physical signs of malnutrition.
  • Psychological evaluation: To assess thoughts, feelings, and eating behaviors.
  • Laboratory tests: To check for medical complications.
  • Diagnostic criteria: The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5) provides specific criteria for diagnosis.

Treating Anorexia Nervosa

Treatment for anorexia nervosa aims to restore a healthy weight, treat psychological issues, and achieve long-term recovery. It usually involves a team approach:

  • Medical care: To address medical complications and stabilize the patient.
  • Nutritional counseling: To help establish healthy eating patterns and restore weight.
  • Psychotherapy:
    • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): To address distorted thoughts and behaviors.
    • Family-Based Therapy (FBT): Especially for adolescents, to involve the family in the refeeding process.
  • Medications: To treat co-occurring conditions like depression or anxiety (not for anorexia itself).
  • Hospitalization: For severe cases, when the patient is medically unstable or severely malnourished.

Prevention of Anorexia Nervosa

It may not be possible to prevent anorexia nervosa, but some measures can help promote a healthy relationship with food and body image:

  • Promote healthy body image: Encourage acceptance of natural body shapes and sizes.
  • Encourage healthy eating habits: Focus on balanced nutrition and regular meals.
  • Address emotional issues: Teach healthy coping mechanisms for stress and difficult emotions.
  • Limit media influence: Be critical of media messages that promote unrealistic beauty standards.
  • Early intervention: Seek help early if you notice disordered eating patterns or concerns about body image.
