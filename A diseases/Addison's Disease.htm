Addison's Disease

About Addison's Disease

Addison's disease, also known as primary adrenal insufficiency, is a rare endocrine disorder in which the adrenal glands do not produce enough of certain hormones, primarily cortisol and often aldosterone.

These hormones play crucial roles in regulating various bodily functions, including metabolism, immune system function, blood pressure, and stress response.

Symptoms of Addison's Disease

Symptoms of Addison's disease often develop slowly over time, and may include:

  • Chronic fatigue and muscle weakness
  • Loss of appetite and weight loss
  • Hyperpigmentation (darkening of the skin), especially in skin folds, scars, and gums
  • Low blood pressure, sometimes leading to dizziness or fainting
  • Salt craving
  • Nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea
  • Abdominal pain
  • Muscle and joint pain
  • Irritability and depression

In some cases, a sudden worsening of symptoms can occur, leading to an adrenal crisis, a life-threatening condition.

Causes of Addison's Disease

Addison's disease occurs when the adrenal glands are damaged. The most common causes are:

  • Autoimmune disease: The body's immune system mistakenly attacks the adrenal glands. This is the most common cause in developed countries.
  • Tuberculosis (TB): An infection that can damage the adrenal glands. This is a more common cause in developing countries.

Other possible causes include:

  • Other infections of the adrenal glands
  • Cancer spreading to the adrenal glands
  • Certain medications
  • Genetic factors

Diagnosing Addison's Disease

Diagnosing Addison's disease involves:

  • Medical history and physical examination
  • Blood tests: To measure levels of cortisol, sodium, potassium, and ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)
  • ACTH stimulation test: The most specific test. Measures how the adrenal glands respond to an injection of ACTH.
  • Imaging tests: Such as CT scans of the adrenal glands, to look for structural abnormalities or damage.

Treating Addison's Disease

Treatment for Addison's disease involves hormone replacement therapy:

  • Corticosteroids: Such as hydrocortisone or prednisone, to replace cortisol.
  • Mineralocorticoids: Such as fludrocortisone, to replace aldosterone.

Patients may also need to increase their medication dosage during times of stress, illness, or surgery.

Adrenal crisis requires immediate medical attention and is treated with:

  • Intravenous corticosteroids
  • Intravenous fluids

Prevention of Addison's Disease

There is no way to prevent Addison's disease that is caused by autoimmune destruction of the adrenal glands.

If Addison's disease is caused by an infection, prompt treatment of the infection may help to prevent it.
