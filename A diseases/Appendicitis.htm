Appendicitis

About Appendicitis

Appendicitis is an inflammation of the appendix, a small, finger-shaped pouch that projects from your colon on the lower right side of your abdomen.

Appendicitis is a medical emergency. If left untreated, an inflamed appendix can rupture, causing infection to spread throughout the abdomen (peritonitis).

Symptoms of Appendicitis

Appendicitis symptoms may include:

  • Sudden pain that begins on the right side of the lower abdomen
  • Sudden pain that begins around your navel and often shifts to your lower right abdomen
  • Pain that worsens if you cough, walk or make other jarring movements
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Loss of appetite
  • Low-grade fever
  • Constipation or diarrhea
  • Abdominal swelling

The location of your pain may vary, depending on your age and the position of your appendix.

Causes of Appendicitis

Appendicitis is most often caused by a blockage in the lining of the appendix that results in infection. The blockage may be due to:

  • A hard piece of stool
  • An enlarged lymphoid follicle
  • Intestinal worms
  • Tumor

Diagnosing Appendicitis

Diagnosing appendicitis may involve:

  • Physical exam: Your doctor may press on your abdomen to check for tenderness and perform other tests.
  • Blood test: To look for signs of infection.
  • Urine test: To rule out a urinary tract infection or kidney stone.
  • Imaging tests:
    • CT scan: To visualize your appendix and look for inflammation.
    • Ultrasound: Can also be used to visualize the appendix.
    • X-ray: Of the abdomen.

Treating Appendicitis

Treatment for appendicitis usually involves surgery to remove the inflamed appendix.

  • Surgery (Appendectomy):
    • Open surgery: A traditional surgical procedure.
    • Laparoscopic surgery: A minimally invasive procedure.
  • Antibiotics: To treat infection before and after surgery.
  • Fasting: You may be asked not to eat or drink before surgery.
  • Pain medication: To help with pain.

Prevention of Appendicitis

There's no known way to prevent appendicitis. However, prompt medical attention when symptoms occur can help prevent complications.
