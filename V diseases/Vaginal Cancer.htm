About Vaginal Cancer

Vaginal cancer is a rare type of cancer that occurs in the vagina — the muscular tube that connects the uterus with the outer genitals. It most commonly occurs in the cells lining the surface of the vagina, also called the vaginal canal.

Symptoms

Early vaginal cancer may not cause any symptoms. As it progresses, signs and symptoms can include:

  • Unusual vaginal bleeding, such as after intercourse or after menopause
  • Vaginal discharge
  • A lump or mass in the vagina
  • Pain in the pelvic area
  • Pain during intercourse
  • Frequent or painful urination
  • Constipation

Causes

The exact causes of vaginal cancer aren't clear. But it's most likely to begin when healthy cells in the vagina develop changes (mutations) in their DNA. A cell's DNA contains instructions that tell a cell what to do. The mutations tell the cells to grow quickly and to continue living when healthy cells would die. The accumulating abnormal cells form a tumor.

Types of vaginal cancer

  • Squamous cell carcinoma.
  • Vaginal adenocarcinoma.
  • Vaginal melanoma.
  • Vaginal sarcoma.

Diagnosing

Vaginal cancer is often discovered during a routine pelvic exam or Pap test. If your doctor suspects vaginal cancer, you'll likely have more tests to confirm the diagnosis, including:

  • Examination of the vagina.
  • Biopsy.
  • Imaging tests.
  • Cystoscopy.
  • Proctoscopy.

Prevention

There's no sure way to prevent vaginal cancer. However, certain strategies may reduce your risk:

  • Get the HPV vaccine.
  • Have regular Pap tests.
  • Don't smoke.
  • Treat precancerous conditions.

Treating

Treatment options for vaginal cancer depend on several factors, including the stage of cancer, the size and location of the tumor, and your overall health.

Options include:

  • Surgery.
  • Radiation therapy.
  • Chemotherapy.
  • Immunotherapy.
