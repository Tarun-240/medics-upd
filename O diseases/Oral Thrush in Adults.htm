Oral Thrush in Adults

Understanding Oral Thrush

Oral thrush, also known as oral candidiasis, is a fungal infection of the mouth caused by an overgrowth of the yeast *Candida albicans*. This yeast is normally present in the mouth in small amounts and is usually kept in check by the immune system and other microorganisms. However, when the balance of the oral microbiome is disrupted, *Candida* can multiply and lead to thrush. While it's more common in infants, older adults, and individuals with weakened immune systems, oral thrush can affect adults of any age.

Common Symptoms of Oral Thrush in Adults

The symptoms of oral thrush in adults can vary, but often include:

  • **White or Creamy Lesions:** These are the most characteristic sign and can appear on the tongue, inner cheeks, gums, palate (roof of the mouth), and throat. They may look slightly raised and have a cottage cheese-like appearance.
  • **Redness or Soreness:** The tissues under the white patches can be red, inflamed, and sore.
  • **Pain or Burning Sensation:** You may experience a burning feeling in your mouth or throat.
  • **Loss of Taste:** Some individuals report a temporary loss of taste.
  • **Difficulty Swallowing (Dysphagia):** In more severe cases, especially if the infection spreads to the oesophagus.
  • **Cracking and Redness at the Corners of the Mouth (Angular Cheilitis):** This can sometimes occur alongside oral thrush.
  • **Slight Bleeding:** If the white patches are rubbed or scraped.

Common Causes and Risk Factors in Adults

Several factors can increase the risk of developing oral thrush in adults by disrupting the normal balance of microorganisms in the mouth or weakening the immune system:

  • **Weakened Immune System:** Conditions like HIV/AIDS, cancer, or organ transplant can impair the immune system's ability to control *Candida* growth.
  • **Certain Medications:**
    • **Corticosteroids (inhaled or oral):** Inhaled corticosteroids for asthma or COPD can increase the risk if the mouth is not rinsed properly after use.
    • **Antibiotics:** Can kill beneficial bacteria in the mouth, allowing *Candida* to overgrow.
    • **Immunosuppressants:** Medications taken after organ transplantation or for autoimmune diseases.
  • **Uncontrolled Diabetes:** High blood sugar levels can promote the growth of *Candida*.
  • **Dentures (especially if poorly fitting or not cleaned properly):** Dentures can create a favorable environment for *Candida* to thrive.
  • **Dry Mouth (Xerostomia):** Saliva helps to cleanse the mouth and control microbial growth. Conditions or medications that cause dry mouth can increase the risk of thrush.
  • **Poor Oral Hygiene:** Not brushing and flossing regularly can contribute to an overgrowth of yeast.
  • **Nutritional Deficiencies:** Lack of certain vitamins or minerals can affect the immune system.
  • **Smoking:** Can irritate the oral tissues and disrupt the oral microbiome.

Diagnosis of Oral Thrush

Oral thrush is usually diagnosed based on a visual examination of the mouth by a doctor or dentist. The characteristic white lesions are often sufficient for diagnosis. In some cases, the healthcare provider may:

  • **Gently scrape a small lesion:** The sample can then be examined under a microscope to identify *Candida* yeast.
  • **Order a blood test:** To check for underlying medical conditions like diabetes or immune deficiencies, especially if thrush is recurrent or severe.
  • **Perform an endoscopy:** If thrush has spread to the oesophagus, an endoscopy may be done to visualize the oesophageal lining and take biopsies.

Treatment of Oral Thrush in Adults

Treatment for oral thrush in adults typically involves antifungal medications. The specific treatment will depend on the severity of the infection and the individual's overall health:

  • **Antifungal Mouthwashes or Lozenges:** For mild to moderate thrush, topical antifungal medications are often prescribed. These may include:
    • **Nystatin suspension:** A liquid mouthwash that you swish in your mouth and then swallow or spit out, usually several times a day.
    • **Clotrimazole lozenges:** These dissolve slowly in the mouth, releasing the antifungal medication.
  • **Oral Antifungal Medications:** For more severe or persistent thrush, or if it has spread to the oesophagus, oral antifungal medications in pill or capsule form may be necessary. These can include:
    • **Fluconazole**
    • **Itraconazole**
    • **Posaconazole** (less common, used for resistant infections)
  • **Addressing Underlying Causes:** It's important to identify and manage any underlying conditions that may be contributing to the thrush, such as uncontrolled diabetes or a weakened immune system. This may involve adjusting medications or treating the underlying illness.
  • **Improving Oral Hygiene:** Maintaining good oral hygiene is crucial for both treating and preventing thrush. This includes regular brushing, flossing, and cleaning dentures properly.

It's important to complete the entire course of antifungal medication as prescribed by your doctor, even if your symptoms improve.

Self-Care and Prevention Tips for Adults

In addition to medical treatment, these self-care measures and preventive tips can be helpful:

  • **Practice Good Oral Hygiene:** Brush your teeth at least twice a day and floss daily.
  • **Rinse Your Mouth After Using Inhaled Corticosteroids:** Use a spacer with your inhaler and rinse your mouth with water after each use to remove any residual medication.
  • **Clean Dentures Regularly and Properly:** Remove dentures at night and clean them thoroughly according to your dentist's instructions. Ensure they fit well.
  • **Manage Underlying Conditions:** Keep conditions like diabetes well-controlled.
  • **Maintain a Healthy Diet:** A balanced diet can support a healthy immune system.
  • **Stay Hydrated:** Adequate saliva production helps to keep *Candida* in check.
  • **Avoid Harsh Mouthwashes:** Some mouthwashes can disrupt the normal oral flora.
  • **Consider Probiotics:** Some studies suggest that probiotics may help maintain a healthy balance of microorganisms in the mouth, but more research is needed.
  • **If You Have Dry Mouth:** Talk to your doctor about ways to manage it, such as using artificial saliva products.

If you suspect you have oral thrush, it's best to consult a healthcare professional for proper diagnosis and treatment. Self-treating may delay appropriate care and could potentially worsen the condition.

Helpful Tips for Oral Thrush

Simple steps to manage and prevent oral thrush in adults.

  • Maintain good oral hygiene
  • Rinse mouth after using inhalers
  • Clean dentures properly
  • Manage underlying conditions
  • Complete prescribed medications
