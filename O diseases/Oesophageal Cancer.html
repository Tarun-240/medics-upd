Oesophageal Cancer

Understanding Oesophageal Cancer

Oesophageal cancer is a type of cancer that occurs in the oesophagus, a long, hollow tube that runs from the throat to the stomach. The oesophagus helps move swallowed food to the stomach through a process called peristalsis. Cancer develops when cells in the lining of the oesophagus grow uncontrollably and form a tumour.

There are two main types of oesophageal cancer:

  • **Squamous Cell Carcinoma:** This type arises from the flat cells lining the surface of the oesophagus. It is often associated with smoking and alcohol use.
  • **Adenocarcinoma:** This type develops in the glandular cells in the lining of the oesophagus, often occurring in the lower part of the oesophagus near the stomach. It is frequently linked to chronic acid reflux (GERD) and Barrett's oesophagus.

Less common types of oesophageal cancer include small cell carcinoma, sarcoma, and lymphoma.

Common Symptoms of Oesophageal Cancer

Symptoms of oesophageal cancer may not appear until the cancer is advanced. Early symptoms can be subtle and easily dismissed. As the cancer grows, more noticeable symptoms may include:

  • **Dysphagia (Difficulty Swallowing):** This is the most common symptom. Initially, there may be trouble swallowing solid foods, which can progress to difficulty swallowing soft foods and liquids.
  • **Weight Loss:** Often unintentional and significant due to difficulty eating.
  • **Chest Pain:** A burning sensation or pressure in the chest, sometimes behind the breastbone.
  • **Heartburn or Acid Reflux:** Worsening or new onset of heartburn.
  • **Hoarseness:** Changes in voice due to the tumour affecting the nerves to the larynx.
  • **Cough:** Chronic cough, sometimes with blood.
  • **Vomiting:** Especially after eating.
  • **Pain Behind the Breastbone or in the Throat:** May be associated with swallowing.
  • **Fatigue:** Feeling unusually tired.
  • **Black or Tarry Stools:** Indicating bleeding in the upper digestive tract.

It's important to note that these symptoms can also be caused by other, less serious conditions. However, if you experience any of these, especially difficulty swallowing, it's crucial to consult a doctor.

Risk Factors for Oesophageal Cancer

Several factors can increase the risk of developing oesophageal cancer:

  • **Smoking:** Tobacco use in any form (cigarettes, cigars, chewing tobacco) significantly increases the risk of squamous cell carcinoma.
  • **Excessive Alcohol Consumption:** Especially linked to squamous cell carcinoma. The risk is higher when combined with smoking.
  • **Chronic Acid Reflux (GERD):** Long-term, frequent heartburn can damage the lining of the oesophagus, increasing the risk of adenocarcinoma.
  • **Barrett's Oesophagus:** A condition where the lining of the lower oesophagus changes to resemble the lining of the intestine, often due to chronic acid reflux. It is a significant risk factor for adenocarcinoma.
  • **Obesity:** Being overweight or obese increases the risk of adenocarcinoma.
  • **Age:** The risk of oesophageal cancer increases with age, with most cases diagnosed in people over 55.
  • **Gender:** Men are more likely to develop oesophageal cancer than women.
  • **Diet:** A diet low in fruits and vegetables and high in processed meats may increase the risk.
  • **Hot Liquids:** Regularly drinking very hot beverages may increase the risk of squamous cell carcinoma.
  • **Achalasia:** A condition where the lower oesophageal sphincter doesn't relax properly, making it difficult for food to pass into the stomach.
  • **Previous Radiation Therapy to the Chest or Upper Abdomen:** Can increase the risk.
  • **Tylosis:** A rare inherited condition that causes thickening of the skin on the palms and soles and is associated with a high risk of squamous cell carcinoma.

Diagnosis of Oesophageal Cancer

If oesophageal cancer is suspected, several tests may be used to diagnose it:

  • **Upper Endoscopy (Oesophagogastroduodenoscopy - EGD):** A thin, flexible tube with a camera attached is inserted down the throat into the oesophagus, stomach, and duodenum. This allows the doctor to visualize the lining of the oesophagus and look for abnormalities.
  • **Biopsy:** During an endoscopy, tissue samples (biopsies) can be taken from any suspicious areas and examined under a microscope for cancer cells.
  • **Barium Swallow:** The patient drinks a liquid containing barium, which coats the lining of the oesophagus. X-rays are then taken to look for any abnormalities in the shape or function of the oesophagus.
  • **CT Scan (Computed Tomography):** Provides detailed cross-sectional images of the chest and abdomen to assess the extent of the cancer and whether it has spread to nearby lymph nodes or other organs.
  • **PET Scan (Positron Emission Tomography):** Uses a radioactive tracer to identify areas of increased metabolic activity, which can indicate cancer. It can help detect the spread of cancer.
  • **Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS):** An endoscope with an ultrasound probe is inserted into the oesophagus to obtain detailed images of the oesophageal wall and nearby structures, including lymph nodes. This helps determine the depth of the tumour and local spread.
  • **Bronchoscopy:** If the tumour is near the top of the oesophagus, a bronchoscope (similar to an endoscope) may be used to examine the airways to ensure the cancer has not spread there.
  • **Laparoscopy or Thoracoscopy:** Surgical procedures to examine the abdominal or chest cavity for signs of cancer spread. Biopsies can also be taken during these procedures.

Once cancer is diagnosed, staging is done to determine the extent of the cancer, which helps guide treatment decisions.

Treatment of Oesophageal Cancer

Treatment for oesophageal cancer depends on several factors, including the stage of the cancer, the patient's overall health, and their preferences. Treatment options may include:

  • **Surgery:** Often the main treatment for early-stage oesophageal cancer. It may involve removing part or all of the oesophagus (oesophagectomy) and sometimes nearby lymph nodes. The stomach is then usually pulled up to connect to the remaining part of the oesophagus.
  • **Chemotherapy:** Uses drugs to kill cancer cells. It can be given before surgery (neoadjuvant) to shrink the tumour, after surgery (adjuvant) to kill any remaining cancer cells, or as the primary treatment for advanced cancer.
  • **Radiation Therapy:** Uses high-energy beams to kill cancer cells. It can be used before or after surgery, or as the main treatment if surgery is not an option. It can also help relieve symptoms of advanced cancer.
  • **Chemoradiation:** A combination of chemotherapy and radiation therapy, often given before surgery or as the primary treatment.
  • **Targeted Therapy:** Drugs that target specific abnormalities in cancer cells. Examples include trastuzumab for adenocarcinomas that overexpress the HER2 protein.
  • **Immunotherapy:** Drugs that help the body's immune system fight cancer. They may be used for advanced oesophageal cancer.
  • **Endoscopic Therapies:** For very early-stage cancers or precancerous conditions like Barrett's oesophagus with high-grade dysplasia, endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR) or radiofrequency ablation (RFA) may be used to remove abnormal cells.
  • **Palliative Care:** Focuses on relieving symptoms and improving quality of life for patients with advanced cancer. This can include treatments to help with swallowing difficulties, pain management, and nutritional support.

Treatment plans are often multidisciplinary, involving surgeons, oncologists (medical, radiation), gastroenterologists, nutritionists, and other specialists.
