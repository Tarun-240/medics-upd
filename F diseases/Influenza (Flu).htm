Influenza (Flu)

About Influenza

Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses. It infects the nose, throat, and lungs. The flu can range from mild to severe, and in some cases, it can be fatal.

The flu is different from the common cold. The flu usually comes on suddenly, and its symptoms are more severe.

Symptoms of Influenza

Flu symptoms can vary, but often include:

  • Fever (usually high)
  • Cough
  • Sore throat
  • Runny or stuffy nose
  • Muscle aches
  • Headache
  • Fatigue (feeling very tired)
  • Chills
  • Sweats

Some people may also experience:

  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhea

Causes of Influenza

Influenza is caused by influenza viruses. There are several types of influenza viruses, but the main types that commonly infect humans are:

  • Influenza A viruses
  • Influenza B viruses

These viruses are constantly changing, which is why new flu vaccines are needed each year.

How Flu Spreads

Flu viruses spread mainly through droplets made when people with flu cough, sneeze, or talk. These droplets can land in the mouths or noses of people who are nearby or possibly be inhaled into the lungs.

Less often, a person might get the flu by touching a surface or object that has flu viruses on it and then touching their own mouth, nose, or eyes.

Risk Factors for Influenza

Anyone can get the flu, but certain factors can increase your risk of developing complications:

  • Age: Young children (especially those younger than 5 years), and adults 65 years and older are at higher risk.
  • Chronic medical conditions: Such as asthma, diabetes, heart disease, and kidney disease.
  • Weakened immune system: Due to conditions like HIV/AIDS or cancer treatment.
  • Pregnancy

Diagnosing Influenza

The flu is often diagnosed based on symptoms, especially during flu season. However, laboratory tests can confirm the diagnosis. These tests include:

  • Rapid influenza diagnostic tests (RIDTs): These tests can provide results within 15-30 minutes, but they are not always accurate.
  • Reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR): This test is more accurate than RIDTs and can identify specific flu virus types.

Treating Influenza

Most people with the flu have mild illness and recover on their own within a few days to two weeks. However, some people may develop complications that require medical treatment.

Treatment options include:

  • Antiviral drugs: Such as oseltamivir (Tamiflu) and zanamivir (Relenza), can shorten the duration of illness and prevent complications. They are most effective when started within 48 hours of symptom onset.
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) medications: Can help relieve symptoms. These include:
    • Pain relievers: Such as acetaminophen (Tylenol) or ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) for fever and muscle aches.
    • Decongestants: To relieve nasal congestion.
    • Cough medicine: To help with cough.
  • Rest: Getting plenty of rest helps your body recover.
  • Fluids: Drink plenty of fluids to prevent dehydration.

Complications of Influenza

Complications from influenza can be serious and may require hospitalization. They include:

  • Pneumonia: A lung infection that can be caused by the flu virus itself or by a secondary bacterial infection.
  • Bronchitis: Inflammation of the bronchial tubes.
  • Sinus infections
  • Ear infections
  • Myocarditis: Inflammation of the heart muscle.
  • Encephalitis: Inflammation of the brain.
  • Sepsis: A life-threatening condition caused by the body's response to an infection.
  • Worsening of chronic medical conditions: Such as asthma, heart disease, or diabetes.

Prevention of Influenza

The best way to prevent the flu is to get vaccinated each year. Other preventive measures include:

  • Get vaccinated: The flu vaccine is safe and effective and can significantly reduce your risk of getting the flu.
  • Practice good hygiene:
    • Wash your hands frequently with soap and water.
    • Use hand sanitizer if soap and water are not available.
    • Cover your mouth and nose when you cough or sneeze.
  • Avoid close contact with sick people: Stay away from people who have flu-like symptoms.
  • Stay home when you are sick: If you have the flu, stay home to prevent spreading it to others.

Treating Influenza

Treatment focuses on relieving symptoms and preventing complications.

  • Antiviral drugs (within 48 hours of symptoms)
  • Over-the-counter medications
