Febrile Seizures

About Febrile Seizures

Febrile seizures are seizures that occur in young children, typically between the ages of 6 months and 5 years, and are triggered by a fever. These seizures are usually associated with a rapid increase in body temperature, often due to an infection.

While febrile seizures can be frightening for parents, they are usually harmless and do not cause long-term neurological damage.

Types of Febrile Seizures

There are two main types of febrile seizures:

  • Simple febrile seizures: These are the most common type. They typically:
    • Last less than 15 minutes
    • Are generalized, meaning they involve the whole body
    • Occur only once during an illness
  • Complex febrile seizures: These are less common and may:
    • Last longer than 15 minutes
    • Are focal, meaning they involve only one part of the body
    • Occur more than once within 24 hours

Symptoms of Febrile Seizures

During a febrile seizure, a child may experience:

  • Loss of consciousness
  • Stiffening of the body
  • Jerking or twitching movements of the arms and legs
  • Eye rolling
  • Drooling
  • Breathing changes
  • Loss of bowel or bladder control (less common)

After the seizure, the child may be sleepy, confused, or irritable for a short period.

Causes of Febrile Seizures

Febrile seizures are caused by a fever, which is most often due to an infection. Common infections that can trigger febrile seizures include:

  • Viral infections: Such as those that cause colds, flu, or roseola.
  • Ear infections (otitis media)
  • Tonsillitis
  • Respiratory infections
  • Urinary tract infections (UTIs)

The exact mechanism of how fever triggers a seizure is not fully understood, but it is thought to be related to the way a child's brain responds to a rapid change in temperature.

Risk Factors for Febrile Seizures

Certain factors can increase the risk of a child having a febrile seizure:

  • Age: Most common between 6 months and 5 years.
  • Family history: Having a family member who has had febrile seizures.
  • High fever: The higher the fever, the greater the risk.
  • Rapid rise in temperature: A sudden increase in body temperature.

Diagnosing Febrile Seizures

Febrile seizures are usually diagnosed based on the child's medical history and the description of the seizure. A doctor will typically:

  • Ask about the child's symptoms and the seizure event.
  • Perform a physical examination.
  • Check the child's temperature.
  • Look for the source of the infection.

In most cases, no further testing is needed. However, the doctor may recommend tests to rule out other conditions or if the seizure is complex:

  • Blood tests: To check for infection or other abnormalities.
  • Urine test: To check for a urinary tract infection.
  • Electroencephalogram (EEG): To measure brain activity (usually not needed for simple febrile seizures).
  • Lumbar puncture (spinal tap): To check for meningitis if there are signs of a serious infection.

Treating Febrile Seizures

Most febrile seizures stop on their own within a few minutes and do not require specific treatment. The focus is on:

  • Ensuring the child's safety during the seizure:
    • Place the child on their side to prevent choking.
    • Clear the area of any hard or sharp objects.
    • Do not put anything in the child's mouth.
  • Managing the fever:
    • Give the child acetaminophen or ibuprofen to reduce fever.
    • Dress the child in light clothing.
    • Use a cool compress on the child's forehead.
  • Treating the underlying infection: If the seizure is caused by an infection, the doctor will treat the infection with antibiotics (for bacterial infections) or other appropriate medication.

In rare cases, if a seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes (status epilepticus), the child may need to be treated with anti-seizure medication, such as diazepam or lorazepam, to stop the seizure. This is usually given rectally or intravenously in a hospital setting.

Prognosis of Febrile Seizures

The prognosis for children who have febrile seizures is generally excellent.

  • Most children do not experience any long-term health problems or neurological damage.
  • The risk of developing epilepsy (a condition of recurrent seizures) after a simple febrile seizure is only slightly increased (about 1-2%).
  • The risk is slightly higher after complex febrile seizures, but still relatively low.

When to Seek Medical Attention

You should seek immediate medical attention if your child:

  • Has a seizure that lasts longer than 5 minutes.
  • Has difficulty breathing after the seizure.
  • Has repeated seizures.
  • Is very drowsy or difficult to wake up after the seizure.
  • Has a stiff neck, vomiting, or other signs of meningitis.
  • Is under 6 months old.

It's also important to follow up with your child's doctor after a febrile seizure to discuss any concerns and determine the need for further evaluation or testing.
