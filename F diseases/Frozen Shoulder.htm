Frozen Shoulder

About Frozen Shoulder

Frozen shoulder, also known as adhesive capsulitis, is a condition characterized by stiffness and pain in your shoulder joint. The condition occurs when the capsule surrounding your shoulder joint thickens and tightens, restricting its movement.

The condition typically develops slowly, progresses through three stages, and eventually resolves, though this process can take months or even years.

Symptoms of Frozen Shoulder

Frozen shoulder is characterized by three stages, each with its own set of symptoms:

  • Freezing stage:
    • Slow onset of pain
    • Increasing stiffness
    • Limited range of motion
    • Lasts 6 to 9 months
  • Frozen stage:
    • Pain may lessen, but stiffness remains
    • Significant restriction of movement
    • Difficulty with daily activities
    • Lasts 4 to 12 months
  • Thawing stage:
    • Gradual return of range of motion
    • Pain starts to decrease
    • Can take 6 months to 2 years

Causes of Frozen Shoulder

The exact cause of frozen shoulder is not fully understood, but several factors can increase your risk:

  • Age: People over 40 are more likely to develop frozen shoulder.
  • Sex: Women are more prone to the condition than men.
  • Immobilization: Prolonged inactivity of the shoulder due to surgery, injury, or illness.
  • Diabetes: People with diabetes are at a higher risk.
  • Other medical conditions: Such as thyroid disorders, Parkinson's disease, and heart disease.

Diagnosing Frozen Shoulder

A frozen shoulder is typically diagnosed through a physical examination. Your doctor will:

  • Ask about your symptoms and medical history
  • Evaluate your shoulder's range of motion, both actively (by yourself) and passively (with the doctor's help)

Imaging tests, such as X-rays or MRIs, are not usually required to diagnose frozen shoulder, but they may be used to rule out other conditions like arthritis or a rotator cuff tear.

Treating Frozen Shoulder

Most frozen shoulder treatment aims to control pain and restore range of motion. Treatment options include:

  • Pain relievers: Over-the-counter medications like NSAIDs can help reduce pain and inflammation.
  • Corticosteroids: These can be injected into the shoulder joint to reduce pain and improve mobility, especially in the early stages.
  • Physical therapy: A physical therapist can teach you stretching exercises to help restore range of motion.
  • Hydrotherapy: Gentle exercises in warm water can help improve comfort and mobility.
  • Nerve blocks: Injections to numb specific nerves can reduce pain.
  • Shoulder manipulation: The doctor moves your shoulder joint under anesthesia to break up the tightness.
  • Surgery: Surgery is rarely needed, but may be an option if other treatments are not effective.

Prevention of Frozen Shoulder

While it's not always possible to prevent frozen shoulder, you can reduce your risk by:

  • Maintaining shoulder mobility: If you've had a shoulder injury or surgery, follow your doctor's instructions for exercises to maintain range of motion.
  • Managing underlying conditions: If you have diabetes or another medical condition that increases your risk, work with your doctor to manage it effectively.
