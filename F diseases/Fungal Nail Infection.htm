Fungal Nail Infection (Onychomycosis)

About Fungal Nail Infection

Fungal nail infection, also known as onychomycosis, is a common condition that affects the toenails or fingernails. It occurs when fungi infect the nail bed and nail plate, leading to changes in the nail's appearance.

The infection can cause the nail to become thick, discolored, brittle, and distorted. It can be difficult to treat and may take a long time to clear up.

Symptoms of Fungal Nail Infection

Fungal nail infections can manifest in several ways, including:

  • Thickening of the nail
  • Discoloration (white, yellow, brown)
  • Brittle, crumbly, or ragged nails
  • Distorted shape of the nail
  • Separation of the nail from the nail bed (onycholysis)
  • Pain or discomfort (in severe cases)

Causes of Fungal Nail Infection

Fungal nail infections are typically caused by:

  • Dermatophytes: The most common cause, these fungi also cause skin infections like athlete's foot and ringworm.
  • Yeasts: Such as Candida, which can also cause infections like thrush.
  • Molds: Various types of molds can infect nails, though this is less common.

These fungi thrive in warm, moist environments, making toenails more commonly affected than fingernails due to the conditions inside shoes.

Risk Factors for Fungal Nail Infection

Several factors can increase the risk of developing a fungal nail infection:

  • Older age: Nails become more brittle and dry with age, making them more susceptible to infection.
  • Reduced blood circulation: Decreased blood flow to the feet can make it harder for the body to fight off infection.
  • Nail injury: Damage to the nail can create an entry point for fungi.
  • Weakened immune system: Conditions like diabetes, HIV, or psoriasis can increase risk.
  • Athlete's foot: Having a fungal skin infection can increase the risk of nail infection.
  • Tight-fitting shoes: Can create a warm, moist environment that promotes fungal growth.
  • Excessive sweating: Heavy sweating can also increase the risk.
  • Certain medical conditions: Such as diabetes and peripheral vascular disease.

Diagnosing Fungal Nail Infection

Diagnosing a fungal nail infection usually involves:

  • Physical examination: The doctor will examine the affected nail(s).
  • Nail clippings or scrapings: A sample of the nail is taken and sent to a laboratory to identify the type of fungus causing the infection. This is important to guide treatment.

Treating Fungal Nail Infection

Fungal nail infections can be challenging to treat, and treatment depends on the severity of the infection and the type of fungus involved. Options include:

  • Oral antifungal medications:
    • Terbinafine: Often the first-line treatment, taken daily for several weeks.
    • Itraconazole: May be given in pulse therapy (cycles of medication) or daily.

    Oral medications are generally more effective than topical treatments but can have potential side effects, such as liver problems. Regular blood tests may be needed.

  • Topical antifungal medications:
    • Antifungal nail lacquers: Such as ciclopirox, applied directly to the nail.
    • Other topical solutions: May be less effective than oral medications and are often used for mild infections or in combination with oral treatment.
  • Nail removal: In severe cases, the infected nail may need to be removed surgically or with a chemical treatment to allow for direct application of medication to the nail bed.
  • Laser treatment: Newer laser therapies aim to kill the fungus, but their effectiveness is still being studied.

Preventing Fungal Nail Infection

Preventing fungal nail infections involves good nail hygiene and taking precautions to reduce exposure to fungi:

  • Practice good nail hygiene: Keep nails clean and dry, trim them straight across, and avoid injuring the skin around the nails.
  • Wear proper footwear: Choose shoes that fit well and allow your feet to breathe.
  • Avoid walking barefoot in public places: Wear sandals or shoes in locker rooms, showers, and pool areas.
  • Keep feet dry: Dry your feet thoroughly after bathing or swimming, especially between the toes.
  • Change socks regularly: Change socks daily or more often if you sweat heavily.
  • Use antifungal powders or sprays: These can help keep your feet dry and prevent fungal growth.
  • Don't share nail clippers or files: Sharing personal care items can spread fungal infections.
