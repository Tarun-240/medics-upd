Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS)

About Fetal Alcohol Syndrome

Fetal alcohol syndrome (FAS) is a condition that results from alcohol exposure during pregnancy. It can cause a range of physical, intellectual, and behavioral impairments. FAS is the most severe form of a spectrum of conditions known as fetal alcohol spectrum disorders (FASDs).

The effects of FAS are permanent, and there is no cure. The best way to prevent FAS is for women to avoid alcohol entirely during pregnancy.

Symptoms of Fetal Alcohol Syndrome

FAS can cause a wide range of symptoms, which can vary in severity. Key features of FAS include:

  • Distinctive facial features:
    • Small head circumference
    • Smooth philtrum (the ridge between the nose and upper lip)
    • Thin upper lip
    • Small eye openings
  • Growth problems:
    • Low birth weight
    • Slower than normal growth
  • Brain damage:
    • Intellectual disability
    • Learning disabilities
    • Poor memory
    • Difficulty with problem-solving
    • Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)
    • Poor judgment
    • Seizures
  • Other problems:
    • Heart defects
    • Kidney problems
    • Skeletal abnormalities
    • Hearing problems
    • Vision problems

Causes of Fetal Alcohol Syndrome

FAS is caused by prenatal alcohol exposure. When a pregnant woman drinks alcohol, it crosses the placenta and reaches the developing fetus. Alcohol interferes with the fetus's development, causing damage that can lead to the characteristic features and problems associated with FAS.

The risk of FAS increases with the amount of alcohol consumed during pregnancy. However, there is no known safe amount of alcohol during pregnancy.

Diagnosing Fetal Alcohol Syndrome

Diagnosing FAS can be challenging, as there is no single test. Diagnosis is based on a combination of:

  • Prenatal alcohol exposure: Confirmed or presumed history of maternal alcohol consumption during pregnancy.
  • Specific facial features: The presence of the distinctive facial features described above.
  • Growth problems: Evidence of growth deficiency.
  • Brain damage: Evidence of central nervous system abnormalities.

A medical professional will evaluate the child's physical, neurological, and developmental status.

Treating Fetal Alcohol Syndrome

There is no cure for FAS, and the damage caused by prenatal alcohol exposure is irreversible. However, early diagnosis and intervention can help reduce some of the secondary disabilities and improve the child's quality of life.

Treatment focuses on managing the symptoms and providing support to the individual and their family. Treatment may include:

  • Medical care: To address specific health problems, such as heart defects or kidney problems.
  • Educational interventions: To provide support for learning disabilities.
  • Behavioral therapy: To help with behavioral problems, such as ADHD.
  • Speech therapy: To improve communication skills.
  • Occupational therapy: To help with daily living skills.
  • Medications: To manage specific symptoms, such as seizures or hyperactivity.
  • Parent training and support: To help parents understand and manage the challenges of raising a child with FAS.

Prevention of Fetal Alcohol Syndrome

FAS is completely preventable. The best way to prevent FAS is for women to:

  • Avoid alcohol entirely during pregnancy.
  • If a woman is planning to become pregnant, she should stop drinking alcohol.
  • Women who think they may be pregnant should stop drinking alcohol until they can confirm their pregnancy with a healthcare provider.

Healthcare providers should counsel women of childbearing age about the risks of alcohol consumption during pregnancy and advise them to abstain from alcohol.
