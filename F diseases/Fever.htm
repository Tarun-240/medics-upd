Fever

About Fever

Fever is a temporary increase in your body temperature. It's often a sign of an underlying illness, most commonly an infection. A fever indicates that your body is fighting off a disease or infection.

What is a Fever?

Body temperature normally varies slightly throughout the day. The average normal body temperature is around 98.6°F (37°C), but it can range from 98°F (36.1°C) to 100°F (37.8°C).

A fever is generally defined as a temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher when measured rectally, or 100°F (37.8°C) or higher when measured orally.

Symptoms of Fever

A fever itself is a symptom. However, it is often accompanied by other symptoms, depending on the underlying cause. Common symptoms that may occur along with a fever include:

  • Sweating
  • Chills and shivering
  • Headache
  • Muscle aches
  • Loss of appetite
  • Dehydration
  • General weakness

Causes of Fever

Fever is most often caused by an infection. Some of the common causes include:

  • Viral infections: Such as the common cold, influenza (flu), and other respiratory illnesses.
  • Bacterial infections: Such as strep throat, urinary tract infections, and pneumonia.
  • Childhood illnesses: Such as chickenpox and measles.
  • COVID-19

However, fever can also be caused by other conditions, including:

  • Inflammatory conditions: Such as rheumatoid arthritis or lupus.
  • Medications: Certain drugs, such as antibiotics, can cause fever as a side effect.
  • Vaccinations: Fever can sometimes occur after vaccinations.
  • Heat exhaustion or heatstroke: Prolonged exposure to high temperatures can cause the body to overheat.
  • Cancer

Diagnosing Fever

Fever is easily diagnosed by taking a temperature using a thermometer. However, finding the cause of the fever may require further investigation. A doctor may:

  • Review your medical history and symptoms
  • Perform a physical examination
  • Order blood tests: To check for infection or other abnormalities.
  • Order a urine test: To check for a urinary tract infection.
  • Order imaging tests: Such as a chest X-ray, if a lung infection is suspected.

Treating Fever

In most cases, a low-grade fever is not dangerous and does not require specific treatment. The focus should be on treating the underlying cause.

To help relieve discomfort from a fever, you can:

  • Take over-the-counter fever reducers: Such as acetaminophen (Tylenol) or ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin). Follow the dosage instructions on the label.
  • Drink plenty of fluids: To prevent dehydration.
  • Rest: To help your body fight off the illness.
  • Keep cool: Dress in light clothing and keep the room temperature comfortable.

When to seek medical attention:

  • High fever: Seek medical attention if you have a temperature of 103°F (39.4°C) or higher.
  • Fever with other severe symptoms: Such as severe headache, stiff neck, shortness of breath, confusion, or seizures.
  • Fever in infants: Seek immediate medical attention for infants under 3 months old with any fever. For older infants and children, consult your doctor if the fever is high or accompanied by other concerning symptoms.
  • Persistent fever: If your fever lasts for more than a few days.

Complications of Fever

Most fevers do not cause complications. However, a high fever, especially in young children, can sometimes lead to:

  • Febrile seizures: Seizures triggered by fever, most common in children between 6 months and 5 years old.
  • Dehydration
