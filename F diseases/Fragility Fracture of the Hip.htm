Fragility Fracture of the Hip

About Fragility Fractures of the Hip

A fragility fracture of the hip is a break in the upper part of the femur (thighbone) that occurs as a result of a fall or minor trauma in individuals with weakened bones, most often due to osteoporosis.

These fractures are a major cause of disability and mortality, particularly in older adults. The hip joint is a ball-and-socket joint where the femur connects to the pelvis.

Types of Hip Fractures

Hip fractures are generally classified into two main types, depending on the location of the break:

  • Femoral neck fractures: These occur in the narrow section of the femur just below the ball (femoral head).
  • Intertrochanteric fractures: These occur in the region of the femur below the femoral neck, where there are bony prominences called trochanters.

Symptoms of a Hip Fracture

Symptoms of a hip fracture typically include:

  • Severe pain in the hip or groin area
  • Inability to move the leg
  • Inability to stand or walk
  • Leg appears shortened or rotated outward
  • Significant bruising

In some cases, especially in older adults, the fall that caused the fracture may be minor, and the person may not be able to clearly describe the fall.

Causes and Risk Factors

The primary cause of fragility fractures of the hip is weakened bones, most commonly due to:

  • Osteoporosis: A disease that causes bones to become thin, weak, and more prone to fractures.

Other risk factors include:

  • Older age: Bone density decreases with age.
  • Female sex: Women are more likely to develop osteoporosis than men.
  • White race: White individuals have a higher risk of osteoporosis.
  • Family history of osteoporosis or hip fracture
  • Low body weight
  • Smoking and excessive alcohol consumption
  • Poor nutrition (low calcium and vitamin D intake)
  • Certain medical conditions: Such as rheumatoid arthritis, hyperthyroidism, and celiac disease.
  • Certain medications: Such as corticosteroids.
  • Impaired mobility or balance

Diagnosing a Hip Fracture

A hip fracture is usually diagnosed with:

  • Physical examination: The doctor will assess the patient's symptoms and examine the injured leg.
  • X-ray: This is the primary imaging test used to confirm a hip fracture and determine its type and location.
  • Other imaging tests: In some cases, if the X-ray is unclear, an MRI or CT scan may be used to get a more detailed view of the hip.

Treating a Hip Fracture

Treatment for a hip fracture typically involves:

  • Surgery: Surgery is almost always required to repair a hip fracture. The type of surgery depends on the location and severity of the fracture, as well as the patient's age and overall health. Surgical options include:
    • Internal fixation: The broken bones are stabilized with screws, rods, or plates. This is often used for intertrochanteric fractures.
    • Hip replacement: The damaged parts of the hip joint are replaced with artificial implants. This may be a partial or total hip replacement, and is often used for femoral neck fractures, especially in older patients.
  • Pain management: Pain medication is given before and after surgery.
  • Physical therapy: Rehabilitation is started as soon as possible after surgery to help the patient regain strength, mobility, and independence.
  • Blood clot prevention: Medications (anticoagulants) may be given to reduce the risk of blood clots.
  • Antibiotics: To prevent infection.

Complications of Hip Fractures

Hip fractures can lead to serious complications, including:

  • Pneumonia
  • Urinary tract infections
  • Blood clots in the legs or lungs
  • Pressure sores
  • Muscle weakness and loss of mobility
  • Increased risk of future falls
  • Increased mortality

Preventing Hip Fractures

Preventing hip fractures focuses on reducing the risk of falls and improving bone strength:

  • Treating osteoporosis: Medications can help increase bone density and reduce fracture risk.
  • Calcium and vitamin D intake: Adequate intake is essential for bone health.
  • Regular exercise: Weight-bearing and muscle-strengthening exercises can improve balance and bone strength.
  • Fall prevention measures:
    • Remove hazards in the home, such as loose rugs and clutter.
    • Install grab bars in bathrooms.
    • Improve lighting.
    • Use assistive devices, such as canes or walkers, if needed.
  • Regular eye exams: To ensure good vision and reduce the risk of falls.
  • Review medications: Some medications can increase the risk of falls.
