Food Allergy

About Food Allergy

A food allergy is an immune system reaction that occurs when a person eats a certain food. The body mistakenly identifies a harmless food protein as a threat and produces antibodies to fight it. When the person eats that food again, the antibodies release chemicals, causing allergy symptoms.

Food allergies can range from mild to severe, and some reactions can be life-threatening (anaphylaxis). It's important to distinguish between a food allergy and a food intolerance. Food intolerance does not involve the immune system and is generally less severe.

Symptoms of Food Allergy

Food allergy symptoms can appear within a few minutes to a few hours after eating the offending food. Symptoms can vary but may include:

  • Skin reactions:
    • Hives (itchy, raised bumps)
    • Eczema (red, itchy skin)
    • Swelling (especially of the face, lips, tongue, or throat)
    • Itching
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms:
    • Nausea
    • Vomiting
    • Abdominal cramps
    • Diarrhea
  • Respiratory symptoms:
    • Sneezing
    • Runny nose
    • Wheezing
    • Coughing
    • Shortness of breath
    • Chest tightness
  • Cardiovascular symptoms:
    • Dizziness
    • Lightheadedness
    • Fainting
  • Anaphylaxis: A severe, potentially life-threatening reaction that can involve multiple body systems. Symptoms include:
    • Difficulty breathing
    • Swelling of the throat
    • Rapid heartbeat
    • Hives
    • Low blood pressure
    • Loss of consciousness

Common Food Allergens

While any food can cause an allergic reaction, eight foods account for most food allergies:

  • Milk
  • Eggs
  • Peanuts
  • Tree nuts (e.g., almonds, walnuts, pecans)
  • Soy
  • Wheat
  • Fish
  • Shellfish (e.g., crab, shrimp, lobster)

Other foods that can cause allergies include sesame, mustard, and certain fruits and vegetables.

Diagnosing Food Allergy

Diagnosing a food allergy typically involves a combination of:

  • Medical history: Your doctor will ask about your symptoms and when they occur.
  • Physical examination: Your doctor will look for signs of an allergic reaction.
  • Elimination diet: You may be asked to eliminate certain foods from your diet and then reintroduce them one at a time to see if they cause a reaction.
  • Skin prick test: A small amount of the suspected allergen is placed on your skin, and the skin is pricked. A raised bump indicates a possible allergy.
  • Blood test: Measures the level of specific IgE antibodies in your blood. High levels suggest an allergy.
  • Oral food challenge: Considered the gold standard for diagnosis. You eat small amounts of the suspected allergen under medical supervision and are monitored for a reaction.

Treating Food Allergy

The best way to manage a food allergy is to avoid the allergenic food entirely.

Other treatment options include:

  • Epinephrine: For severe reactions (anaphylaxis), an epinephrine auto-injector (e.g., EpiPen) is crucial. It should be administered immediately.
  • Antihistamines: Can help relieve mild symptoms like itching and hives.
  • Corticosteroids: May be used to treat severe or persistent symptoms.
  • Oral immunotherapy (OIT): A newer treatment that involves gradually increasing exposure to the allergen under medical supervision. It aims to desensitize the person to the allergen.

Prevention of Food Allergy Reactions

The most important aspect of preventing food allergy reactions is strict avoidance of the allergenic food. This involves:

  • Reading food labels carefully: Allergens must be listed on packaged foods.
  • Being cautious when eating out: Inform restaurant staff about your allergy and ask about ingredients.
  • Educating yourself and others: Make sure family members, friends, and caregivers know about the allergy and how to respond to a reaction.
  • Carrying an epinephrine auto-injector: If you have a severe allergy, always carry an epinephrine auto-injector and know how to use it.

Living with a Food Allergy

Living with a food allergy requires careful management and vigilance. Here are some tips:

  • Work with an allergist: An allergist can provide guidance on managing your allergy and developing an emergency plan.
  • Create an action plan: This plan should outline what to do in case of a reaction.
  • Wear a medical alert bracelet: This can inform others about your allergy in case of an emergency.
  • Join a support group: Connecting with others who have food allergies can provide emotional support and practical advice.
