About RSV

Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) is a common respiratory virus that infects the lungs and breathing passages. Most people recover within a week or two, but RSV can be serious, especially for infants, young children, older adults, and people with weakened immune systems. It is a major cause of bronchiolitis and pneumonia in young children.

Symptoms

RSV symptoms can vary, depending on age and the severity of the infection. In adults and older children, RSV usually causes mild, cold-like symptoms, including:

  • Runny nose
  • Congestion
  • Cough
  • Fever
  • Sore throat

In infants and young children, RSV can cause more severe symptoms, such as:

  • Wheezing
  • Rapid breathing
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Coughing
  • Poor feeding
  • Irritability

Causes

RSV infection is caused by the respiratory syncytial virus. It spreads easily through:

  • Airborne droplets: When an infected person coughs or sneezes.
  • Direct contact: Touching an infected person.
  • Contaminated surfaces: Touching surfaces with the virus and then touching your face.

RSV can survive for many hours on hard surfaces.

Diagnosing

Diagnosing RSV infection is usually based on symptoms, especially in infants and young children during RSV season. However, laboratory tests can confirm the diagnosis:

  • Nasal swab or wash: To detect the virus.
  • Blood test: To check for complications.
  • Chest X-ray: To check for pneumonia.

Prevention

There are several ways to help prevent the spread of RSV:

  • Frequent hand washing: With soap and water for at least 20 seconds.
  • Avoiding close contact: With sick people.
  • Covering coughs and sneezes: With a tissue or your elbow.
  • Cleaning and disinfecting surfaces: That are frequently touched.
  • Staying home when sick.
  • Vaccination: For certain high-risk groups.

Treating

Most RSV infections clear up on their own in 1 to 2 weeks. Treatment focuses on relieving symptoms:

  • For mild cases:
    • Rest
    • Fluids
    • Fever reducers
  • For severe cases: (especially in infants)
    • Hospitalization
    • Oxygen therapy
    • Intravenous (IV) fluids
    • Mechanical ventilation (in some cases)
