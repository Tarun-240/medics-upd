About Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic inflammatory autoimmune disease that primarily affects the joints. It causes pain, swelling, stiffness, and loss of function in the joints. RA can also affect other parts of the body, including the skin, eyes, lungs, heart, and blood vessels. It is a systemic disease, meaning it can affect the whole body.

Symptoms

Symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis can vary from person to person and may come and go. Common symptoms include:

  • Joint pain, swelling, and stiffness
  • Warmth and tenderness in the joints
  • Symmetrical joint involvement (affecting the same joints on both sides of the body)
  • Fatigue
  • Fever
  • Loss of appetite
  • Weakness
  • Deformity of the joints over time

Other symptoms may include:

  • Rheumatoid nodules (bumps under the skin)
  • Dry eyes and mouth (Sj√∂gren's syndrome)
  • Inflammation of the lining of the lungs (pleurisy)
  • Inflammation of the sac around the heart (pericarditis)
  • Anemia

Causes

Rheumatoid arthritis is an autoimmune disease. This means that the body's immune system, which normally attacks foreign substances like bacteria and viruses, mistakenly attacks the lining of the joints (synovium). The exact cause of this abnormal immune response is unknown, but several factors are believed to play a role:

  • Genetic predisposition: Certain genes, particularly HLA (human leukocyte antigen) genes, increase the risk of developing RA.
  • Environmental factors: Exposure to certain environmental factors, such as smoking, may trigger the disease in people who are genetically susceptible.
  • Infections: Some infections may play a role in triggering RA, but no single infectious agent has been identified.
  • Hormones: Hormones may play a role, as RA is more common in women.

Diagnosing

Diagnosing rheumatoid arthritis can be challenging, as there is no single test that can definitively confirm the disease in its early stages. Diagnosis is based on a combination of:

  • Medical history
  • Physical examination
  • Laboratory tests:
    • Rheumatoid factor (RF)
    • Anti-citrullinated peptide antibodies (ACPA)
    • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
    • C-reactive protein (CRP)
    • Complete blood count (CBC)
  • Imaging tests:
    • X-rays
    • Ultrasound
    • MRI (magnetic resonance imaging)

Doctors use the American College of Rheumatology/European League Against Rheumatism (ACR/EULAR) classification criteria to help diagnose RA.

Prevention

There is no known way to prevent rheumatoid arthritis. However, some factors can help reduce the risk or delay its onset:

  • Not smoking
  • Maintaining a healthy weight
  • Eating a healthy diet

Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to help prevent joint damage and long-term complications.

Treating

There is no cure for rheumatoid arthritis, but treatment aims to:

  • Relieve pain
  • Reduce inflammation
  • Slow down joint damage
  • Improve function and well-being

Treatment options include:

  • Medications:
    • Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
    • Corticosteroids
    • Disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs)
    • Biologic agents
  • Physical therapy
  • Occupational therapy
  • Surgery (in some cases, to repair or replace damaged joints)
  • Lifestyle modifications
