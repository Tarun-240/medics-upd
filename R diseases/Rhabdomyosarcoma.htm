About Rhabdomyosarcoma

Rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS) is a rare type of cancer that forms in soft tissue, specifically skeletal muscle tissue (the muscle that you control). RMS can occur anywhere in the body, but it most commonly starts in the:

  • Head and neck
  • Arms or legs
  • Urinary or reproductive organs

RMS can affect people of any age, but it is most common in children and adolescents.

Symptoms

Symptoms of rhabdomyosarcoma vary depending on where the cancer starts. Some general symptoms may include:

  • A lump or swelling that doesn't go away
  • Pain or soreness in the affected area

Other symptoms, depending on the tumor location, may include:

  • Head and neck: Headaches, sinus congestion, nosebleeds, or changes in vision.
  • Arms or legs: Swelling, pain, or difficulty moving the limb.
  • Urinary or reproductive organs: Difficulty urinating, blood in the urine, or bleeding from the vagina or rectum.

Causes

The exact causes of rhabdomyosarcoma are not fully understood. It occurs when muscle cells don't develop normally. Normally, muscle cells mature and stop growing, but in RMS, they continue to divide and form a tumor. While the cause is often unknown, some factors may play a role:

  • Genetic mutations: Changes in the DNA within cells. Certain genetic syndromes can increase the risk.
  • Family history: Having a family history of certain cancers may increase the risk.

Diagnosing

Diagnosing rhabdomyosarcoma may involve:

  • Physical exam
  • Imaging tests:
    • X-rays
    • Ultrasound
    • CT scan (computed tomography)
    • MRI (magnetic resonance imaging)
    • PET scan (positron emission tomography)
    • Bone scan
  • Biopsy: Removing tissue for examination.
  • Bone marrow aspiration and biopsy: To check if the cancer has spread.

Prevention

Most cases of rhabdomyosarcoma cannot be prevented, as the exact causes are not well-understood. If a child has a known genetic syndrome that increases the risk, regular checkups and screenings may be recommended.

Treating

Treatment for rhabdomyosarcoma depends on several factors, including the type of RMS, where it started, the size of the tumor, and whether it has spread. Treatment options may include:

  • Surgery: To remove the tumor.
  • Chemotherapy: To kill cancer cells.
  • Radiation therapy: To destroy cancer cells.
  • Targeted therapy: Drugs that attack specific cancer cells.
  • Immunotherapy: To help the body's immune system fight cancer.
