About Recurrent Miscarriage

Recurrent miscarriage, also known as recurrent pregnancy loss (RPL), is defined as two or more consecutive miscarriages before 20 weeks of gestation. Miscarriage is the spontaneous loss of a pregnancy. Recurrent miscarriage is a complex issue, and identifying the underlying cause can be challenging but is important for future pregnancy planning.

Symptoms

The primary symptom of a miscarriage is vaginal bleeding. This bleeding can vary from light spotting to heavy bleeding with clots. Other symptoms include:

  • Cramping or pain in your abdomen or lower back
  • Passage of tissue or fluid from your vagina
  • A decrease in pregnancy symptoms, such as nausea or breast tenderness

It's important to note that experiencing some of these symptoms doesn't always mean you are having a miscarriage. However, if you experience any of these symptoms, it's crucial to contact your doctor or healthcare provider immediately.

Causes

The causes of recurrent miscarriage are varied, and in some cases, a specific cause cannot be identified. However, several factors are known to contribute:

  • Genetic factors: Chromosomal abnormalities in the embryo are a common cause, particularly in first-trimester losses.
  • Uterine abnormalities: Problems with the shape or structure of the uterus, such as a septate uterus, fibroids, or adhesions.
  • Hormonal imbalances: Conditions like polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) or thyroid problems.
  • Blood clotting disorders: Such as antiphospholipid syndrome (APS), which can cause blood clots in the placenta.
  • Immune system disorders: Problems with the body's immune system.
  • Lifestyle factors: Smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, and high caffeine intake.

Other potential factors being researched include:

  • Infections
  • Environmental factors

Diagnosing

Diagnosing recurrent miscarriage involves a thorough evaluation to identify potential underlying causes. This may include:

  • Medical history: Detailed history of previous pregnancies and miscarriages.
  • Physical exam: General health assessment.
  • Blood tests: To check hormone levels, clotting factors, and immune system function.
  • Ultrasound: To examine the uterus and ovaries.
  • Hysteroscopy: A procedure to examine the inside of the uterus.
  • Genetic testing: Of the parents or the tissue from a miscarriage (if available).
  • Karyotype testing: To check the chromosomes of both partners.

Prevention

While recurrent miscarriage cannot always be prevented, there are some things that may help improve the chances of a successful pregnancy:

  • Maintaining a healthy lifestyle: This includes a balanced diet, regular exercise, and stress management.
  • Managing underlying health conditions: Such as diabetes, thyroid disorders, or clotting disorders.
  • Avoiding smoking, alcohol, and excessive caffeine.
  • Considering genetic counseling: If chromosomal abnormalities are a concern.
  • Uterine surgery: In some cases, surgery to correct uterine abnormalities can be beneficial.

Treating

Treatment for recurrent miscarriage focuses on addressing the underlying cause, if one is identified. Treatment options may include:

  • Medications: Such as blood thinners for antiphospholipid syndrome or hormones for hormonal imbalances.
  • Surgery: To correct uterine abnormalities.
  • In vitro fertilization (IVF) with preimplantation genetic testing (PGT): To screen embryos for chromosomal abnormalities.
  • Immunotherapy: For certain immune-related issues.
  • Lifestyle changes: Maintaining a healthy lifestyle.
  • Supportive care: Counseling and emotional support.
