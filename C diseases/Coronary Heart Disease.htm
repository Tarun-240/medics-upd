Coronary Heart Disease

About Coronary Heart Disease

Coronary heart disease (CHD), also known as coronary artery disease (CAD), is a condition in which the heart's blood vessels become narrowed or blocked. This narrowing is caused by the buildup of plaque, which is made up of cholesterol, fatty substances, cellular waste products, calcium, and fibrin.

The coronary arteries supply the heart muscle with oxygen-rich blood. When plaque builds up, it can reduce or completely block blood flow to the heart, leading to chest pain (angina), heart attack, or even death.

Symptoms of Coronary Heart Disease

Symptoms of CHD can vary depending on the severity of the blockage. Some people may have no symptoms at all, especially in the early stages.

Common symptoms include:

  • Angina: Chest pain or discomfort, often described as a squeezing, pressure, tightness, or burning sensation. It is usually triggered by physical exertion or emotional stress and relieved by rest or medication.
  • Shortness of breath: May occur during exertion or at rest if the heart is weakened.
  • Fatigue: Feeling unusually tired.
  • Heart attack: Occurs when blood flow to the heart is completely blocked, causing severe chest pain, sweating, nausea, vomiting, and shortness of breath. A heart attack can lead to death.

It's important to note that women may experience different or less typical symptoms of a heart attack, such as nausea, vomiting, back or jaw pain, and shortness of breath, without significant chest pain.

Causes of Coronary Heart Disease

The primary cause of CHD is atherosclerosis, the buildup of plaque in the coronary arteries.

Factors that contribute to atherosclerosis include:

  • High cholesterol: High levels of LDL ("bad") cholesterol can lead to plaque formation.
  • High blood pressure: Increases the strain on the arteries and can damage them.
  • Smoking: Damages blood vessels and increases the risk of plaque buildup.
  • Insulin resistance/Diabetes: Increases the risk of high cholesterol and high blood pressure.
  • Inflammation: Chronic inflammation in the arteries can promote plaque development.

Risk Factors for Coronary Heart Disease

Several factors can increase your risk of developing CHD:

  • Age: The risk increases as you get older.
  • Sex: Men are generally at a higher risk than women, though the risk for women increases after menopause.
  • Family history: Having a close relative who had CHD at an early age increases your risk.
  • High cholesterol: Elevated total cholesterol and LDL cholesterol levels.
  • Low HDL cholesterol: Low levels of "good" cholesterol.
  • High blood pressure
  • Smoking
  • Diabetes
  • Obesity or overweight
  • Physical inactivity
  • Stress
  • Unhealthy diet: A diet high in saturated and trans fats, cholesterol, sodium, and sugar.

Diagnosing Coronary Heart Disease

CHD is diagnosed through a variety of tests:

  • Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG): Records the electrical activity of the heart and can detect signs of damage or abnormal heart rhythms.
  • Echocardiogram: Uses sound waves to create images of the heart, showing its structure and function.
  • Stress test: Monitors heart function during exercise to see if blood flow is reduced. This can be combined with an ECG (exercise stress test) or imaging (stress echocardiogram or nuclear stress test).
  • Coronary angiography: A special X-ray using dye to visualize the coronary arteries and identify any blockages.
  • Cardiac CT scan: Can detect calcium deposits in the coronary arteries, a sign of atherosclerosis. Sometimes CT angiography is used to visualize the arteries.

Treating Coronary Heart Disease

Treatment for CHD aims to relieve symptoms, reduce risk factors, slow or reverse plaque buildup, and prevent heart attacks and other complications.

Treatment options include:

  • Lifestyle changes:
    • Heart-healthy diet: Low in saturated and trans fats, cholesterol, sodium, and sugar, and rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains.
    • Regular exercise: At least 30 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise most days of the week.
    • Smoking cessation: Quitting smoking is crucial.
    • Weight management: Losing weight if overweight or obese.
    • Stress management: Techniques such as meditation, yoga, or deep breathing.
  • Medications:
    • Statins: To lower cholesterol levels.
    • Antiplatelet drugs: Such as aspirin or clopidogrel, to prevent blood clots.
    • Beta-blockers: To lower heart rate and blood pressure.
    • ACE inhibitors or ARBs: To lower blood pressure and protect the heart.
    • Nitroglycerin: To relieve angina.
  • Procedures to restore blood flow:
    • Angioplasty and stenting: A catheter with a balloon is inserted into a narrowed artery, and the balloon is inflated to widen the artery. A stent (a small mesh tube) is often placed to keep the artery open.
    • Coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG): A surgical procedure to bypass blocked arteries using a healthy blood vessel from another part of the body.
  • Cardiac rehabilitation: A program of exercise, education, and counseling to help people recover after a heart attack or heart surgery and manage their CHD.

Prevention of Coronary Heart Disease

Many of the same lifestyle changes used to treat CHD can also help prevent it.

Preventive measures include:

  • Maintain a healthy diet
  • Exercise regularly
  • Don't smoke
  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Manage stress
  • Control cholesterol and blood pressure
  • Control blood sugar if you have diabetes
  • Get regular checkups
