Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

About Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

Chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) is a type of cancer that starts in the blood-forming cells of the bone marrow. It is characterized by the overproduction of white blood cells.

CML is a relatively slow-progressing leukemia compared to acute leukemias. It has three phases: chronic, accelerated, and blastic (or blast crisis).

Phases of Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

  • Chronic phase: This is the earliest and usually the least aggressive phase. Many people are diagnosed in this phase and may have few or no symptoms.
  • Accelerated phase: The disease progresses, and the number of abnormal white blood cells increases. Symptoms may become more pronounced.
  • Blastic phase (Blast crisis): This is the most advanced and aggressive phase, where the disease transforms into an acute leukemia. There is a rapid increase in immature white blood cells (blasts).

Symptoms of Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

Many people with CML, especially in the early stages, may not have any symptoms. When symptoms do occur, they can be vague and may include:

  • Fatigue
  • Weakness
  • Fever
  • Night sweats
  • Weight loss
  • Bone pain
  • Enlarged spleen (splenomegaly), which can cause a feeling of fullness in the abdomen
  • Easy bleeding or bruising
  • Frequent infections

Causes of Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

CML is caused by a specific genetic mutation called the Philadelphia chromosome.

The Philadelphia chromosome is formed when a piece of chromosome 9 breaks off and attaches to chromosome 22. This creates an abnormal gene called BCR-ABL, which produces a protein that causes the bone marrow to make too many abnormal white blood cells.

The exact reason why this chromosomal translocation occurs is not known. It is generally not an inherited condition.

Risk Factors for Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

While the exact cause of the Philadelphia chromosome is unknown, certain factors can increase the risk of developing CML:

  • Older age: CML is more common in adults, with the average age of diagnosis around 64.
  • Exposure to high-dose radiation: Such as from radiation therapy.
  • Sex: Slightly more common in men than in women.

Diagnosing Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

CML is typically diagnosed through a combination of tests:

  • Blood tests:
    • Complete blood count (CBC): Shows an increased number of white blood cells.
  • Bone marrow aspiration and biopsy: A sample of bone marrow is taken and examined under a microscope to confirm the diagnosis and check for the Philadelphia chromosome.
  • Cytogenetic analysis: To identify the Philadelphia chromosome in blood or bone marrow cells.
  • Molecular tests: Such as PCR (polymerase chain reaction) to detect the BCR-ABL gene.
  • FISH (fluorescent in situ hybridization): Another test to detect the BCR-ABL gene.

Treating Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

Treatment for CML has been revolutionized by the development of targeted therapies.

The main treatment options include:

  • Targeted therapy:
    • Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs): These drugs, such as imatinib, dasatinib, nilotinib, bosutinib, and ponatinib, target the BCR-ABL protein and are highly effective in controlling CML. They are typically the first-line treatment.
  • Chemotherapy: May be used in the accelerated or blastic phases, or in combination with other treatments.
  • Stem cell transplantation (bone marrow transplantation): May be an option for people who do not respond to TKIs or who are in the accelerated or blastic phase.
  • Surgery: Splenectomy (removal of the spleen) may be needed in rare cases if the spleen is enlarged and causing problems.

Prognosis for Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

The prognosis for people with CML has improved significantly with the development of TKIs. Many people with CML can now live for many years with a good quality of life.

Factors that can affect prognosis include:

  • Phase of the disease at diagnosis
  • Response to treatment
  • Overall health

Prevention of Chronic Myeloid Leukemia

There is no known way to prevent CML, as the chromosomal translocation that causes it is generally not inherited and its cause is unknown.
