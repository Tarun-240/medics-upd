Cystitis

About Cystitis

Cystitis is an inflammation of the bladder. It's most often caused by a bacterial infection, but it can also occur as a result of other factors.

A urinary tract infection (UTI) is a general term that refers to an infection in any part of the urinary system, including the bladder, urethra, ureters, and kidneys. Cystitis is a specific type of UTI that affects the bladder.

Symptoms of Cystitis

Cystitis can cause a variety of uncomfortable symptoms, including:

  • Pain or burning sensation during urination (dysuria)
  • Frequent urination
  • Urgent need to urinate
  • Cloudy or strong-smelling urine
  • Blood in the urine (hematuria)
  • Lower abdominal pain or pressure
  • Low-grade fever

In children, symptoms of a UTI may be less specific and can include:

  • Fever
  • Irritability
  • Poor feeding
  • Vomiting
  • Bedwetting

Causes of Cystitis

The most common cause of cystitis is a bacterial infection. Bacteria, usually Escherichia coli (E. coli), enter the bladder through the urethra and multiply.

Other less common causes of cystitis include:

  • Interstitial cystitis: A chronic bladder condition with unknown cause, characterized by bladder pain and urinary symptoms.
  • Medications: Certain drugs can irritate the bladder lining.
  • Irritants: Products like feminine hygiene sprays, douches, and spermicidal jellies can irritate the bladder.
  • Radiation therapy: Radiation treatment to the pelvic area can damage the bladder.
  • Catheter use: Long-term use of a urinary catheter can increase the risk of bladder infection.
  • Complications of other conditions: Such as diabetes, kidney stones, or an enlarged prostate.

Risk Factors for Cystitis

Several factors can increase the risk of developing cystitis:

  • Female anatomy: Women have a shorter urethra than men, which makes it easier for bacteria to reach the bladder.
  • Sexual activity: Sexual intercourse can introduce bacteria into the urinary tract.
  • Menopause: A decline in estrogen levels after menopause can make the urinary tract more susceptible to infection.
  • Use of certain contraceptives: Diaphragms and spermicides can increase the risk.
  • Urinary tract blockages: Conditions like kidney stones or an enlarged prostate can trap urine in the bladder, increasing the risk of infection.
  • Suppressed immune system: A weakened immune system makes it easier for bacteria to cause an infection.
  • Catheter use

Diagnosing Cystitis

Diagnosing cystitis typically involves:

  • Medical history: Your doctor will ask about your symptoms and medical history.
  • Urinalysis: A urine sample is tested for bacteria, blood, and white blood cells.
  • Cystoscopy: A thin, flexible tube with a camera is inserted into the urethra to examine the inside of the bladder. This is usually done if you have recurrent infections or if your doctor suspects an underlying problem.
  • Imaging tests: In some cases, your doctor may recommend an X-ray or CT scan to look for abnormalities in the urinary tract.

Treating Cystitis

Treatment for cystitis depends on the underlying cause.

For bacterial cystitis, the most common treatment is:

  • Antibiotics: To kill the bacteria causing the infection. A short course of antibiotics is often effective for uncomplicated cystitis.

For interstitial cystitis, treatment focuses on relieving symptoms and may include:

  • Pain relievers: To help with bladder pain.
  • Bladder distention: Stretching the bladder with water.
  • Medications: Such as pentosan polysulfate sodium.
  • Nerve stimulation: To reduce urinary urgency and frequency.
  • Surgery: Rarely, surgery may be an option for severe cases.

General self-care measures that can help with cystitis symptoms:

  • Drink plenty of water: To help flush bacteria out of the bladder.
  • Avoid bladder irritants: Such as caffeine, alcohol, and acidic foods.
  • Use a heating pad: To relieve bladder pain.
  • Take over-the-counter pain relievers: Such as acetaminophen or ibuprofen.
  • Urinate frequently: And don't hold your urine for long periods.
  • Wipe from front to back: After using the toilet, to prevent bacteria from spreading.

Complications of Cystitis

With prompt treatment, bacterial cystitis rarely leads to complications. However, if left untreated, it can lead to:

  • Kidney infection (pyelonephritis): The infection can spread to the kidneys, which can cause serious illness.
  • Chronic bladder infections: Some people may experience recurrent bladder infections.
  • Blood in the urine (hematuria)

Prevention of Cystitis

You can take these steps to reduce your risk of cystitis:

  • Drink plenty of water
  • Urinate frequently and don't hold urine
  • Wipe from front to back
  • Avoid irritating feminine products
  • Take showers instead of baths
  • Wear cotton underwear
  • Urinate after sexual intercourse
  • If you experience recurrent UTIs, your doctor may recommend:
    • Low-dose antibiotics: To prevent recurrent infections.
    • Post-coital antibiotics: Taking a single dose of antibiotic after sexual intercourse.
    • Vaginal estrogen therapy: For postmenopausal women.
