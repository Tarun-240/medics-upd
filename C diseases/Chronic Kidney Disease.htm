Chronic Kidney Disease

About Chronic Kidney Disease

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is a progressive condition in which the kidneys gradually lose their ability to function properly. The kidneys filter waste and excess fluids from the blood, and when they are damaged, waste products can build up in the body, leading to various health problems.

CKD is a long-term condition that often progresses slowly over many years. In the early stages, there may be few or no symptoms.

Stages of Chronic Kidney Disease

CKD is classified into five stages based on the glomerular filtration rate (GFR), which measures how well the kidneys filter blood:

  • Stage 1: Kidney damage with normal or high GFR (≥90 mL/min/1.73 m2)
  • Stage 2: Kidney damage with mildly decreased GFR (60-89 mL/min/1.73 m2)
  • Stage 3: Moderately decreased GFR (30-59 mL/min/1.73 m2)
    • Stage 3a: GFR 45-59 mL/min/1.73 m2
    • Stage 3b: GFR 30-44 mL/min/1.73 m2
  • Stage 4: Severely decreased GFR (15-29 mL/min/1.73 m2)
  • Stage 5: End-stage renal disease (ESRD) (GFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m2)

Symptoms of Chronic Kidney Disease

In the early stages of CKD, there may be no symptoms. As the disease progresses, symptoms can include:

  • Fatigue
  • Swelling in the legs, ankles, or feet (edema)
  • Shortness of breath
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Loss of appetite
  • Changes in urination (increased or decreased frequency)
  • Itching
  • Muscle cramps
  • Chest pain
  • High blood pressure
  • Difficulty sleeping

In the later stages, complications can arise due to the buildup of waste products in the body.

Causes of Chronic Kidney Disease

The most common causes of CKD are:

  • Diabetes: High blood sugar levels can damage the kidneys over time.
  • High blood pressure: Can put extra strain on the kidneys and damage them.
  • Glomerulonephritis: Inflammation of the kidney's filtering units (glomeruli).
  • Polycystic kidney disease: An inherited condition that causes cysts to grow in the kidneys.
  • Urinary tract obstructions: Such as kidney stones or an enlarged prostate.

Other conditions that can contribute to CKD include:

  • Lupus
  • Sickle cell disease
  • Certain medications

Risk Factors for Chronic Kidney Disease

Several factors can increase the risk of developing CKD:

  • Diabetes
  • High blood pressure
  • Family history of kidney disease
  • Older age
  • African American, Hispanic, or Native American ethnicity

Diagnosing Chronic Kidney Disease

CKD is diagnosed through a combination of tests:

  • Blood tests:
    • Creatinine: A waste product that builds up in the blood when kidneys are not working well.
    • Blood urea nitrogen (BUN): Another waste product that increases with kidney damage.
    • GFR: Estimated from creatinine levels, indicates kidney function.
  • Urine tests:
    • Proteinuria: Protein in the urine, an early sign of kidney damage.
    • Albuminuria: Specifically measures albumin in the urine.
  • Imaging tests:
    • Ultrasound: To assess the size and structure of the kidneys.
    • CT scan or MRI: May be used in some cases to identify specific problems.
  • Kidney biopsy: A tissue sample from the kidney is examined under a microscope, but this is usually done to determine the cause of CKD, not to diagnose CKD itself.

Treating Chronic Kidney Disease

Treatment for CKD focuses on slowing the progression of the disease, managing symptoms, and reducing the risk of complications.

Treatment options include:

  • Lifestyle changes:
    • Dietary modifications: Limiting sodium, phosphorus, and potassium intake.
    • Fluid restriction: In later stages.
    • Regular exercise: As tolerated.
    • Smoking cessation: If applicable.
  • Medications:
    • ACE inhibitors or ARBs: To control blood pressure and protect kidney function.
    • Diuretics: To reduce fluid retention.
    • Phosphate binders: To control phosphorus levels.
    • Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs): To treat anemia.
    • Vitamin D supplements: To help with calcium absorption and bone health.
  • Dialysis: A procedure to remove waste products and excess fluid from the blood when the kidneys can no longer do so.
    • Hemodialysis
    • Peritoneal dialysis
  • Kidney transplant: Replacing the damaged kidney with a healthy one from a donor.

Complications of Chronic Kidney Disease

CKD can lead to various complications, including:

  • Anemia
  • Bone disease
  • Heart disease
  • High potassium levels (hyperkalemia)
  • Fluid buildup in the lungs (pulmonary edema)
  • Increased risk of infection
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Nerve damage (neuropathy)

Prevention of Chronic Kidney Disease

While not all cases of CKD are preventable, you can take steps to reduce your risk:

  • Manage diabetes: Control blood sugar levels.
  • Control blood pressure: Maintain healthy blood pressure.
  • Maintain a healthy weight.
  • Follow a healthy diet: Low in sodium and saturated fats.
  • Don't smoke.
  • Use medications as prescribed: Especially NSAIDs, sparingly.
  • Get regular checkups: To monitor kidney function, especially if you have risk factors.
