Cellulitis

About Cellulitis

Cellulitis is a bacterial skin infection that causes redness, swelling, pain, and warmth in the affected area. It occurs when bacteria, most commonly streptococcus and staphylococcus, enter the skin through a break, such as a cut, scratch, or insect bite.

Cellulitis can occur anywhere on the body, but it most often affects the lower legs. The infection can spread to deeper tissues and the bloodstream, which can be life-threatening if not treated promptly.

Symptoms of Cellulitis

Symptoms of cellulitis may include:

  • Redness: The affected area of skin becomes red and may spread.
  • Swelling: The skin may become swollen, tight, and shiny.
  • Pain and tenderness: The area is often painful to the touch.
  • Warmth: The affected skin feels warm.
  • Fever: You may develop a fever, especially if the infection is spreading.
  • Chills
  • Blisters or skin dimpling
  • Fatigue

If the infection spreads, symptoms may include:

  • Swollen lymph nodes
  • Red streaks spreading from the affected area
  • Increased pain and swelling

Causes of Cellulitis

Cellulitis is caused by bacteria entering the skin through a break. Common bacteria that cause cellulitis include:

  • Streptococcus (strep)
  • Staphylococcus (staph)

Breaks in the skin that can allow bacteria to enter include:

  • Cuts
  • Scratches
  • Insect bites
  • Surgical wounds
  • Ulcers
  • Athlete's foot
  • Dry, cracked skin

Risk Factors for Cellulitis

Several factors can increase your risk of developing cellulitis:

  • Previous history of cellulitis
  • Weakened immune system: Due to conditions like diabetes, HIV/AIDS, or certain medications.
  • Skin conditions: Such as eczema, psoriasis, or athlete's foot.
  • Chronic swelling (lymphedema): Often occurs after surgery or radiation therapy.
  • Obesity
  • Poor circulation
  • Intravenous drug use
  • Breaks in the skin

Diagnosing Cellulitis

Diagnosing cellulitis typically involves:

  • Physical examination: Your doctor will examine the affected skin and look for signs of infection.
  • Medical history: Your doctor will ask about your symptoms, any recent injuries, and any underlying medical conditions.
  • Blood tests: May be done to check for signs of infection, especially if the infection is severe or spreading.
  • Wound culture: If there is an open wound, a sample of the fluid may be taken to identify the bacteria causing the infection.
  • Imaging tests: In some cases, imaging tests like ultrasound, CT scan, or MRI may be used to rule out other conditions or check for complications.

Treating Cellulitis

Prompt treatment of cellulitis is important to prevent the infection from spreading. Treatment options include:

  • Antibiotics: Oral antibiotics are usually effective for mild cases. Severe cases may require intravenous (IV) antibiotics in a hospital.
  • Pain relief: Over-the-counter pain relievers, such as acetaminophen or ibuprofen, can help manage pain and fever.
  • Elevation: Elevating the affected limb can help reduce swelling.
  • Rest: Resting the affected area can promote healing.
  • Wound care: Keeping any open wounds clean and covered can help prevent further infection.
  • Surgery: In rare cases, surgery may be necessary to drain an abscess or remove infected tissue.

Complications of Cellulitis

If not treated promptly, cellulitis can lead to complications, including:

  • Spread of infection: The infection can spread to deeper tissues, such as muscles and bones (osteomyelitis).
  • Bloodstream infection (bacteremia): This can lead to sepsis, a life-threatening condition.
  • Lymphangitis: Inflammation of the lymph vessels.
  • Abscess formation: A collection of pus may form at the site of infection.
  • Necrotizing fasciitis: A rare but severe infection that destroys tissue under the skin.

Prevention of Cellulitis

While it may not always be possible to prevent cellulitis, you can take steps to reduce your risk:

  • Practice good skin hygiene: Keep your skin clean and moisturized.
  • Prevent skin injuries: Take precautions to avoid cuts, scrapes, and insect bites.
  • Proper wound care: Clean any skin injuries thoroughly and cover them with a bandage until healed.
  • Manage underlying conditions: Effectively manage conditions like diabetes and eczema.
  • Wear appropriate footwear: Protect your feet to prevent cuts and cracks.
