Congenital Heart Disease

About Congenital Heart Disease

Congenital heart disease (CHD) is a term used to describe heart defects that are present at birth. These defects can affect the heart's walls, valves, or blood vessels, and they disrupt the normal flow of blood through the heart.

CHD is one of the most common types of birth defects, and its severity can range from mild to life-threatening.

Types of Congenital Heart Disease

There are many different types of congenital heart defects. Some of the most common include:

  • Septal Defects
  • Obstructive Defects
  • Cyanotic Defects

Septal Defects

Septal defects are holes in the walls (septa) that separate the chambers of the heart.

  • Atrial septal defect (ASD): A hole in the wall between the two upper chambers of the heart (atria).
  • Ventricular septal defect (VSD): A hole in the wall between the two lower chambers of the heart (ventricles).
  • Atrioventricular canal defect (AVCD): A large hole in the center of the heart where the atria and ventricles meet.

Obstructive Defects

Obstructive defects occur when there is a narrowing (stenosis) in the heart valves or blood vessels, making it harder for blood to flow.

  • Coarctation of the aorta: Narrowing of the aorta, the large artery that carries blood from the heart to the body.
  • Pulmonary stenosis: Narrowing of the pulmonary valve, which controls blood flow from the heart to the lungs.
  • Aortic stenosis: Narrowing of the aortic valve, which controls blood flow from the heart to the body.

Cyanotic Defects

Cyanotic defects are complex heart defects that result in low oxygen levels in the blood, causing the baby to have a bluish tint to their skin (cyanosis).

  • Tetralogy of Fallot: A combination of four defects: VSD, pulmonary stenosis, overriding aorta, and right ventricular hypertrophy.
  • Transposition of the great arteries (TGA): The aorta and pulmonary artery are switched, so blood flow is not properly circulated.
  • Tricuspid atresia: Absence of the tricuspid valve, which controls blood flow from the right atrium to the right ventricle.
  • Hypoplastic left heart syndrome (HLHS): The left side of the heart is severely underdeveloped.

Causes of Congenital Heart Disease

The exact causes of most congenital heart defects are unknown. However, several factors can increase the risk:

  • Genetics: Some heart defects are caused by changes in genes or chromosomes.
  • Family history: Having a family history of CHD increases the risk.
  • Maternal health conditions: Certain conditions in the mother during pregnancy can increase the risk, including:
    • Diabetes
    • Rubella (German measles) infection
    • Lupus
  • Exposure to certain substances: Such as alcohol, tobacco, or certain medications during pregnancy.

Symptoms of Congenital Heart Disease

Symptoms of CHD can vary depending on the type and severity of the defect. Some defects may cause no symptoms, while others can cause life-threatening problems shortly after birth.

Possible symptoms include:

  • Cyanosis: Bluish tint to the skin, lips, or nails.
  • Rapid or labored breathing
  • Poor feeding or difficulty feeding
  • Failure to thrive (slow weight gain)
  • Swelling in the legs, abdomen, or around the eyes
  • Fatigue
  • Heart murmur: An abnormal sound heard during a heartbeat.

Diagnosing Congenital Heart Disease

CHD can be diagnosed before birth (prenatally) or after birth (postnatally).

Prenatal diagnosis may involve:

  • Fetal echocardiogram: An ultrasound of the fetal heart.

Postnatal diagnosis may involve:

  • Physical examination: Listening to the heart with a stethoscope.
  • Echocardiogram: An ultrasound of the heart.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG): Measures the electrical activity of the heart.
  • Chest X-ray: To see the size and shape of the heart and lungs.
  • Pulse oximetry: Measures the oxygen level in the blood.
  • Cardiac catheterization: A thin tube is inserted into a blood vessel and guided to the heart.

Treating Congenital Heart Disease

Treatment for CHD depends on the type and severity of the defect. Some defects may require no treatment, while others may require complex surgery or other interventions.

Treatment options include:

  • Medications: To help the heart work more efficiently, prevent blood clots, or control blood pressure.
  • Interventional procedures:
    • Cardiac catheterization: Can be used to repair some defects without surgery, such as closing holes with a device or widening narrowed vessels with a balloon.
  • Surgery: Many heart defects require surgery to repair the heart. This may involve:
    • Closing holes in the heart
    • Repairing or replacing heart valves
    • Reconstructing blood vessels
    • Heart transplantation: In the most severe cases.
  • Follow-up care: Lifelong monitoring and care are often needed, even after successful treatment.

Living with Congenital Heart Disease

With advances in diagnosis and treatment, many people with CHD can live long and healthy lives. However, they may require ongoing medical care and may face challenges such as:

  • Increased risk of heart problems
  • Need for additional surgeries or procedures
  • Developmental delays
  • Emotional and psychological challenges

Support and resources are available to help people with CHD and their families manage these challenges.
