Carcinoid Syndrome and Carcinoid Tumors

About Carcinoid Tumors

Carcinoid tumors are a type of slow-growing cancer that can occur in various parts of the body, most commonly in the digestive system (especially the small intestine), but also in the lungs and rarely other sites. They are a subtype of neuroendocrine tumors (NETs).

These tumors are characterized by their ability to secrete certain substances, such as serotonin, which can lead to a distinct set of symptoms known as carcinoid syndrome.

About Carcinoid Syndrome

Carcinoid syndrome is a group of symptoms that occur as a result of carcinoid tumors secreting hormones, most notably serotonin, into the bloodstream. Not all carcinoid tumors cause carcinoid syndrome; it typically occurs when the tumor has metastasized (spread) to the liver. The liver normally metabolizes these hormones, so when tumors are in the liver, the hormones can enter systemic circulation.

Symptoms of Carcinoid Syndrome

The symptoms of carcinoid syndrome can vary but commonly include:

  • Flushing of the skin: The face and upper body may become red and feel hot.
  • Diarrhea: Frequent, watery bowel movements.
  • Wheezing and shortness of breath: Due to bronchoconstriction.
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Abdominal pain and cramping
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Facial swelling
  • Skin lesions: Pellagra-like rash due to niacin deficiency (rare).
  • Heart valve problems: Long-term exposure to serotonin can damage heart valves.

Symptoms can be triggered by certain foods, alcohol, stress, or exercise.

Causes of Carcinoid Tumors

The exact causes of carcinoid tumors are not fully understood. Like other cancers, they arise from cells that grow abnormally.

Risk factors that may be associated with the development of carcinoid tumors include:

  • Age: They can occur at any age, but are more common in older adults.
  • Sex: Some types are more common in women.
  • Family history: Conditions like multiple endocrine neoplasia type 1 (MEN 1) can increase risk.
  • Smoking: Increases the risk of bronchial carcinoids.

Diagnosing Carcinoid Tumors and Syndrome

Diagnosing carcinoid tumors and carcinoid syndrome involves several steps:

  • Medical history and physical examination: The doctor will evaluate symptoms and perform a physical exam.
  • Blood tests:
    • 5-HIAA (5-hydroxyindoleacetic acid): A breakdown product of serotonin, elevated in carcinoid syndrome.
    • Chromogranin A: A general marker for neuroendocrine tumors.
  • Urine tests: To measure 5-HIAA levels.
  • Imaging tests:
    • CT scan: To locate the tumor and check for metastasis.
    • MRI: Provides detailed images of soft tissues.
    • Octreotide scan (somatostatin receptor scintigraphy): Helps locate neuroendocrine tumors.
    • PET scan: Can be useful in some cases.
  • Biopsy: A tissue sample is taken to confirm the diagnosis and determine the tumor's characteristics.
  • Echocardiogram: To assess heart valve function.

Treating Carcinoid Tumors

Treatment for carcinoid tumors depends on the location, size, stage, and whether the tumor is causing carcinoid syndrome.

Treatment options include:

  • Surgery: To remove the tumor, especially if it is localized.
  • Liver-directed therapies:
    • Embolization: To block blood supply to liver metastases.
    • Ablation: To destroy liver metastases with heat or cold.
  • Medications:
    • Somatostatin analogs (e.g., octreotide, lanreotide): To control hormone secretion and symptoms.
    • Telotristat ethyl: Specifically targets serotonin production.
    • Chemotherapy: For more advanced or aggressive tumors.
    • Targeted therapy (e.g., everolimus): To slow tumor growth.
  • Radiation therapy: To shrink tumors and relieve symptoms.
  • Liver transplantation: In rare cases of extensive liver metastases.

Treating Carcinoid Syndrome

Treating carcinoid syndrome primarily focuses on managing the symptoms, as the underlying tumor may not always be curable, especially if it has metastasized.

Treatment options include:

  • Somatostatin analogs (e.g., octreotide, lanreotide): These are the mainstay of treatment to control flushing and diarrhea.
  • Telotristat ethyl: To control diarrhea by reducing serotonin production.
  • Bronchodilators: To relieve wheezing.
  • Anti-diarrheal medications: To manage diarrhea.
  • Surgery: To remove or debulk tumors, which can sometimes alleviate symptoms.
  • Heart valve surgery: If heart valve damage is severe.

Prognosis

The prognosis for carcinoid tumors and carcinoid syndrome varies widely depending on the tumor's location, stage, and how early it is diagnosed. Localized tumors that can be completely removed with surgery have the best prognosis. Metastatic disease has a poorer prognosis, but treatment can often control symptoms and prolong survival.
