Chickenpox

About Chickenpox

Chickenpox is a highly contagious disease caused by the varicella-zoster virus. It causes a blister-like rash, itching, fatigue, and fever.

Most cases occur in children, but people of any age can get it. Once you've had chickenpox, you're usually immune for life. However, the virus can remain dormant in your body and may reactivate later in life as shingles.

Symptoms of Chickenpox

The classic symptom of chickenpox is a rash that develops in stages:

  • Papules: Raised, pink or red bumps.
  • Vesicles: Fluid-filled blisters.
  • Crusts: The blisters eventually scab over.

The rash usually appears first on the chest, back, and face, then spreads to the rest of the body. Other symptoms can appear 1 to 2 days before the rash:

  • Fever
  • Fatigue
  • Headache
  • Muscle aches
  • Loss of appetite

The illness typically lasts about 5 to 7 days.

Causes of Chickenpox

Chickenpox is caused by the varicella-zoster virus. It is highly contagious and spreads easily through:

  • Direct contact with the fluid from chickenpox blisters
  • Airborne transmission through coughing or sneezing

You can spread the virus to others for 1 to 2 days before the rash appears and until all the blisters have formed scabs.

Risk Factors for Chickenpox

The greatest risk factor for chickenpox is never having had the disease or the vaccine. Other risk factors include:

  • Being a young child (though unvaccinated individuals of any age are susceptible)
  • Close contact with an infected person

Diagnosing Chickenpox

Chickenpox is usually diagnosed based on the characteristic rash. Further tests are generally not needed. However, in complicated cases, a doctor may order:

  • Laboratory tests: To confirm the diagnosis, such as a viral culture or blood test.

Treating Chickenpox

Most healthy children with chickenpox need only supportive care. Treatment focuses on relieving symptoms and preventing complications.

Treatment options include:

  • Home care:
    • Calamine lotion: To relieve itching.
    • Cool compresses or baths: To soothe the skin.
    • Rest: To help the body recover.
    • Fluids: To prevent dehydration.
  • Medications:
    • Antiviral medications: Such as acyclovir, may be prescribed for people at high risk of complications (e.g., adolescents, adults, pregnant women, people with weakened immune systems). These medications can shorten the duration of the illness and reduce the risk of complications.
    • Antihistamines: To help with itching.
    • Acetaminophen: For fever and pain. (Aspirin should be avoided in children and teenagers with chickenpox, as it can lead to Reye's syndrome, a rare but serious condition.)

Complications of Chickenpox

Chickenpox is usually mild, but complications can occur, especially in:

  • Newborns
  • Adults
  • Pregnant women
  • People with weakened immune systems

Complications can include:

  • Bacterial infections of the skin: Such as impetigo or cellulitis.
  • Pneumonia: Infection of the lungs.
  • Encephalitis: Inflammation of the brain.
  • Cerebellar ataxia: Problems with coordination and balance.
  • Hepatitis: Inflammation of the liver.
  • Reye's syndrome: A rare but serious condition that can cause brain and liver damage (associated with aspirin use in children with viral infections).
  • Shingles (herpes zoster): A painful rash that can occur later in life due to reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus.

Prevention of Chickenpox

The most effective way to prevent chickenpox is through vaccination.

  • Chickenpox vaccine: The varicella vaccine is safe and effective. Two doses are recommended for lasting protection.

The vaccine is recommended for:

  • All healthy children
  • Unvaccinated older children, adolescents, and adults
  • People who have never had chickenpox
