Cirrhosis

About Cirrhosis

Cirrhosis is a late-stage liver disease in which healthy liver tissue is replaced by scar tissue, blocking blood flow through the liver and preventing it from functioning properly. The scar tissue disrupts the liver's ability to perform its vital functions.

The liver is responsible for many essential functions, including filtering toxins from the blood, producing bile (which helps with digestion), storing energy, and making proteins that help with blood clotting.

Symptoms of Cirrhosis

In the early stages, many people with cirrhosis have no symptoms. As the disease progresses, symptoms may include:

  • Fatigue
  • Weakness
  • Loss of appetite
  • Nausea
  • Weight loss
  • Abdominal pain
  • Swelling in the legs, ankles, or feet (edema)
  • Swelling in the abdomen (ascites)
  • Itching
  • Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes)
  • Easy bruising or bleeding
  • Dark urine
  • Pale or clay-colored stools

As cirrhosis progresses, more severe complications can occur.

Causes of Cirrhosis

Cirrhosis is most commonly caused by:

  • Chronic alcohol abuse: Long-term excessive alcohol consumption is a major cause of cirrhosis.
  • Chronic hepatitis infections: Hepatitis B and C infections can cause long-term inflammation and damage to the liver.
  • Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) and nonalcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH): Conditions associated with obesity, diabetes, and high cholesterol. Fat builds up in the liver, leading to inflammation and damage.

Other less common causes of cirrhosis include:

  • Autoimmune hepatitis: The body's immune system attacks the liver.
  • Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC): A condition that damages the bile ducts in the liver.
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC): Inflammation and scarring of the bile ducts.
  • Genetic disorders: Such as hemochromatosis (iron buildup in the body) and Wilson's disease (copper buildup in the liver).
  • Certain medications and toxins
  • Repeated episodes of heart failure with liver congestion

Complications of Cirrhosis

Cirrhosis can lead to a number of serious complications:

  • Portal hypertension: Increased pressure in the portal vein, which carries blood from the digestive system to the liver.
  • Varices: Enlarged veins in the esophagus or stomach, which can rupture and cause life-threatening bleeding.
  • Ascites: Accumulation of fluid in the abdomen.
  • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP): An infection of the fluid in the abdomen.
  • Hepatic encephalopathy: Brain dysfunction due to the buildup of toxins in the blood.
  • Jaundice: Yellowing of the skin and eyes.
  • Liver cancer (hepatocellular carcinoma): Cirrhosis increases the risk of liver cancer.
  • Kidney failure (hepatorenal syndrome)
  • Increased risk of infections
  • Malnutrition

Diagnosing Cirrhosis

Diagnosing cirrhosis typically involves a combination of:

  • Medical history: The doctor will ask about your alcohol consumption, hepatitis history, and other risk factors.
  • Physical examination: The doctor will look for signs of liver damage, such as jaundice, ascites, and enlarged spleen.
  • Blood tests:
    • Liver function tests: To check levels of liver enzymes (AST, ALT), bilirubin, and albumin.
    • Complete blood count (CBC): To check for anemia and other abnormalities.
    • Coagulation tests: To assess blood clotting ability.
    • Tests for hepatitis viruses
  • Imaging tests:
    • Ultrasound: To visualize the liver and look for signs of scarring and other abnormalities.
    • CT scan or MRI: Can provide more detailed images of the liver.
    • FibroScan: A noninvasive test that measures liver stiffness.
  • Liver biopsy: A tissue sample from the liver is examined under a microscope to confirm the diagnosis and determine the cause and severity of liver damage.
  • Endoscopy: To check for varices in the esophagus and stomach.

Treating Cirrhosis

Treatment for cirrhosis focuses on slowing the progression of liver damage, managing symptoms, and preventing complications.

Treatment options may include:

  • Treating the underlying cause:
    • Alcohol cessation: For alcohol-related cirrhosis.
    • Antiviral medications: For hepatitis B or C.
    • Weight loss and diabetes management: For NAFLD/NASH.
    • Corticosteroids or other medications: For autoimmune hepatitis.
    • Medications to treat primary biliary cholangitis (PBC)
  • Managing complications:
    • Diuretics: To reduce fluid buildup in the abdomen and legs.
    • Beta-blockers: To reduce portal hypertension and the risk of variceal bleeding.
    • Endoscopic procedures: To treat varices (banding, sclerotherapy).
    • Paracentesis: To remove fluid from the abdomen.
    • Antibiotics: To treat infections like spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP).
    • Lactulose and rifaximin: To treat hepatic encephalopathy.
  • Liver transplant: In severe cases of cirrhosis, a liver transplant may be the only option.
  • Lifestyle changes:
    • Low-sodium diet: To reduce fluid retention.
    • Alcohol abstinence: Essential for all types of cirrhosis.
    • Healthy diet: To provide adequate nutrition.

Prognosis for Cirrhosis

The prognosis for cirrhosis varies depending on the severity of liver damage and the development of complications.

The Child-Pugh score and the Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD) score are used to assess the severity of cirrhosis and predict survival.

If cirrhosis is diagnosed early and the underlying cause is treated, the progression of the disease can sometimes be slowed. However, cirrhosis is a progressive condition, and liver function will continue to decline over time.

Liver transplantation can significantly improve the survival and quality of life for people with severe cirrhosis.
