Clavicle Fracture

About Clavicle Fracture

A clavicle fracture, commonly known as a broken collarbone, is a break in one of the two bones that connect the shoulder to the chest. The clavicle is located between the rib cage and the shoulder blade.

Clavicle fractures are common, particularly in children and young adults. They often result from falls, sports injuries, or vehicle accidents.

Symptoms of Clavicle Fracture

Symptoms of a clavicle fracture can include:

  • Pain: Intense pain at the site of the fracture.
  • Swelling: Swelling and tenderness over the clavicle.
  • Deformity: A visible bump or deformity in the shoulder area.
  • Bruising: Bruising around the fracture site.
  • Limited range of motion: Difficulty moving the arm and shoulder.
  • A grinding sensation: When attempting to move the shoulder.
  • Popping sound: A snapping or popping sound at the time of the injury.

Causes of Clavicle Fracture

Clavicle fractures are typically caused by:

  • Falls: Falling onto the shoulder or an outstretched arm.
  • Sports injuries: Direct blows to the shoulder, such as in contact sports.
  • Vehicle accidents: Collisions can cause significant force to the shoulder area.
  • Birth injuries: Infants can sometimes fracture their clavicle during the birth process.

Diagnosing Clavicle Fracture

Diagnosing a clavicle fracture usually involves:

  • Physical examination: The doctor will examine the shoulder area, looking for signs of deformity, swelling, and tenderness.
  • X-ray: An X-ray is typically used to confirm the diagnosis and determine the location and severity of the fracture.

Treating Clavicle Fracture

Treatment for a clavicle fracture depends on the severity and location of the break. Most clavicle fractures heal with non-surgical treatment.

Treatment options include:

  • Non-surgical treatment:
    • Pain management: Medications such as acetaminophen or ibuprofen can help relieve pain.
    • Immobilization: A sling is used to support the arm and shoulder and keep the clavicle from moving while it heals.
    • Rest: Avoiding activities that could further injure the clavicle.
    • Physical therapy: Once the initial healing has occurred, physical therapy can help restore strength and range of motion.
  • Surgical treatment:
    • Surgery may be necessary for severe fractures, displaced fractures, or when non-surgical treatment fails. The procedure often involves:
      • Open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF): The broken ends of the clavicle are repositioned and held in place with plates and screws.

Recovery and Rehabilitation

Recovery time for a clavicle fracture varies depending on the severity of the injury and the individual's healing process.

General recovery guidelines include:

  • Healing time: Typically takes 6 to 12 weeks for complete healing in adults. Children often heal faster.
  • Sling use: The sling is usually worn for several weeks.
  • Physical therapy: Exercises to regain shoulder strength and movement.
  • Return to activity: Gradual return to normal activities, avoiding strenuous activity until fully healed.

Complications of Clavicle Fracture

Most clavicle fractures heal without complications. However, potential complications can include:

  • Nonunion: The bone does not heal properly.
  • Malunion: The bone heals in a misaligned position.
  • Nerve or blood vessel injury: Rare, but can occur at the time of the fracture or during surgery.
  • Stiffness: Loss of range of motion in the shoulder.
  • Cosmetic deformity: A visible bump or prominence at the fracture site after healing.
  • Infection: If surgery is required.

Prevention of Clavicle Fracture

While it is not always possible to prevent clavicle fractures, some measures can help reduce the risk:

  • Take precautions to avoid falls.
  • Wear appropriate protective gear during sports activities.
  • Use seat belts in vehicles.
