Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

About Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) is a common condition that causes pain, numbness, and tingling in the hand and arm. It occurs when the median nerve, which runs from the forearm into the hand, is compressed as it passes through a narrow passageway in the wrist called the carpal tunnel.

The carpal tunnel is a narrow, bony passageway on the palm side of your wrist. This tunnel protects the median nerve and the tendons that bend your fingers.

Symptoms of Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Symptoms of carpal tunnel syndrome often start gradually and can include:

  • Numbness, tingling, burning, or a pins-and-needles sensation in the fingers (especially the thumb, index, middle, and ring fingers)
  • Pain in the hand and wrist that may radiate up the arm towards the shoulder
  • Weakness in the hand, making it difficult to grip objects or form a fist
  • Dropping things due to weakness or numbness
  • Worsening symptoms at night, which may disrupt sleep
  • Aching in the forearm

In many cases, symptoms are worse in the dominant hand.

Causes of Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Carpal tunnel syndrome is caused by anything that compresses or irritates the median nerve in the carpal tunnel. This can be due to:

  • Repetitive hand and wrist movements: Such as those performed in assembly line work, sewing, typing, or using power tools.
  • Prolonged or frequent use of vibrating tools.
  • Wrist anatomy: A smaller carpal tunnel may predispose someone to CTS.
  • Underlying health conditions:
    • Rheumatoid arthritis and other inflammatory conditions
    • Diabetes
    • Thyroid disorders
    • Fluid retention during pregnancy or menopause
  • Wrist injuries: Such as sprains or fractures.

Diagnosing Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Diagnosing carpal tunnel syndrome typically involves:

  • Medical history: The doctor will ask about your symptoms, activities, and medical conditions.
  • Physical examination:
    • Tinel's sign: Tapping on the median nerve at the wrist to see if it causes tingling.
    • Phalen's test: Bending the wrists together for a minute to see if it causes symptoms.
    • Evaluating hand strength and sensation.
  • Nerve conduction studies: To measure how well the median nerve is working.
  • Electromyography (EMG): To check the muscles controlled by the median nerve.
  • Imaging tests (X-rays, ultrasound, MRI): May be used to rule out other causes of wrist pain, such as arthritis or a fracture, but are not typically used to diagnose CTS itself.

Treating Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Treatment for carpal tunnel syndrome aims to relieve symptoms and prevent nerve damage. Treatment options vary depending on the severity of the condition:

  • Nonsurgical treatments:
    • Wrist splinting: Wearing a splint, especially at night, to keep the wrist in a neutral position.
    • Activity modification: Avoiding activities that worsen symptoms.
    • Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs): To relieve pain and inflammation.
    • Corticosteroid injections: Injecting the carpal tunnel with corticosteroids to reduce inflammation and pressure on the median nerve.
    • Physical therapy: Exercises to strengthen the wrist and hand.
    • Ergonomic changes: Adjusting the workspace to reduce strain on the wrists.
  • Surgical treatment:
    • Carpal tunnel release surgery: Cutting the carpal ligament to relieve pressure on the median nerve. This can be done through an open incision or with an endoscopic technique.

Prevention of Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

While it's not always possible to prevent carpal tunnel syndrome, some measures may help reduce your risk:

  • Maintain a neutral wrist position: Avoid bending your wrist up or down for extended periods.
  • Reduce force and relax your grip: Avoid gripping tools or objects too tightly.
  • Take short breaks: Rest your hands and wrists frequently when performing repetitive tasks.
  • Improve your posture: Proper posture can help prevent strain on your wrists and hands.
  • Keep your hands warm: Cold hands are more likely to develop pain and stiffness.
  • Use proper tools and equipment: Ensure that your tools are the right size and design for your hands and the task.
  • Manage underlying health conditions: Such as diabetes and arthritis.
