Cough

About Cough

A cough is a reflex action that helps clear the airways of mucus, irritants, and foreign particles. It's a common symptom of many respiratory conditions and can be either acute (short-term) or chronic (long-term).

Coughing is a vital defense mechanism, but persistent coughing can be uncomfortable, exhausting, and may indicate an underlying medical problem.

Types of Cough

Coughs can be classified based on their duration and characteristics:

  • Acute cough: Lasts less than 3 weeks. It's often caused by a viral infection, such as the common cold or flu.
  • Subacute cough: Lasts between 3 and 8 weeks. It may occur after an acute infection or be due to other causes.
  • Chronic cough: Persists for more than 8 weeks. It can be a symptom of an underlying medical condition.

Coughs can also be described by their characteristics:

  • Dry cough: Doesn't produce mucus or phlegm. It may be caused by irritants, allergies, or certain medications.
  • Wet cough (productive cough): Produces mucus or phlegm. It's often associated with infections like bronchitis or pneumonia.
  • Barking cough: A harsh, loud cough, often associated with croup in children.
  • Whooping cough: A severe cough with a "whoop" sound when inhaling, characteristic of pertussis.

Causes of Cough

Coughs can be caused by a wide range of factors, including:

  • Infections:
    • Common cold
    • Influenza (flu)
    • Pneumonia
    • Bronchitis
    • Sinusitis
    • Whooping cough (pertussis)
    • Croup (in children)
  • Allergies: Such as allergic rhinitis (hay fever)
  • Asthma: Coughing may be the main symptom, especially in cough-variant asthma.
  • Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD): Stomach acid backing up into the esophagus can trigger a cough.
  • Postnasal drip: Mucus from the nose dripping down the throat.
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD): A group of lung diseases, including emphysema and chronic bronchitis.
  • Smoking: A common cause of chronic cough.
  • Medications: Some drugs, such as ACE inhibitors (used for high blood pressure), can cause a dry cough.
  • Irritants: Inhaling irritants like dust, fumes, smoke, or pollutants.
  • Lung diseases: Such as pulmonary fibrosis or lung cancer.
  • Vocal cord disorders
  • Nervous system conditions

When to See a Doctor

While most coughs are not serious and resolve on their own, you should see a doctor if:

  • The cough lasts for more than 3 weeks.
  • You are coughing up blood.
  • You have difficulty breathing or shortness of breath.
  • You have chest pain.
  • You have a high fever.
  • You are wheezing.
  • You have a weak immune system.
  • Your cough is accompanied by other concerning symptoms, such as unexplained weight loss, night sweats, or fatigue.
  • You suspect whooping cough.

Diagnosing Cough

To determine the cause of a cough, a doctor may:

  • Ask about your medical history and symptoms.
  • Perform a physical exam, including listening to your lungs with a stethoscope.
  • Order tests, such as:
    • Chest X-ray: To check for lung infections or other lung conditions.
    • Sputum test: To analyze mucus for bacteria or other organisms.
    • Pulmonary function tests: To assess lung function.
    • Sinus X-rays or CT scan: To check for sinusitis.
    • Bronchoscopy: To examine the airways.
    • Allergy tests: To identify allergies.
    • pH monitoring or endoscopy: To check for GERD.

Treating Cough

Treatment for cough depends on the underlying cause.

General recommendations:

  • Drink plenty of fluids: To stay hydrated and loosen mucus.
  • Rest: To allow your body to recover.
  • Avoid irritants: Such as smoke, dust, and pollutants.

Specific treatments may include:

  • For infections:
    • Antibiotics: For bacterial infections like pneumonia or bronchitis.
    • Antiviral medications: For influenza.
  • For allergies:
    • Antihistamines
    • Nasal corticosteroids
    • Decongestants
  • For asthma:
    • Inhaled corticosteroids
    • Bronchodilators
  • For GERD:
    • Antacids
    • H2 blockers
    • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)
  • For postnasal drip:
    • Saline nasal sprays
    • Decongestants
  • For chronic cough: Treatment of the underlying condition, such as COPD, lung disease, or heart failure.
  • Cough suppressants: Over-the-counter or prescription medications to suppress the cough reflex (e.g., dextromethorphan, codeine). Use with caution, and generally not recommended for productive coughs.
  • Expectorants: Medications to help loosen mucus (e.g., guaifenesin).

Home Remedies for Cough

Several home remedies can help soothe a cough:

  • Honey: Can help soothe a sore throat and suppress coughs, especially in children (not for infants under 1 year old).
  • Warm liquids: Such as tea or broth, can help soothe the throat and loosen mucus.
  • Humidifier: Can help moisten the air and ease congestion.
  • Saltwater gargle: Can help soothe a sore throat.
  • Ginger: May help with coughs related to colds.
  • Steam inhalation: Can help loosen mucus.
