About NAFLD

Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is a condition in which excess fat accumulates in the liver of people who drink little or no alcohol. It is one of the most common causes of chronic liver disease in the world. If left untreated, NAFLD can progress to more severe conditions, including non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), cirrhosis, and liver cancer.

Symptoms

NAFLD often has no symptoms in its early stages. When symptoms do occur, they may include:

  • Fatigue
  • Abdominal pain or discomfort in the upper right abdomen
  • Enlarged liver

If NAFLD progresses to NASH, symptoms may worsen and include:

  • Abdominal swelling
  • Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes)
  • Easy bruising or bleeding

Causes

The exact causes of NAFLD are not fully understood, but it is closely linked to:

  • Obesity
  • Insulin resistance
  • High levels of triglycerides or "bad" cholesterol (LDL)
  • Metabolic syndrome (a cluster of conditions that increase the risk of heart disease, stroke, and type 2 diabetes)
  • Type 2 diabetes

Other potential factors include:

  • Genetics
  • Certain medications

Diagnosing

Diagnosing NAFLD may involve:

  • Blood tests (liver enzymes, etc.)
  • Imaging tests:
    • Ultrasound
    • CT scan (computed tomography)
    • MRI (magnetic resonance imaging)
  • Liver biopsy (to examine a tissue sample - especially for NASH)

Prevention

You can reduce your risk of NAFLD by:

  • Maintaining a healthy weight
  • Eating a healthy diet
  • Exercising regularly
  • Controlling diabetes and cholesterol

Treating

The primary treatment for NAFLD involves lifestyle changes:

  • Weight loss
  • Healthy diet
  • Regular exercise
  • Medications (to manage related conditions like diabetes, high cholesterol)
  • Vitamin E (in some cases)
