About Neuroblastoma

Neuroblastoma is a type of cancer that develops in immature nerve cells called neuroblasts. These cells are found in several areas of the body, but neuroblastoma most commonly begins in the adrenal glands, which sit atop the kidneys. It can also occur in nerve tissue in the neck, chest, abdomen, or pelvis. Neuroblastoma is most common in infants and young children.

Symptoms

Symptoms of neuroblastoma vary depending on the location of the tumor. Some general symptoms may include:

  • Abdominal pain or swelling
  • Bone pain
  • Chest pain
  • Coughing or difficulty breathing
  • Fatigue
  • Fever
  • Loss of appetite
  • Lumps in the neck, chest, abdomen, or pelvis

Additional symptoms, depending on the tumor location:

  • In the chest: May cause wheezing, changes in the eyes (Horner's syndrome).
  • In the abdomen: May cause swelling, constipation, changes in bowel habits.
  • In the spinal cord: May cause weakness, numbness, or loss of movement.

Causes

The exact causes of neuroblastoma are not fully understood. It occurs due to errors in the development of neuroblasts. Normally, neuroblasts mature into nerve cells, but in neuroblastoma, they grow uncontrollably. While the cause is usually unknown, some factors may be involved:

  • Genetic mutations: Changes in DNA within the cells.
  • Family history: A small percentage of cases are hereditary.

Diagnosing

Diagnosing neuroblastoma may involve:

  • Physical exam
  • Imaging tests:
    • X-rays
    • CT scan (computed tomography)
    • MRI (magnetic resonance imaging)
    • MIBG scan (metaiodobenzylguanidine)
    • Bone scan
  • Urine and blood tests
  • Biopsy (removing tissue for examination)
  • Bone marrow aspiration and biopsy

Prevention

There is no known way to prevent most cases of neuroblastoma, as the exact causes are not well-understood.

Treating

Treatment for neuroblastoma depends on several factors, including the child's age, the stage of the cancer, and the location of the tumor. Treatment options may include:

  • Surgery (to remove the tumor)
  • Chemotherapy (to kill cancer cells)
  • Radiation therapy (to destroy cancer cells)
  • Stem cell transplantation (high-dose chemotherapy followed by stem cell rescue)
  • Immunotherapy (to help the body's immune system fight the cancer)
  • Targeted therapy (drugs that attack specific cancer cells)
  • Observation (in some low-risk cases)
