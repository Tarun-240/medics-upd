About Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

Non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL) is a type of cancer that begins in the lymphatic system, which is part of the body's immune system. The lymphatic system helps fight infections and includes lymph nodes, spleen, thymus gland, and bone marrow. In NHL, white blood cells called lymphocytes grow abnormally and form tumors. There are many different subtypes of NHL.

Symptoms

Symptoms of NHL can vary depending on the subtype and the part of the body affected. Some common symptoms include:

  • Swollen lymph nodes (in the neck, armpits, or groin)
  • Fever
  • Night sweats
  • Fatigue
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Cough, chest pain, or shortness of breath
  • Abdominal pain or swelling
  • Skin rash or itchy skin

Causes

The exact causes of NHL are not fully understood, but it occurs when lymphocytes develop changes (mutations) in their DNA. These mutations cause the cells to grow and divide uncontrollably. Risk factors that can increase the likelihood of developing NHL include:

  • A weakened immune system (e.g., due to HIV/AIDS, organ transplant, or certain medications)
  • Certain infections (e.g., Epstein-Barr virus, Helicobacter pylori)
  • Older age
  • Exposure to certain chemicals (e.g., pesticides, benzene)
  • Previous cancer treatment (chemotherapy or radiation therapy)
  • Family history of lymphoma

Diagnosing

Diagnosing NHL may involve:

  • Physical exam
  • Lymph node biopsy (removing a lymph node or a piece of it for examination)
  • Blood tests
  • Imaging tests:
    • CT scan (computed tomography)
    • PET scan (positron emission tomography)
    • MRI (magnetic resonance imaging)
  • Bone marrow biopsy

Prevention

Most cases of NHL cannot be prevented. However, you can reduce your risk by:

  • Maintaining a healthy immune system
  • Avoiding exposure to known carcinogens (cancer-causing substances)
  • Treating infections promptly

Treating

Treatment for NHL depends on the subtype, stage, and the patient's overall health. Options may include:

  • Chemotherapy (to kill cancer cells)
  • Radiation therapy (to destroy cancer cells)
  • Immunotherapy (to help the body's immune system fight the cancer)
  • Targeted therapy (drugs that attack specific cancer cells)
  • Stem cell transplantation (in some cases)
  • Surgery (rarely used)
  • Watchful waiting (for slow-growing lymphomas)
