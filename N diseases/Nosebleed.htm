About Nosebleeds

A nosebleed, also known as epistaxis, is bleeding from the nose. Nosebleeds are common and can usually be managed at home. They occur because the lining of the nose contains many tiny blood vessels that are close to the surface and can be easily damaged.

Symptoms

The primary symptom of a nosebleed is bleeding from one or both nostrils. The blood flow can range from a slow trickle to a heavy flow. In most cases, the blood flows out of the nose, but it can also flow down the back of the throat, especially if the person is lying down.

Causes

Nosebleeds can be caused by various factors. Common causes include:

  • Dry air: Dry nasal passages are more prone to bleeding.
  • Nose picking: Can injure the delicate lining of the nose.
  • Sinusitis and colds: Infections can cause congestion and irritation.
  • Forceful blowing: Can irritate the nasal lining.
  • Allergies: Can cause itching and inflammation.
  • Blood-thinning medications: Such as aspirin or warfarin.
  • High blood pressure: May contribute to nosebleeds.
  • Deviated septum: An abnormal structure within the nose.

Diagnosing

Most nosebleeds do not require a visit to the doctor. However, if nosebleeds are frequent, severe, or difficult to control, a doctor may perform the following:

  • Physical exam
  • Medical history review
  • Nasal endoscopy (using a small camera to view the inside of the nose)
  • Blood tests (in some cases)
  • Imaging tests (if an underlying problem is suspected)

Prevention

You can reduce your risk of nosebleeds by:

  • Keeping the nasal passages moist (using a humidifier or saline nasal spray)
  • Avoiding nose picking
  • Blowing your nose gently
  • Avoiding dry air
  • Managing allergies

Treating

Most nosebleeds can be treated at home. Here's what you can do:

  • Sit upright and lean forward
  • Pinch your nostrils closed for 10-15 minutes
  • Breathe through your mouth
  • Apply a cold compress to your nose

If nosebleeds are severe or recurrent, medical treatment may include:

  • Nasal packing
  • Cauterization (sealing the blood vessels)
  • Medications
  • Surgery (in rare cases)
