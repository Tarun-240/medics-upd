Low Blood Pressure (Hypotension)

Understanding Low Blood Pressure

Low blood pressure, also known as hypotension, occurs when the force of blood pushing against the walls of your arteries is consistently too low. Blood pressure is measured with two numbers: systolic (the pressure when your heart beats) and diastolic (the pressure when your heart rests between beats). Generally, blood pressure readings below 90 millimeters of mercury (mm Hg) for the systolic reading or 60 mm Hg for the diastolic reading are considered low.

For many healthy individuals, low blood pressure without symptoms is not a cause for concern and may even be normal. However, when blood pressure drops too low, it can reduce blood flow to your brain and other vital organs, leading to symptoms.

Common Symptoms of Low Blood Pressure

Symptoms of low blood pressure can vary from person to person and may depend on how quickly the blood pressure drops. Common symptoms include:

  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Fainting (syncope)
  • Blurred or fading vision
  • Nausea
  • Fatigue
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Cold, clammy, pale skin
  • Rapid, shallow breathing
  • Thirst

In severe cases, low blood pressure can lead to shock, a life-threatening condition. Symptoms of shock include confusion, rapid heartbeat, rapid breathing, and decreased or no urine output.

Types and Causes of Low Blood Pressure

Low blood pressure can be categorized into several types, each with different causes:

  • **Orthostatic Hypotension (Postural Hypotension):** A sudden drop in blood pressure when you stand up from a sitting or lying position. This is a common cause of dizziness. It can be due to dehydration, prolonged bed rest, certain medications, or nervous system disorders.
  • **Postprandial Hypotension:** A drop in blood pressure that occurs 1 to 2 hours after eating. It's more common in older adults and may be related to blood flow shifting to the digestive system.
  • **Neurally Mediated Hypotension (NMH):** This occurs when there's a miscommunication between the heart and the brain that can lead to a sudden drop in blood pressure after standing for long periods. It's more common in children and young adults.
  • **Severe Hypotension Related to Medical Conditions:** Certain medical conditions can cause low blood pressure, including:
    • Heart problems (e.g., heart failure, heart valve problems, bradycardia)
    • Endocrine problems (e.g., hypothyroidism, Addison's disease, low blood sugar)
    • Dehydration
    • Severe infection (septicemia)
    • Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis)
    • Blood loss (e.g., from injury or internal bleeding)
    • Nutritional deficiencies (e.g., low vitamin B12 or folate)
  • **Medications:** Many medications can cause low blood pressure as a side effect, including diuretics, beta-blockers, alpha-blockers, some antidepressants, and medications for Parkinson's disease.
  • **Pregnancy:** Blood pressure may drop during pregnancy due to circulatory system changes. It usually returns to normal after delivery.

When to Seek Medical Advice for Low Blood Pressure

While occasional dizziness or lightheadedness may not be serious, you should see a doctor if you experience low blood pressure symptoms regularly, especially if they are severe or persistent. Seek immediate medical attention if you have symptoms of shock.

It's important to discuss any symptoms of low blood pressure with your doctor, especially if you are taking medications that could be contributing to it or if you have underlying medical conditions.

Diagnosis of Low Blood Pressure

Your doctor will diagnose low blood pressure based on:

  • **Blood Pressure Readings:** Repeatedly checking your blood pressure, including while sitting, lying down, and standing up (to check for orthostatic hypotension).
  • **Medical History and Physical Exam:** Discussing your symptoms, medical conditions, and medications.
  • **Blood Tests:** To check for underlying conditions like anemia, low blood sugar, or thyroid problems.
  • **Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG):** To check your heart's electrical activity.
  • **Tilt Table Test:** To evaluate how your body responds to changes in position. You lie on a table that is tilted from a horizontal to an upright position while your blood pressure and heart rate are monitored.

Management and Lifestyle Tips for Low Blood Pressure

Treatment for low blood pressure depends on the cause and severity of your symptoms. If you don't have significant symptoms, treatment may not be necessary. However, if you do experience symptoms, the following lifestyle changes and medical treatments may help:

  • **Increase Salt Intake:** Unless your doctor advises otherwise for other medical conditions, slightly increasing your salt intake can help raise blood pressure.
  • **Stay Hydrated:** Drink plenty of fluids, especially water, to prevent dehydration, which can lower blood pressure.
  • **Avoid Alcohol:** Alcohol can dehydrate you and lower blood pressure.
  • **Eat Small, Frequent Meals:** To help prevent postprandial hypotension.
  • **Wear Compression Stockings:** These can help reduce blood pooling in your legs and improve blood circulation.
  • **Avoid Prolonged Standing:** If you need to stand for a long time, shift your weight and flex your leg muscles.
  • **Rise Slowly:** When getting up from a lying or sitting position, do so slowly to avoid orthostatic hypotension.
  • **Elevate Your Head While Sleeping:** This may help reduce the effects of gravity on blood pressure.
  • **Medications:** In some cases, your doctor may prescribe medications to help raise your blood pressure. Examples include fludrocortisone and midodrine.
  • **Manage Underlying Conditions:** Treating any underlying medical conditions that are contributing to low blood pressure is crucial.
  • **Review Medications:** Your doctor may adjust or change medications that are causing low blood pressure.

It's important to work closely with your doctor to identify the cause of your low blood pressure and develop an appropriate management plan.

Tips for Managing Low Blood Pressure

Lifestyle adjustments to help raise blood pressure.

  • Increase fluid intake
  • Eat salty foods in moderation
  • Avoid excessive alcohol
  • Rise slowly from sitting or lying
  • Wear compression stockings
  • Eat smaller, frequent meals
