About Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder is a mental health condition that causes unusual shifts in mood, energy, activity levels, concentration, and the ability to carry out day-to-day tasks. There are several types of bipolar disorder, including bipolar I, bipolar II, and cyclothymic disorder.

Symptoms

Bipolar disorder involves periods of extremely elevated mood (mania or hypomania) and periods of extremely low mood (depression).

  • Manic symptoms may include:
    • Excessive happiness, excitement, or irritability
    • Increased energy and activity
    • Decreased need for sleep
    • Racing thoughts
    • Talkativeness
    • Impulsive behavior (e.g., reckless spending, risky sexual behavior)
    • Inflated self-esteem
  • Depressive symptoms may include:
    • Persistent sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness
    • Loss of interest in activities
    • Fatigue or low energy
    • Difficulty concentrating
    • Changes in appetite or weight
    • Sleep problems
    • Thoughts of death or suicide

Causes

The exact cause of bipolar disorder is unknown, but it likely results from a combination of factors:

  • Genetics: Having a family history of bipolar disorder increases your risk.
  • Brain structure and chemistry: Imbalances in brain chemicals (neurotransmitters) and differences in brain structure may play a role.
  • Stress: Stressful life events can sometimes trigger episodes.

Diagnosing

Bipolar disorder is diagnosed by a mental health professional based on:

  • A thorough psychiatric evaluation
  • Medical history review
  • Discussion of symptoms
  • Mood charting
  • Ruling out other conditions

Prevention

While bipolar disorder cannot always be prevented, early diagnosis and treatment can help manage the condition and reduce the severity and frequency of episodes. Adhering to a treatment plan is crucial.

  • Early diagnosis and treatment
  • Adhering to treatment plans
  • Stress management
  • Healthy lifestyle habits

Treating

Treatment for bipolar disorder typically involves a combination of:

  • Medications (mood stabilizers, antipsychotics)
  • Psychotherapy (e.g., CBT, family-focused therapy)
  • Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) (in some cases)
  • Hospitalization (during severe episodes)
