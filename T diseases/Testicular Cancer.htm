Testicular Cancer

Understanding Testicular Cancer

Testicular cancer is a cancer that begins in the testicles, which are located inside the scrotum, a loose sac of skin under the penis. The testicles produce male sex hormones and sperm for reproduction. Testicular cancer is relatively rare compared to other types of cancer, but it is the most common cancer in American males between the ages of 15 and 35.

Testicular cancer is highly treatable, even when it has spread beyond the testicle. The prognosis is generally very good, especially when detected early.

Common Signs and Symptoms

Many men with testicular cancer don't have any symptoms, especially in the early stages. However, some common signs and symptoms include:

  • **A lump or enlargement in either testicle:** This is the most common sign. The lump may be painless.
  • **A feeling of heaviness in the scrotum.**
  • **A dull ache in the abdomen or groin.**
  • **A sudden collection of fluid in the scrotum.**
  • **Pain or discomfort in a testicle or the scrotum.**
  • **Changes in the size or firmness of a testicle.**

It's important to note that some of these symptoms can also be caused by other conditions, such as injury or infection. However, if you experience any of these signs, it's crucial to see a doctor for evaluation.

Risk Factors

Several factors can increase the risk of developing testicular cancer:

  • **Undescended testicle (cryptorchidism):** A testicle that doesn't descend into the scrotum during infancy. This is the most significant risk factor. Even if the testicle is surgically moved to the scrotum, the risk remains higher.
  • **Family history of testicular cancer:** Having a father or brother who has had testicular cancer increases your risk.
  • **Personal history of testicular cancer:** If you've had cancer in one testicle, you have an increased risk of developing it in the other.
  • **Age:** Testicular cancer is most common in men between 15 and 35 years old.
  • **Race:** White men are more likely to develop testicular cancer than men of other races.
  • **Abnormal testicle development:** Conditions such as Klinefelter syndrome may increase the risk.

Diagnosis

If a doctor suspects testicular cancer, they will likely perform several tests:

  • **Physical exam:** The doctor will examine the testicles, checking for lumps, swelling, or tenderness.
  • **Ultrasound:** An imaging test that uses sound waves to create pictures of the inside of the scrotum. It can help determine if a lump is solid or fluid-filled and can identify abnormalities in the testicle.
  • **Blood tests:** To measure the levels of certain tumor markers, such as alpha-fetoprotein (AFP), human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH). Elevated levels of these markers can suggest testicular cancer.
  • **Surgery (radical inguinal orchiectomy):** If cancer is suspected, the entire testicle is usually surgically removed through an incision in the groin. This is the definitive way to diagnose testicular cancer and determine its type and stage. A biopsy is not typically done on the testicle itself due to the risk of spreading cancer cells.

After the testicle is removed, it is examined under a microscope to determine the type of cancer cells and the extent of the disease (stage). Staging helps guide treatment decisions.

Treatment

Treatment for testicular cancer depends on the type and stage of the cancer, as well as the patient's overall health and preferences. Common treatment options include:

  • **Surgery (orchiectomy):** Removal of the affected testicle is the primary treatment for most stages of testicular cancer.
  • **Radiation therapy:** Uses high-energy beams to kill cancer cells. It may be used after surgery, especially for certain types of testicular cancer or if the cancer has spread to nearby lymph nodes.
  • **Chemotherapy:** Uses drugs to kill cancer cells throughout the body. It may be used after surgery if there is a high risk of the cancer returning or if it has spread to other parts of the body.
  • **Surveillance:** In some early-stage cases, especially for certain types of non-seminoma tumors, doctors may recommend active surveillance. This involves regular checkups, blood tests, and imaging scans to monitor for any signs of cancer recurrence. Treatment is started if the cancer returns or progresses.

Testicular cancer is often treated by a team of specialists, including urologists, oncologists, and radiation oncologists. The treatment plan is tailored to the individual patient.

Self-Examination

While not a substitute for regular medical checkups, testicular self-examination (TSE) can help men become familiar with the normal size and feel of their testicles and identify any changes that may warrant medical attention. The best time to perform TSE is during or after a warm shower or bath, when the scrotum is relaxed.

Here's how to perform a testicular self-exam:

  1. Gently roll each testicle between your thumb and fingers.
  2. Feel for any hard lumps or smooth, rounded masses.
  3. Note any changes in the size, shape, or consistency of your testicles.
  4. It's normal for one testicle to be slightly larger than the other and for the epididymis (a tube at the back of the testicle) to be felt.
  5. Perform this exam once a month and report any changes to your doctor.

Important Considerations

  • Early detection is crucial for successful treatment of testicular cancer.
  • Regular self-examination can help identify potential problems early.
  • Don't hesitate to see a doctor if you notice any changes in your testicles.
  • Testicular cancer is highly treatable, and most men go on to live full and healthy lives after treatment.

Important Information

Regular testicular self-exams and awareness of the signs and symptoms are important for early detection of testicular cancer. If you notice any changes in your testicles, consult a doctor promptly. Early diagnosis significantly improves the chances of successful treatment.
