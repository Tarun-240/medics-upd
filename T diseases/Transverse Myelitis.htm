Transverse Myelitis

Understanding Transverse Myelitis

Transverse myelitis (TM) is a rare neurological disorder caused by inflammation of the spinal cord. This inflammation can damage the myelin sheath, the insulating layer covering nerve fibers, which disrupts the signals between the spinal cord and the rest of the body. TM can result in pain, muscle weakness, sensory problems, and bowel and bladder dysfunction.

The term "transverse" refers to the inflammation often extending across the entire width of the spinal cord. The severity of symptoms and the extent of recovery vary greatly among individuals.

Common Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms of transverse myelitis can develop rapidly over hours to days or gradually over several weeks. They often include one or more of the following:

  • **Pain:** Often starts suddenly in the back and may radiate around the torso or down the arms or legs.
  • **Weakness in the arms or legs:** This can range from mild heaviness to complete paralysis.
  • **Sensory problems:** Numbness, tingling, burning, coldness, or a heightened sensitivity to touch. Some individuals describe a band-like sensation of tightness around the torso.
  • **Bowel and bladder dysfunction:** This can include difficulty starting urination, urinary retention, increased frequency, incontinence, constipation, or loss of bowel control.

Other possible symptoms include:

  • Spasticity or muscle stiffness.
  • Lhermitte's sign (an electrical sensation that runs down the back and limbs upon neck flexion).

Possible Causes

The exact cause of transverse myelitis is not always clear (idiopathic). However, several conditions are associated with its development:

  • **Multiple sclerosis (MS):** TM can be the first symptom of MS or occur during the course of the disease.
  • **Neuromyelitis optica (NMO):** An autoimmune disorder that primarily affects the optic nerves and spinal cord.
  • **Infections:** Viral (e.g., herpes viruses, Epstein-Barr virus, HIV), bacterial (e.g., Lyme disease, syphilis), or fungal infections can trigger TM.
  • **Autoimmune disorders:** Conditions like systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), Sjogren's syndrome, and sarcoidosis can be associated with TM.
  • **Inflammatory conditions:** Such as acute disseminated encephalomyelitis (ADEM).
  • **Vaccinations:** Rarely, TM has been reported following certain vaccinations.
  • **Vascular disorders:** Reduced blood flow to the spinal cord.

Diagnosis

Diagnosing transverse myelitis involves a thorough neurological examination and several tests:

  • **Medical history and physical exam:** The doctor will ask about your symptoms, their onset, and your medical history. A neurological exam will assess your muscle strength, sensation, reflexes, and coordination.
  • **Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the spinal cord:** MRI can show inflammation or lesions in the spinal cord and help rule out other conditions like spinal cord compression.
  • **Lumbar puncture (spinal tap):** Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is collected and analyzed for signs of inflammation, infection, or abnormal antibodies.
  • **Blood tests:** To check for infections, autoimmune disorders, and specific antibodies associated with conditions like NMO.

Treatment

Treatment for transverse myelitis focuses on reducing spinal cord inflammation, managing symptoms, and preventing complications. The initial treatment often involves:

  • **Corticosteroids:** High-dose intravenous corticosteroids are typically the first-line treatment to reduce inflammation in the spinal cord. This is usually followed by oral corticosteroids.
  • **Plasma exchange therapy (plasmapheresis):** This procedure may be used if corticosteroids are not effective. It involves removing blood, separating the plasma (the liquid part), and replacing it with new plasma or a plasma substitute. This can help remove harmful antibodies from the blood.
  • **Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIg):** In some cases, IVIg therapy may be used to modulate the immune system.
  • **Pain management:** Medications such as nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), neuropathic pain medications (e.g., gabapentin, pregabalin), and muscle relaxants may be used to manage pain and spasticity.
  • **Treatment of underlying conditions:** If TM is associated with an infection or autoimmune disorder, treating that underlying condition is crucial.

Long-term management and rehabilitation are essential for recovery:

  • **Physical therapy:** To improve muscle strength, flexibility, balance, and coordination.
  • **Occupational therapy:** To help with daily living activities and adaptive strategies.
  • **Bowel and bladder retraining:** To manage incontinence and improve function.
  • **Psychological support:** To address emotional challenges such as depression and anxiety.

The recovery from transverse myelitis varies greatly. Some individuals may have significant recovery, while others may experience ongoing disability. Early diagnosis and treatment are important for improving the chances of a better outcome.

Important Considerations

  • Transverse myelitis is a rare condition, and its course can be unpredictable.
  • Prompt medical attention is crucial for diagnosis and treatment.
  • Rehabilitation plays a vital role in maximizing recovery and adapting to any residual deficits.
  • Ongoing management and support are often necessary.

Important Information

If you experience sudden back pain, weakness in your limbs, sensory changes, or bowel/bladder problems, seek immediate medical attention. Early diagnosis and treatment of transverse myelitis are essential for the best possible outcome.
