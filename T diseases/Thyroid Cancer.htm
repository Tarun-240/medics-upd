Thyroid Cancer

Understanding Thyroid Cancer

Thyroid cancer is a type of cancer that begins in the thyroid gland, a butterfly-shaped gland located at the base of your neck, just below your Adam's apple. The thyroid gland produces hormones that regulate your heart rate, blood pressure, body temperature, and weight.

Thyroid cancer is relatively common and is often highly treatable, especially when detected early. There are several main types of thyroid cancer, with papillary and follicular thyroid cancers being the most common. Other less common types include medullary and anaplastic thyroid cancers.

Common Signs and Symptoms

In the early stages, thyroid cancer may not cause any noticeable symptoms. As the cancer grows, you may experience:

  • **A lump (nodule) in the neck:** This is the most common sign. The nodule is often painless but can sometimes grow quickly.
  • **Hoarseness or other voice changes.**
  • **Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia).**
  • **Pain in the neck or throat.**
  • **Swollen lymph nodes in the neck.**

It's important to note that many benign (non-cancerous) conditions can also cause nodules or swelling in the thyroid gland. However, if you experience any of these symptoms, it's essential to see a doctor for evaluation.

Risk Factors

Factors that may increase your risk of thyroid cancer include:

  • **A history of radiation exposure:** Especially to the head and neck during childhood.
  • **Family history of thyroid cancer or certain genetic syndromes:** Such as multiple endocrine neoplasia type 2 (MEN 2).
  • **Female sex:** Thyroid cancer is more common in women than in men.
  • **Age:** While it can occur at any age, some types are more common in certain age groups. Papillary and follicular cancers are more common in younger adults, while anaplastic cancer is more common in older adults.
  • **Iodine deficiency (in some parts of the world):** While less common in areas with iodized salt, iodine deficiency can increase the risk of follicular thyroid cancer.
  • **Certain thyroid conditions:** Such as Hashimoto's thyroiditis and goiter.

Diagnosis

Diagnosing thyroid cancer typically involves one or more of the following procedures:

  • **Physical exam:** The doctor will feel your neck to check for nodules or swelling.
  • **Blood tests:** To measure thyroid hormone levels (TSH, T3, T4) and thyroid antibodies, which can help identify thyroid disease but are not specific for cancer.
  • **Ultrasound:** An imaging test that uses sound waves to create pictures of the thyroid gland. It can help determine the size and characteristics of thyroid nodules.
  • **Fine-needle aspiration (FNA) biopsy:** A thin needle is guided by ultrasound to extract cells from the thyroid nodule. These cells are then examined under a microscope to determine if cancer cells are present. FNA is the most common way to diagnose thyroid cancer.
  • **Thyroid scan:** A radioactive iodine tracer is swallowed or injected, and a special camera is used to see how the thyroid gland takes up the iodine. "Cold" nodules (those that don't absorb much iodine) are more likely to be cancerous.
  • **Surgical biopsy:** If FNA results are inconclusive or if there are other concerning signs, a surgical biopsy (removing part or all of the thyroid gland) may be necessary for diagnosis.
  • **Genetic testing:** May be recommended in certain cases, especially if there is a family history of medullary thyroid cancer or other related genetic syndromes.

Once thyroid cancer is diagnosed, further tests, such as CT scans or bone scans, may be done to determine if the cancer has spread (staging).

Treatment

Treatment for thyroid cancer is highly effective for most types and stages. Common treatment options include:

  • **Surgery:** The primary treatment for most thyroid cancers involves surgically removing all or part of the thyroid gland (thyroidectomy). The extent of surgery depends on the size and type of the cancer and whether it has spread. Nearby lymph nodes in the neck may also be removed (lymph node dissection) if they contain cancer cells.
  • **Radioactive iodine (RAI) therapy:** After a total or near-total thyroidectomy for papillary and follicular thyroid cancers, RAI therapy may be used to destroy any remaining thyroid tissue and cancer cells. You swallow a capsule or liquid containing radioactive iodine, which is absorbed by thyroid cells.
  • **Thyroid hormone therapy:** After thyroid removal, you will need to take synthetic thyroid hormone (levothyroxine) for life to replace the hormones your thyroid used to produce. The dosage is carefully monitored.
  • **External beam radiation therapy:** Uses high-energy beams directed at the cancer from outside the body. It may be used for thyroid cancers that can't be completely removed by surgery, for cancer that has spread, or for types of thyroid cancer that don't respond well to RAI (like medullary and anaplastic).
  • **Targeted therapy:** Drugs that target specific abnormalities in cancer cells. These may be used for advanced thyroid cancers that haven't responded to other treatments.
  • **Chemotherapy:** Less commonly used for most types of thyroid cancer but may be an option for aggressive cancers like anaplastic thyroid cancer or for advanced cancers that haven't responded to other treatments.
  • **Active surveillance:** For very small, low-risk papillary thyroid cancers, doctors may recommend active surveillance, which involves regular monitoring with ultrasound and blood tests instead of immediate treatment. Treatment is started if the cancer shows signs of growth or change.

Treatment plans are individualized and depend on the specific type and stage of thyroid cancer, as well as the patient's age and overall health.

Important Considerations

  • Most types of thyroid cancer have a high cure rate, especially when diagnosed and treated early.
  • Long-term follow-up is important after treatment to monitor for any signs of recurrence.
  • Living without a thyroid gland requires lifelong thyroid hormone replacement therapy.
  • Support groups and resources are available for individuals with thyroid cancer and their families.

Important Information

If you notice a lump or swelling in your neck, or experience voice changes or difficulty swallowing, it's important to see a doctor for evaluation. While most thyroid nodules are benign, prompt diagnosis and treatment are crucial for thyroid cancer.
