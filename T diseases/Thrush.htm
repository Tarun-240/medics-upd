Thrush (Candidiasis)

Understanding Thrush

Thrush, also known as candidiasis, is a fungal infection caused by an overgrowth of *Candida* yeast, a type of fungus that naturally lives in small amounts in the mouth, throat, gut, and on the skin. Usually, the immune system and other microbes keep *Candida* levels in check. However, when this balance is disrupted, *Candida* can multiply and cause an infection.

Thrush can occur in various parts of the body, but oral thrush (in the mouth and throat) and vaginal thrush (yeast infection) are the most common. This information focuses primarily on oral thrush.

Common Symptoms of Oral Thrush

Symptoms of oral thrush can vary depending on the severity of the infection. Common signs include:

  • **White or yellowish, slightly raised lesions:** These often occur on the tongue, inner cheeks, gums, tonsils, and throat. They may look like cottage cheese.
  • **Redness or soreness inside the mouth.**
  • **A burning sensation in the mouth.**
  • **Pain when eating or swallowing (odynophagia).**
  • **Cracking and redness at the corners of the mouth (angular cheilitis).**
  • **A loss of taste.**
  • **A cottony feeling in the mouth.**
  • **Slight bleeding if the lesions are rubbed or scraped.**

In some cases, especially in individuals with weakened immune systems, thrush can spread to the esophagus (esophageal candidiasis), causing pain when swallowing and a feeling that food is stuck in the throat.

Risk Factors for Oral Thrush

Several factors can increase the risk of developing oral thrush by disrupting the normal balance of microbes in the mouth or weakening the immune system:

  • **Weakened immune system:** Due to conditions like HIV/AIDS, cancer, organ transplant, or certain medications like corticosteroids or immunosuppressants.
  • **Antibiotic use:** Antibiotics can kill beneficial bacteria in the mouth, allowing *Candida* to overgrow.
  • **Poor oral hygiene:** Not brushing and flossing regularly can contribute to *Candida* overgrowth.
  • **Dentures, especially if they don't fit well or are not cleaned properly.**
  • **Dry mouth (xerostomia):** Saliva helps to control *Candida* growth.
  • **Diabetes (especially if blood sugar is poorly controlled):** High glucose levels in saliva can promote *Candida* growth.
  • **Infancy:** Newborns can develop thrush due to their developing immune systems.
  • **Pregnancy:** Hormonal changes during pregnancy can sometimes increase the risk.
  • **Smoking:** Can irritate the mouth and disrupt the oral microbiome.

Diagnosis

Oral thrush is usually diagnosed based on a physical examination of the mouth and throat. The characteristic white lesions are often a clear indicator. In some cases, a doctor may:

  • **Gently scrape a small lesion:** The sample can be examined under a microscope to identify *Candida* yeast.
  • **Order a culture:** A sample from the mouth or throat can be sent to a lab to grow and identify the specific type of *Candida*.
  • **Perform an endoscopy:** If esophageal thrush is suspected, an endoscope (a flexible tube with a camera) can be inserted into the esophagus to visualize the lining. A biopsy may be taken.

Treatment

Treatment for thrush aims to control the overgrowth of *Candida* and relieve symptoms. The specific treatment depends on the severity and location of the infection, as well as the individual's overall health:

Oral Thrush Treatment

  • **Antifungal medications:** These are usually prescribed as liquids, tablets, or lozenges that are dissolved slowly in the mouth or swallowed. Common medications include nystatin, clotrimazole, fluconazole, and miconazole. The duration of treatment typically lasts for 7 to 14 days.
  • **Good oral hygiene:** Continue to brush your teeth gently (use a soft toothbrush if your mouth is sore) and floss regularly.
  • **Rinse your mouth:** Warm salt water rinses can help soothe the mouth.
  • **For denture wearers:** Clean dentures thoroughly and soak them in an antifungal solution if recommended by your doctor. Ensure dentures fit properly.

Esophageal Thrush Treatment

  • **Oral or intravenous antifungal medications:** Stronger antifungal medications, such as fluconazole or other azole antifungals, are usually required and may be given orally or intravenously. Treatment may last longer.

Infant Thrush Treatment

  • **Antifungal liquid:** A doctor may prescribe a liquid antifungal medication (like nystatin) to be applied to the infant's mouth several times a day.
  • **For breastfeeding mothers:** If the mother's nipples are also infected, an antifungal cream may be prescribed. Maintaining good hygiene of the nipples is important.

Prevention

While thrush can sometimes be unavoidable, especially in individuals with weakened immune systems, some measures can help reduce the risk:

  • **Maintain good oral hygiene:** Brush your teeth at least twice a day and floss daily.
  • **Rinse your mouth regularly:** Especially after using corticosteroid inhalers.
  • **Clean dentures properly:** Remove and clean dentures daily.
  • **Manage underlying conditions:** Keep diabetes under control.
  • **Use antibiotics judiciously:** Only take antibiotics when prescribed and complete the full course.
  • **If you use corticosteroid inhalers:** Rinse your mouth with water after each use to remove any residual medication.
  • **Consider probiotics:** Some studies suggest that probiotics may help maintain a healthy balance of microbes in the body.

Important Information

If you suspect you have thrush, it's important to see a doctor for diagnosis and treatment, especially if you have a weakened immune system or if the infection spreads. Follow your doctor's instructions carefully and complete the full course of any prescribed medication to ensure the infection is fully treated and to prevent recurrence.
