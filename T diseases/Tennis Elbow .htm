Tennis Elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis)

Understanding Tennis Elbow

Tennis elbow, also known as lateral epicondylitis, is a common condition that causes pain on the outside of the elbow. Despite its name, it can affect anyone, not just tennis players. It occurs when the tendons that connect the forearm muscles to the bony bump on the outside of the elbow (lateral epicondyle) become irritated and inflamed due to overuse and repetitive motions of the wrist and arm.

These tendons are involved in extending the wrist and fingers. When these muscles are repeatedly strained, tiny tears can develop in the tendons, leading to pain and weakness.

Common Symptoms

The pain associated with tennis elbow typically starts gradually and worsens over weeks or months. Common symptoms include:

  • Pain and tenderness on the outer side of the elbow.
  • Pain that may radiate down the forearm towards the wrist.
  • Weak grip strength.
  • Pain when twisting the forearm (e.g., turning a doorknob).
  • Pain when lifting or bending the arm.
  • Difficulty fully straightening the arm.
  • Stiffness in the elbow.

The pain may be more noticeable when:

  • Shaking hands.
  • Gripping or lifting objects.
  • Opening jars.
  • Using tools like screwdrivers.
  • Playing racket sports.
  • Typing or using a computer mouse.

Risk Factors

While repetitive activities are the primary cause, several factors can increase your risk of developing tennis elbow:

  • **Age:** It's most common in adults between 30 and 50 years old.
  • **Occupation:** Jobs involving repetitive wrist and arm movements, such as plumbers, painters, carpenters, butchers, and cooks.
  • **Sports:** Racket sports (especially with poor technique), and other activities involving repetitive arm motions.
  • **Poor technique:** Incorrect form during sports or work activities can strain the tendons.
  • **Overuse:** Engaging in activities for too long or too intensely without adequate rest.
  • **Weak forearm muscles:** Insufficient strength can make the tendons more susceptible to injury.

Diagnosis

A healthcare professional can usually diagnose tennis elbow based on a physical examination and your medical history. Specific tests they might perform include:

  • **Physical Examination:** The doctor will likely press on the outside of your elbow to check for tenderness and ask you to perform certain movements with your wrist and fingers to see if they provoke pain.
  • **Cozen's Test (Resisted Wrist Extension):** With your elbow straight and palm facing down, you'll be asked to extend your wrist against resistance from the examiner. Pain on the outside of the elbow suggests tennis elbow.
  • **Mill's Test:** The examiner will passively flex your wrist and pronate your forearm while extending your elbow. Pain indicates a positive test.
  • **Maudsley's Test (Middle Finger Extension):** You'll be asked to extend your middle finger against resistance while your elbow is straight and palm is down. Pain on the outer elbow is a positive sign.
  • **Imaging Tests:** X-rays are usually not helpful for diagnosing tennis elbow itself but may be ordered to rule out other conditions like arthritis or fractures. In some cases, MRI or ultrasound may be used to visualize the tendons, but this is not typically necessary for diagnosis.

Treatment

The goal of treatment is to relieve pain and restore function to your elbow. Most cases of tennis elbow improve with non-surgical treatments over several weeks or months:

  • **Rest:** Avoiding or reducing the activities that aggravate your pain is crucial.
  • **Ice:** Applying ice packs to the elbow for 15-20 minutes several times a day can help reduce pain and inflammation.
  • **Pain Relievers:** Over-the-counter pain medications like ibuprofen or naproxen can help manage pain and inflammation.
  • **Bracing:** Wearing a forearm brace or strap just below the elbow can help reduce stress on the injured tendons.
  • **Physical Therapy:** A physical therapist can teach you specific exercises to stretch and strengthen the forearm muscles, improve flexibility, and promote healing. This may include eccentric exercises (strengthening the muscle as it lengthens).
  • **Steroid Injections:** In some cases, your doctor may inject a corticosteroid into the affected area to reduce pain and inflammation. However, these injections may not provide long-term relief.
  • **Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) Injections:** This involves injecting a concentration of your own platelets into the injured tendon to promote healing. The evidence for its effectiveness is still evolving.
  • **Other Therapies:** Ultrasound therapy, massage, and dry needling may also be used.
  • **Surgery:** Surgery is rarely needed (only in cases where symptoms haven't improved after 6-12 months of non-surgical treatment). It typically involves removing the damaged part of the tendon.

Prevention

Several strategies can help prevent tennis elbow:

  • **Use proper technique:** Ensure correct form when playing sports or performing repetitive tasks. Consider lessons or guidance from a professional.
  • **Use appropriate equipment:** For racket sports, use a racket with the right grip size and string tension.
  • **Warm-up and stretch:** Before engaging in activities, warm up your arm muscles and perform stretches.
  • **Strengthen forearm muscles:** Regular exercises can help build strength and endurance in the forearm muscles.
  • **Take breaks:** During repetitive activities, take frequent short breaks to rest your arm.
  • **Modify activities:** If certain movements cause pain, try to modify how you perform them or avoid them altogether.
  • **Maintain good posture:** Proper posture can reduce strain on the arm and elbow.
  • **Avoid heavy lifting with an outstretched arm.**
  • **Use two hands for heavy tasks whenever possible.**

Helpful Tips for Recovery

Be patient with the healing process. It can take time to recover from tennis elbow. Follow your healthcare provider's recommendations and be consistent with your exercises.

  • Rest when you feel pain.
  • Apply ice regularly to reduce inflammation.
  • Perform prescribed exercises consistently.
  • Use a forearm brace as recommended.
  • Avoid activities that aggravate your symptoms.
